The distance between reasoning about a system and being responsible for one is where engineering education tends to go quiet. Graduate school is very good at preparing students to think through complex problems. It is imperfect at preparing them for the 2am moment when the system is failing, the documentation is wrong, the team in the UK won’t be online for three hours, and the thing that exists in the world is the thing you built. Not a simulation. Not a deliverable. A system that people are depending on right now.

I watched a student reach that moment this past year. His name is Aashish Chaple, and he reached it at Motorola Solutions, leading development of an AI-powered video search system for public safety agencies — emergency responders in airports, schools, hospitals, transit hubs, searching hundreds of simultaneous camera feeds in near real-time during active threats. When I say “near real-time,” I mean the performance envelope is measured in seconds that can contain catastrophe. He was a graduate student. He treated it as responsibility.

That’s the argument of this essay. Not a profile of one student, but a claim about what engineering education is actually for — and what it looks like when it works.

The Questions Production Teaches

Aashish closed the distance between knowing and being responsible faster than any student I have supervised.

The engineering problems at Motorola Solutions were severe. Efficient indexing of continuous video streams. Query latency that accommodates operational urgency. Fault tolerance that holds when components fail unpredictably, because emergencies do not schedule themselves. Cross-functional coordination with teams in the United Kingdom — genuine technical collaboration on systems where the interface between components is exactly where failure hides.

He made these decisions. He did not make them perfectly, because no first production system is built perfectly.

The gap between perfection and good-enough is where engineering judgment actually lives.

He held that gap. He optimized within it. The system shipped. Amazon Web Services, watching from a distance, made him an offer. He is there now, building large-scale distributed infrastructure for global cloud systems. That trajectory — graduate student to production engineer at AWS — is not common. It is the result of preparation that allowed him to receive complex work, judgment that allowed him to execute it, and awareness that allowed him to understand what he was building for.

The awareness is the part that cannot be taught in a lecture. It has to be earned. Aashish earned it in six months of sustained, high-stakes work that asked him, daily, to make decisions whose consequences would outlast his enrollment.

When he came back to my research seminar, his questions about AI systems were not abstract. He had built one. He knew what it meant to deploy consequential automation under uncertainty, and he needed a framework to hold that weight. The research on Computation and Skepticism in AI he conducted in my supervision was not philosophy for its own sake. It was the intellectual processing of an engineer who had spent half a year building systems with consequences, asking: what should we trust AI systems to do?

The Obligation of Specificity

There is a version of engineering ethics that remains perpetually general — that speaks of impact and responsibility and stakeholder harm without ever naming a specific person, a specific choice, a specific human being absorbing the consequence. This version is useless. It produces engineers who know technology has social effects but have never thought carefully about which technology, which effects, which person.

Aashish’s work refuses that generality. Everywhere you look in his record, the same question underneath: where is the system failing the people it claims to serve?

At a hackathon, he reimagined the psychiatric intake form — the thing patients fill out when they are most frightened — as a game. Standard clinical intake is alienating and bureaucratic at exactly the moment a person needs to feel safe enough to be honest. He reduced that friction without reducing diagnostic value. Second place at Northeastern.

At Harvard’s EasyA hackathon, he built a decentralized lottery on blockchain infrastructure, eliminating the attack vectors that enable fraud in centralized systems. Second place again. Two different domains. Two wins. One consistent question underneath both.

But the most revealing project is the one with no technical component at all.

Aashish noticed that cricket — the second most popular sport in the world — had no institutional presence at Northeastern, despite the fact that it is central to the lives of a significant portion of the international student population. He did not suggest someone else build a tournament. He designed the format, presented the proposal to Office of Global Services program managers, secured collaboration with University Recreation, managed logistics from marketing to prizes, and executed an event that drew over one hundred participants across graduate and undergraduate programs.

For many of those students, it was the first university-sponsored event that reflected their backgrounds.

That is what the obligation of specificity looks like when it operates without a technical platform: you see the gap, you identify what would close it, and you build the exact thing. Not a general solution. The specific one.

What the First Paper Contains

The research Aashish conducted in my supervision deserves its own account, because it reveals something about his formation that his professional record cannot fully capture.

Research on Computation and Skepticism in AI requires engaging with questions that have no clean empirical answer — questions about the nature of machine knowledge, the limits of computational inference, the gap between what an AI system can learn and what it should be trusted to decide. These are not questions with textbooks. They are questions with traditions: philosophy of mind, epistemology, reliability theory, ethics of automation.

He synthesized this material into course modules I will use in future courses. He recorded video components. He structured pedagogical sequences that move students from confusion to informed disagreement — which is the correct outcome for questions that resist resolution.

The students who encounter these questions will move through a pedagogical structure he designed. They will have no idea. The knowledge will reach them anyway.

This is what distinguishes a student who has been through production work from one who has not: the research questions are sharper, because they are no longer abstract. Aashish had spent six months deploying AI systems under real-world constraints before he sat down to write about the epistemic limits of AI systems. The philosophical inquiry completed something the engineering work had started.

That connection — between building and understanding what you are building — is what graduate education is supposed to produce. It is not common. When it happens, it looks like an engineer who can hold the complexity of a production system and the complexity of a philosophical question about that system, simultaneously, without collapsing either into the other.

The Infrastructure He Leaves Behind

There is a version of excellence that is purely personal — accumulated credentials, elevated metrics, individual distinction that moves with the person who earned it. And then there is excellence that creates infrastructure: systems and resources that function independently of the person who built them.

Aashish’s record is almost entirely the second kind.

The mentorship model he refined at the Office of Global Services — forty-plus students from ten-plus countries, sustained guidance across a full year — did not disappear when he graduated. The proof of concept remains: that graduate students who have navigated the international student experience can provide sustained peer support that no institutional office can replicate. The course modules he built are in my curriculum now. The students who encounter questions about AI reliability and epistemic limitation will do so through a pedagogical structure he designed.

The cricket tournament demonstrated that international student programming at institutional scale is achievable through student initiative. That demonstration is itself a form of infrastructure. It changes what future students understand to be possible.

The AI-powered search system he helped build at Motorola Solutions will support frontline public safety workers in situations that have not yet occurred, responding to threats that have not yet materialized, protecting people who do not know the system exists.

This is what it looks like when a graduate student builds beyond themselves. Not every excellent student does this. Most accumulate their excellence and carry it into their careers. Aashish built things that stay.

The Weight

I want to return to the image I opened with: the operations center, the analyst, the active threat, the system that must work.

That system contains Aashish’s architectural decisions. His performance optimizations. His fault tolerance mechanisms. These will either hold or they will not, in conditions he cannot control, for users he will never meet. He knew this when he was building. He built accordingly.

The awareness that the weight of what you build is real and belongs to you — permanently, because your systems will operate in situations you cannot specify — is the most important thing graduate education can produce in an engineer. It is not a skill. It is an orientation. It is the difference between a technologist who optimizes for metrics and one who builds for the person on the other end of the system.

What the operations center image finally asks is not about Aashish. It is about every engineer who builds consequential systems for people they will never meet. It asks: when your system is in that room, what will it carry from the decisions you made?

That question belongs to all of us.
