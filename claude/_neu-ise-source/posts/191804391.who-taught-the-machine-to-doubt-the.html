The most consequential arguments about power are being made in notation. The paper before us—”The Competing Reality: An Ontological and Operational Analysis of the Digital Twin as an Assertive Model”—makes one of them. It argues, in the careful language of engineering science, that the machines we’re building to model reality have started to define it. That when the digital twin disagrees with the sensor, the twin sometimes wins.

It does not ask, even once, who decided that should happen.

There is a moment in every technological revolution when the metaphor stops being a metaphor. We called the internet an information highway, and then we built our commerce, our friendships, our political lives on it, and one morning we woke to find that the road had become the destination. Something similar is happening now with the digital twin. We are building machines that model factories, cities, aircraft, human bodies—and we are telling ourselves, with the confidence of engineers who have solved the simpler problems, that the simulation serves the real. That the mirror is just a mirror.

This paper tells us otherwise.

The question it asks, carefully, through engineering language, is: who should win when the model and the physical world disagree? The question it does not ask—but which the evidence demands—is: who decided the model got to compete in the first place, and what did they believe they were responsible for when things went wrong?

When the Model Decides

A model does not simply process data. It encodes what counts as normal, which signals are trustworthy, which edge cases matter, which can be ignored.

The paper traces a three-stage evolution that shows how this became consequential. First there was the digital shadow: data flowing one direction, from physical asset to digital record, a camera that takes pictures. Then came the conventional digital twin: bidirectional, capable of simulation, useful for prediction, but still fundamentally subservient to its physical referent. The model served the machine.

Now there is the active digital twin—an entity that has internalized a generative model of the world, that generates predictions about what sensors should be reading, and that, when the sensors contradict those predictions, must make a choice. Update the model, or dismiss the sensor.

This is not a marginal design decision. It is a philosophical position encoded in mathematics.

The active digital twin minimizes what the paper calls variational free energy—a measure of the discrepancy between the model’s internal beliefs and incoming observations. Before the system updates what it believes, it weighs the sensor’s report against what it already expected to find. A large enough gap, and the model doesn’t simply learn. It decides the sensor is wrong.

But here is the critical detail: the system is not evaluating data in isolation. It is evaluating data against prior beliefs—assumptions about how the world works, how reliable sensors are, what counts as plausible.

The model does not begin neutral. It begins with commitments.

Not all disagreement between model and sensor is pathological. In aerospace, vibration and temperature cycles introduce systematic error that cannot be recalibrated mid-flight. In such cases, models compensate for known failure modes using historical data as reference. Distrusting the sensor is not a failure here. It is the correct behavior.

The distinction matters. There is a difference between compensating for a sensor the system has reason to believe is degraded, and overriding a healthy sensor because the model is more confident in itself. The danger begins when systems cannot tell the difference.

There are environments where no physical sensor can survive. In a glass furnace at 1600°C, silicon melts before it can measure. The digital model becomes the only available truth. Virtual sensors—mathematical constructs that infer what a physical sensor would read if it could survive—are not a workaround. They are the system. This is necessity.

The problem is not necessity. The problem is when the logic of necessity migrates into contexts where the sensor is not molten, but merely inconvenient.

What the 737 MAX Was Telling Us

Let me be precise about Boeing, because precision is the only honest response to disaster.

The Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System—MCAS—was designed to prevent aerodynamic stall. It relied on input from a single angle-of-attack sensor. When that sensor malfunctioned—on Lion Air Flight 610 in October 2018, on Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 in March 2019—it reported a stall condition that did not exist.

The pilots knew the aircraft was not stalling. They followed procedure. They resisted the system.

The system persisted.

The model disagreed. The model won.

But the failure was not simply one of authority. It was one of assumption. MCAS did not meaningfully represent the possibility that its sensor could fail. It operated with a prior belief that sensor reliability was absolute. A faulty reading was not treated as a hypothesis to be tested. It was treated as reality.

The system could not ask: what if the sensor is wrong?

Because that possibility was never encoded.

This is what it means to embed belief into a system. This is the difference between a tool that processes data and a system that has already decided what data means.

The paper calls this a “static priority” and recommends dynamic arbitration—a framework in which sensor data, model predictions, and human input are weighed against each other. This is a technical improvement. It is also a philosophical shift: from systems that assume correctness to systems that must reason about uncertainty.

What the 737 MAX demonstrated—at the cost of 346 lives—is that when you encode authority without encoding the conditions under which that authority should yield, you have not built a safety system. You have built a hierarchy.

Hierarchies, especially the invisible ones embedded in software, tend to protect themselves.

The Model Is Not Neutral. It Never Was.

A model does not simply process data. It encodes what counts as normal, which signals are trustworthy, which edge cases matter, which can be ignored. These decisions are made before the system ever encounters the world. Every model is built on data collected at a particular time, from particular populations—which is to say, from particular decisions about whose experience was worth measuring. Those assumptions do not disappear into the math.

They become the math.

The paper documents five specific ways that digital twins distort human behavior when applied to people: stereotyping, insufficient individuation, representation bias, ideological bias, hyper-rationality. Each of these has a technical name. Each of them is also a way of saying: the model was built by someone, on data that came from somewhere, encoding judgments invisible to the builders because judgments always are. And then the model was given authority over the people it was supposed to represent.

The paper extends this logic into healthcare through the “simulacra twin”—a comprehensive digital model of a human being, built from genomics, behavioral data, real-time biometrics, medical history, designed to predict disease trajectories and optimize treatment pathways. These systems today are largely assistive. They do not yet operate independently of clinical judgment or physical examination.

The concern is not present harm. The concern is that a system optimized to predict will eventually be trusted to prescribe.

If predictive models become sufficiently authoritative—if clinical systems begin organizing around projections rather than presentations—a shift becomes possible. From treating the patient as primary, to treating the model’s prediction as primary. And when that happens, disagreement is no longer between doctor and patient. It is between patient and model.

The question becomes: which one does the system believe?

The paper is careful here. It notes the possibility of patients being “reduced to their data.” It gestures toward the cultural context that models cannot capture. This is responsible scholarship.

It is also not quite enough. Because naming a risk and governing against it are different acts. And the gap between them is where harm lives.

Governed Autonomy and Its Silences

Above-the-loop leadership governs outcomes. It does not audit the beliefs that generate them.

The paper concludes with “governed autonomy”—a framework in which humans move “above the loop,” designing the policies, thresholds, and escalation logic that autonomous systems execute. Leadership no longer approves individual outputs. It approves the systems that generate outputs. The framework is serious, and the proposed mechanisms—Reality Gap Analysis modules, dynamic arbitration logic, federated unlearning architectures—represent genuine engineering progress.

But it is not sufficient.

Because what governed autonomy does not govern is the most important layer: the assumptions embedded in the model itself. What counts as an error? How reliable are sensors assumed to be? How strongly should prior beliefs resist new observations? These are not runtime decisions. They are design-time commitments.

To close this gap requires something the field does not yet have: a formal account of model humility. Not a system that is less capable. A system that knows the limits of its own claims—that can represent when its priors are weak, when its assumptions are untested, when it should defer.

Not every system that prioritizes models over sensors is wrong. But without visibility into the assumptions that govern that choice, we have no reliable way of knowing when it is.

The body, in the physical world, is still the final authority. It resists abstraction. It asserts itself through consequence.

The question the digital twin raises—every time a model overrides a sensor, every time a virtual sensor substitutes for a physical one, every time a prediction outweighs an observation—is whether we are prepared to grant our systems the same authority we grant reality.

That question is not technical.

It is the oldest question there is.
