When my professor assigned a project involving a question-answering system over a custom document set, I thought: how hard can this be? I’d heard of Retrieval-Augmented Generation. I’d seen the diagrams. Arrows going from a document, into a vector store, into an LLM, out comes a perfect answer. Clean. Simple.

Reader, it was not clean. It was not simple.

Here’s the honest version of what building a RAG pipeline actually looks like, using LangChain and Pinecone, including the parts that took me three frustrated evenings to figure out.

What RAG Actually Is (In One Paragraph)

The core idea behind RAG is elegantly practical. Instead of fine-tuning a language model on your own data (which is expensive, slow, and requires retraining every time your data changes), you keep a pre-trained LLM exactly as it is and teach it to look things up at the moment someone asks a question. You pre-process your documents by converting them into numerical representations called embeddings and storing them in a vector database. When a query comes in, you embed that too, find the document chunks that are mathematically closest to it in meaning, and inject them directly into the prompt. The LLM then answers using that retrieved context rather than relying purely on what it learned during training. In theory, this prevents it from hallucinating facts about your specific data. In practice... we’ll get to that.

Step 1: Ingesting Documents: The Part Everyone Skips Over

LangChain provides document loaders for almost every file type imaginable: PDFs, Word docs, web pages, Notion exports. On the surface, loading a PDF is just two lines of code using PyPDFLoader. It feels like magic. The problem is what comes out the other side.

PDFs are notoriously bad source material. A document with a two-column academic layout will have its text extracted in the wrong order. The loader reads left-to-right across the full page width, so you end up with sentences from column one and column two interleaved into gibberish. Scanned PDFs return almost nothing unless you’ve set up an OCR layer first. Tables get flattened into word soup with no structure preserved.

The lesson I kept having to relearn: always inspect your raw loaded text before doing anything else. Print the first few hundred characters of a few documents and actually read them. If the source material is broken, every downstream step (chunking, embedding, retrieval) will be quietly broken too, and you won’t realize it until your answers are inexplicably bad.

Step 2: Chunking: Where Most Tutorials Lie to You

This is the step that almost every tutorial glosses over, and it’s the one that will make or break your pipeline. Chunking is the process of splitting your loaded documents into smaller pieces before embedding them. LangChain’s RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter is the standard tool for this, and it takes two key parameters: chunk_size (how long each piece is) and chunk_overlap (how many characters are shared between consecutive chunks).

Most tutorials set these to some arbitrary default and move on, as though the numbers are irrelevant. They are not. Getting them wrong is one of the most common reasons a RAG system gives poor answers even when the right information is clearly in your documents.

If your chunks are too small (say, 100 characters), each retrieved piece will likely be a sentence fragment or a partial idea that has no meaning without the surrounding context. The LLM gets handed a puzzle piece with no picture on it. On the other hand, if chunks are too large (2,000 characters or more), you start cramming irrelevant content into your prompt. The model has to wade through noise to find the answer, which hurts both accuracy and cost.

The overlap parameter is subtler but equally important. It ensures that a key sentence sitting right at the boundary between two chunks doesn’t get split in half and lost entirely. But set it too high and you’re storing near-duplicate content across your vector database, which wastes space and can skew retrieval results.

After a lot of experimentation, I landed on a chunk_size of 600 and a chunk_overlap of 80 for my academic text. That’s not a universal answer; it’s a starting point. The right values depend entirely on the structure and density of your specific documents.

Step 3: Embeddings: Picking the Right Model

Once your documents are chunked, each chunk needs to be converted into an embedding, a vector of numbers that captures its semantic meaning in a way that allows mathematical comparison. LangChain makes it easy to utilize OpenAI’s text-embedding-ada-002 model for this purpose, which produces high-quality embeddings and integrates seamlessly with Pinecone.

There’s one rule here that sounds obvious until you violate it: the embedding model you use when processing your documents must be the exact same model you use when embedding a user’s query at retrieval time. The reason is that different models map language into different vector spaces. If you embed your documents with one model and your queries with another, the similarity scores between them become meaningless. You’re essentially comparing coordinates from two different maps. I ran into this after swapping embedding models mid-experiment and spending a baffling hour trying to understand why my retrieval had suddenly gotten terrible. Once you pick a model, commit to it for the entire pipeline.

Step 4: Pinecone: Getting the Vector Store Running

Pinecone is a managed vector database, meaning you don’t have to set up or maintain any infrastructure yourself. You initialize a connection with your API key, create an index, and upload your embedded chunks. LangChain’s Pinecone integration wraps most of this into a single method call, which makes it genuinely the smoothest part of the whole setup process.

A few practical things worth knowing before you start: Pinecone’s free tier only allows one active index, so if you’re running multiple experiments you’ll need to delete and recreate it each time. More importantly, when you create the index you need to specify the dimensionality (the length of the embedding vectors), and this must match exactly what your chosen model produces. For ada-002, that’s 1,536 dimensions. Finally, uploading (or “upserting”) large numbers of vectors takes time. Don’t close your notebook or assume it’s done until you see a confirmation, or you’ll be puzzled later when your vector store appears empty.

Step 5: Retrieval + Generation: Where Hallucinations Still Happen

Here’s the thing nobody fully prepares you for: RAG reduces hallucinations. It does not eliminate them. This distinction matters, especially if you’re building something people will actually rely on.

LangChain’s RetrievalQA chain handles the end-to-end flow. It takes a user’s question, retrieves the most relevant chunks from Pinecone (typically the top four, controlled by the k parameter), and passes them to the LLM as context for generating an answer. Setting temperature=0 is important here: it makes the model deterministic and discourages creative improvisation, which is the last thing you want when accuracy matters.

The single most useful debugging tool in the entire pipeline is the return_source_documents=True flag. When your answer looks wrong, this lets you see exactly which chunks were retrieved. More often than I expected, the problem wasn’t the language model at all. It was that the retrieval step had surfaced the wrong chunks. The right information was in my documents; it just wasn’t being found. When you see a bad answer, always check what was retrieved before assuming the LLM is to blame.

A practical debugging checklist: first, confirm the relevant answer actually exists somewhere in your documents. Then check whether the correct chunks are being retrieved. If they’re not, the culprit is usually chunk size cutting off critical context, or a mismatch between how the query is phrased and how the information is written in the source material. If the right chunks are being retrieved but the answer is still wrong, that’s when you look at the model and the prompt.

What I’d Do Differently

Start with ten documents, not a hundred. Get every stage of the pipeline producing correct results at a small scale before you try to ingest everything. Bugs are much easier to find when the data is manageable.

Treat retrieval and generation as two separate problems. It’s tempting to just read the final output and judge whether it’s right, but that doesn’t tell you where things went wrong. Evaluate what’s being retrieved independently of what the LLM does with it.

Use Pinecone’s metadata filtering. Every chunk you upload can carry metadata (the source document name, the page number, a date, a category). You can then filter retrieval by these fields, so a question about a specific topic only searches relevant documents rather than everything. I skipped this feature to save time and ended up with noisier retrieval results throughout the project.

Log everything, obsessively. The questions asked, the chunks retrieved, the answers given. When something breaks at midnight before a deadline (and something will), those logs are the only thing standing between you and total confusion.

Final Thoughts

RAG is genuinely one of the most practical things you can build with LLMs right now, and LangChain with Pinecone is a solid, well-documented stack to learn on. But there’s a real gap between the clean architecture diagram and a system that actually gives reliable answers. That gap is filled with chunking decisions you have to make by feel, embedding consistency requirements that are easy to violate, and retrieval failures that look like model failures until you dig into them.

The good news is that once you’ve worked through these problems once, the mental model clicks and stays with you. And a working RAG pipeline, even one built for a class assignment, is a concrete, demonstrable thing you can walk someone through in an interview. That’s worth more than another bullet point on a resume.

If you’re about to build your first one, I hope this saves you at least one of those three frustrated evenings.
