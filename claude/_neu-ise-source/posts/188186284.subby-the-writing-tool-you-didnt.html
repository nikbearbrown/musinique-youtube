There’s a peculiar gap in the world of writing tools. On one side, you have the blank page—infinite possibility, paralyzing freedom. On the other, you have templates that promise structure but deliver only rigid boxes to fill. What’s missing is something in between: a system that understands what you’re trying to accomplish and helps you accomplish it without flattening your voice or forcing you into someone else’s logic.

This is what Subby does. Not by replacing your thinking, but by giving it shape.

What Subby Actually Is

Subby is a command-based writing assistant built on a framework of eleven specialized commands, each designed for a different writing task. You don’t describe what you want in vague terms and hope the AI interprets correctly. You invoke a specific command—ezsay for literary essays, rollirev for review-style criticism, bear_case for academic case studies—and the system applies the exact structure, tone, and constraints that format requires.

The commands aren’t shortcuts. They’re frameworks that preserve intellectual rigor while eliminating the friction of “How do I structure this?” or “What tone does this format expect?”

Consider the difference:

Without Subby: “Write me a book review that’s like The New Yorker but shorter.”
The AI guesses. You get something essay-adjacent, maybe too wandering, maybe too compressed, probably missing the rhythmic variation that makes a New Yorker piece readable.

With Subby: ezsay [book title and your notes]
You get 1,800-2,500 words structured exactly as the command specifies: an opening that establishes stakes, one substantial digression that reframes the subject, a return that synthesizes without repeating, and a closing that resonates. The rhythm varies deliberately. The intellectual depth is preserved. The format is consistent.

The command doesn’t do the thinking for you. It structures your thinking so you can focus on what you’re actually trying to say.

A Few Examples, To Make This Concrete

Example 1: The Literary Review Essay (ezsay)

You’re reviewing a book on causal machine learning. You have notes on the technical approach, some thoughts on how it compares to other texts in the field, and a nagging question about whether the book’s accessibility undermines its rigor.

You invoke: ezsay

What you get:

  • Opening (300-400 words): Begins with an intellectual question—not slow buildup, but immediate engagement. The book emerges naturally from that question.

  • Subject unfolds (400-600 words): The scope, methods, and key ideas, described with literary attention but forward momentum. Technical details become story elements, not spec sheets.

  • The digression (600-800 words): Here’s where you explore that nagging question about accessibility vs. rigor. One substantial tangent that reframes the entire discussion, then returns to the book with new understanding.

  • Synthesis (300-400 words): Concrete outcomes, actual results, honest complications. No evasion, no hand-waving.

  • Closing (200-300 words): Resonant without being precious. One question lingers, but you’ve made your case.

Total: 1,800-2,500 words. Economical but thoughtful. Essayistic but purposeful.

The alternative—telling an AI “write a literary review”—produces something shapeless, wandering, or overly compressed. The command gives you the middle path: room to think, but not room to meander.

Example 2: The Rolling Stone Literary Review (rollirev)

Same book, different format. Now you want something sharper, more opinionated, culturally grounded. You want readers to know where you stand by paragraph three.

You invoke: rollirev

What changes:

  • Opening (200-300 words): Your verdict, delivered with punch. “This book succeeds because...” or “This book fails when...” No slow wind-up. The reader knows your position immediately.

  • Subject unfolds (500-700 words): Still descriptive, but now evaluation colors everything. You’re not neutrally summarizing; you’re building your argument as you describe.

  • Cultural context (600-800 words): This is where you connect the book to broader trends in machine learning pedagogy, or the tension between academic rigor and industry practice, or the history of technical textbooks that tried to be accessible and what happened to them.

  • Return with sharper focus (300-400 words): What works, what doesn’t, backed by evidence. Complications acknowledged but synthesized.

  • The closer (200-300 words): Final verdict with energy. Not trailing off, but landing.

Same total length. Completely different experience. The command adapts structure to purpose.

Example 3: The Investigative Narrative (investnon)

Now you’re not reviewing the book. You’re writing about the project that produced it—the years of course development, the pedagogical experiments, the students who struggled and succeeded. This isn’t analysis; it’s storytelling grounded in verifiable facts.

You invoke: investnon

What you get:

  • Opening: A “you” scenario or stark data point that establishes stakes. “You open the textbook to Chapter 3. The math looks familiar, but the examples are robots learning to walk. Somewhere between the equations and the code, you realize you’re not just learning causality—you’re learning how to teach machines to reason about intervention.”

  • Project genesis: Brief, vivid origin story using only verifiable facts. No invented quotes, no composite characters.

  • The work itself: Methodology, scale, components described with strong verbs and concrete details. “He built the curriculum around counter-examples. Not ‘here’s the right answer,’ but ‘here’s where everyone gets it wrong.’”

  • Impact/stakes: Statistics and documented outcomes. “Seventy-three percent of students who completed the course went on to implement causal models in production. Not for assignments. For actual systems people use.”

  • Closing: Implications that resonate beyond the immediate project.

Length: 500-800 words per section. Investigative tone, literary pacing, strict adherence to verifiable facts.

The alternative—asking an AI to “write narratively about this project”—often produces invented dialogue, fabricated testimonials, or generic observations that sound plausible but aren’t actually true. The command enforces the constraint: no fabrication, only verifiable facts and clearly labeled hypotheticals.

What Makes This Different

Most writing tools offer one of two things: total freedom (the blank page) or rigid templates (fill in the boxes). Subby offers a third option: structured flexibility. The commands define the architecture—how sections flow, how rhythm varies, what tone fits the format—but the thinking is yours.

The system doesn’t invent content to fill gaps. If you haven’t provided enough information, it tells you what’s missing. If a section requires data you don’t have, it asks for that data. The constraint against fabrication isn’t a limitation; it’s what makes the output trustworthy.

Consider bear_case, the command for academic case studies. It doesn’t just format your proposal; it inserts critical inline commentary—the questions a professor would ask, the gaps that would get flagged in peer review, the optimistic timelines that need reality checks. The commentary is built into the command’s structure. You get not just a document, but a dialectic.

Or consider ogily, the command for direct-response YouTube scripts. It enforces Ogilvy’s principle: start with the big idea, talk to the viewer like a human being, focus on what the information does for them. But it also enforces constraints from literary writing: rhythm that varies deliberately, no fabrication, no empty adjectives. The result is persuasive without being manipulative, conversational without being sloppy.
