PART 1: Chapter-by-Chapter Logical Mapping

Introduction: Towards an Artificial Democracy? (Montigny & Biancalana)

Core Claim: Democracy is being transformed—conditioned, manipulated, and potentially optimized—by AI and big data across three dimensions: polity (regulatory frameworks), politics (electoral practices), and policy (government surveillance).

Evidence Presented:

  • 48 of 104 democracies in decline (IDEA 2022)

  • 52% prefer strong leader over parliamentary systems, up from 38% in 2009

  • Historical pattern: each information revolution reshapes party structures (Bimber 2003)

Logical Method: Establishes problem through quantitative deterioration metrics, then proposes tripartite analytical framework (polity/politics/policy) as organizing principle for examining AI/data impacts.

Gaps:

  • No mechanism established for how AI differs from previous information revolutions beyond “more complex ethical challenges”

  • Causal chain from data practices to democratic decline assumed rather than proven

  • “Artificial” defined multiply (human-made, simulated, false) but relationships between definitions not clarified

Assessment: Strong framing device. The three-part structure provides clear analytical boundaries. Weakness: treats “artificial intelligence” and “big data” somewhat interchangeably when they require separate analysis.

Chapter 1: Big Data and Electoral Democracy: The Epistemic Risk (Blais)

Core Claim: Closed communications (microtargeting) represent epistemic threat to democracy by fragmenting deliberative space into echo chambers, undermining rationality and impartiality requirements for “right choices.”

Evidence Presented:

  • Goodman et al. (2017): candidates more divisive in targeted ads than open ads

  • Gorton (2016): restricted audiences facilitate exaggeration, ego-flattering

  • Fragmented audiences enable contradictory messaging

Logical Method: Deductive from normative theory. Establishes epistemic democracy requires (1) rational/informed arguments and (2) impartial arguments. Shows microtargeting violates both by design.

Gaps:

  • No empirical measurement of actual echo chamber formation from microtargeting

  • Assumes targeted messaging creates echo chambers; doesn’t prove it

  • “Right choices” defined normatively but measurement criteria absent

  • Alternative explanation unaddressed: voters already in echo chambers, microtargeting merely reflects this

Assessment: Philosophically rigorous but empirically speculative. The normative case is sound (publicity essential to public reason), but the causal mechanism from microtargeting to epistemic degradation is asserted more than proven.

Chapter 2: Big Data: A Collective Resource in a Connected World (Trudel)

Core Claim: Personal information protection laws fail because they treat data as individual property requiring individual consent, when aggregated data are collective resources requiring collective governance.

Evidence Presented:

  • Google/Waze use aggregated location data for traffic information

  • Consent mechanism trivialized by ubiquitous “I agree” buttons

  • Value extraction occurs without compensation despite consent

Logical Method: Ontological argument. Data changes nature when aggregated—ceases to be “personal” like air ceases to belong to individual when exhaled. Therefore legal framework must change to match ontological shift.

Gaps:

  • No specification of how “collective governance” would function institutionally

  • Analogy (data as air) illuminates but doesn’t establish governance mechanism

  • Sovereignty claims asserted but not reconciled with individual rights

  • Missing: who decides collective good? Through what process?

Assessment: Diagnostic strength, prescriptive weakness. Correctly identifies fundamental flaw in consent-based frameworks. The air analogy is elegant but elides the hard question: if data are collective resource, who governs collection/use and through what democratic mechanisms?

Chapter 3: The Democratic Specifications (Pellegrini)

Core Claim: Democratic regimes must adopt formal technical specifications preventing surveillance systems from “scaling up” to preserve resistance capacity against future tyranny—renouncing the “democratic postulate” that democracy is permanent.

Evidence Presented:

  • SAFARI project (1974) opposition led to Informatics and Liberties law

  • CJEU rulings: mass data retention only for national security, not crime fighting

  • French FNAEG genetic database: 5.2 million entries (7.5% of population), 76% never convicted

  • Netherlands Toeslagenaffaire: algorithmic fraud targeting caused systemic harm

Logical Method: Historical precedent + formal specification methodology. Examines failed protections (genetic databases, encryption backdoors) to derive rules preventing tyrannical misuse.

Gaps:

  • “Scalable” undefined operationally—what’s the threshold?

  • Assumes resistance requires technical inefficiency but offers no empirical support

  • Raymond Forni’s “room for fraud” quote powerful but not reconciled with rule of law

  • Backdoor prohibition assumes criminals can’t be caught other ways; EncroChat example contradicts this but isn’t fully developed

Assessment: Most rigorous on technical-legal analysis. Weakness: specifications are prohibitions without positive construction. What systems should democracies build? The “renounce democratic postulate” principle is philosophically sound but practically destabilizing if taken as license for permanent emergency posture.

Chapter 4: The Closing of Ranks (Bennett)

Core Claim: Canadian federal political parties exhibit cartel-like collusion to resist privacy regulation, collectively defending access to personal data as essential campaign resource.

Evidence Presented:

  • No privacy law coverage of federal parties despite coverage of businesses/government

  • Unified resistance to PIPEDA application across Liberal/Conservative/NDP

  • Joint legal briefs in BC litigation

  • Elections Canada survey: only 36% of candidates secured voter lists, only 24% destroyed them post-election

Logical Method: Applies Katz & Mair (1995) cartel party thesis through behavioral observation. Documents parallel arguments, coordinated litigation, consistent opposition across competitors as evidence of collusion.

Gaps:

  • Cartel thesis critiqued as unstable (MacIvor 1996); Bennett doesn’t address this

  • No smoking gun of explicit coordination—parallel behavior could be independent rational choice

  • Doesn’t establish that privacy regulation would significantly harm campaign effectiveness (the supposed motive for collusion)

  • Missing: comparative analysis with other jurisdictions where parties are regulated

Assessment: Strongest on documentation of resistance patterns. The behavioral evidence (unified legal arguments, NDP backtracking on 2018 commitment) supports collusion hypothesis. Weakness: motive unclear. If microtargeting effectiveness is disputed (as Blais notes), why would parties risk public backlash to protect unproven advantage?

Chapter 5: Digital Data as a Lens on Voters’ Lifestyle (Ouellet & Dufresne)

Core Claim: Lifestyle characteristics (music preferences, consumption choices) predict voting behavior as traditional sociodemographic variables lose predictive power—and these data are digital traces available for targeting.

Evidence Presented:

  • DellaPosta et al. (2015), Mutz & Rao (2018): coffee preferences predict partisanship

  • Hetherington & Weiler (2018): car type (Prius vs pickup) reveals political identity

  • Datagotchi collected 30,000+ responses (2021), 90,000+ (2022)

Logical Method: Substitution argument. As class/religion weaken as predictors, lifestyle fills explanatory gap because it reflects socialization patterns.

Gaps:

  • Correlation vs causation: does music preference cause political views or do both stem from third variable?

  • Datagotchi self-selection bias acknowledged but not resolved—”reweighting” mentioned but not demonstrated

  • No specification of actual predictive accuracy—how much variance explained?

  • Circular risk: if parties target based on lifestyle, does this create the correlation?

Assessment: Innovative data collection but theoretical foundation shaky. The claim that lifestyle “reflects socialization” is plausible but the mechanism is unspecified. Why would preferring metal music make you conservative? The chapter describes correlations without establishing causal pathways. The practical contribution (showing richness of available data) exceeds theoretical contribution.

Chapter 6: Party Members, Canvassing, and Microtargeting (Biancalana)

Core Claim: US-model data-driven campaigns fail to translate to European contexts due to cultural resistance, volunteer skepticism, and structural differences—actual practices diverge from rationalized strategies.

Evidence Presented:

  • Noi Siamo Torino (NST): only 27 active volunteers in city of 900,000

  • 7 of 10 interviewed volunteers were PD party members, contradicting “lay citizen” goal

  • Volunteers stopped collecting data: “I collected two emails and a phone number, then stopped asking”

  • Staff turned campaign bags inside-out when off-duty

Logical Method: Ethnographic observation reveals gap between campaign design (rationalized data collection) and implementation (skeptical volunteers, suspicious citizens).

Gaps:

  • Single case study—generalization limited

  • No comparison with successful Italian campaigns to isolate failure factors

  • Volunteer sample tiny (n=10)—selection bias likely

  • Missing: did data actually get used for microtargeting? What messages were sent?

Assessment: Valuable corrective to microtargeting hype. The ethnographic detail (bags turned inside-out, volunteers quitting data collection) provides evidence that sophisticated campaigns can fail at implementation. Limitation: we don’t know if failure was contextual (Italian skepticism) or fundamental (microtargeting doesn’t work).

Chapter 7: Surveillance Capitalism Meets the Pandemic (Lyon)

Core Claim: COVID-19 represents largest surveillance surge in history, enabled by government-platform partnerships that undermine “social contract” by reducing freedom/fairness without meaningful citizen consent or accountability.

Evidence Presented:

  • Google-Apple Exposure Notification (GAEN) API: platforms entered public health domain

  • Vaccine passports create excludable “risky other” category

  • Work-from-home monitoring (Prodoscore), exam proctoring (Examity), universal shopping (Amazon)

  • Indigenous Peoples suffer both insufficient surveillance (lack of data) and slanted surveillance (mistaken assumptions)

Logical Method: Accumulation of instances demonstrating surveillance expansion across multiple domains (health, work, education, commerce) without corresponding rights protections.

Gaps:

  • Social contract “reset” advocated but not specified—what would it look like?

  • No measurement of actual surveillance increase—”largest ever” asserted but not quantified

  • Alternative explanation unaddressed: surveillance accepted because pandemic fear overrode privacy concerns

  • Missing: exit strategy—how do temporary measures become permanent?

Assessment: Descriptively strong, normatively weak. The catalog of surveillance expansions is compelling. The “social contract” framing is evocative but operationally vague. What specific reforms would constitute a “reset”? The call for “data justice” is correct but the chapter doesn’t specify institutional mechanisms.

Chapter 8: The Use of COVID-19 Exposure Notification Apps in Canada (Déziel)

Core Claim: Provinces collected personal information to verify COVID Alert users’ identities despite federal government’s claim that app collected “no personal information”—disconnect reveals coordination failure and privacy framework gaps.

Evidence Presented:

  • Federal PIA explicitly excluded provincial operations from analysis

  • Memorandum of Understanding required provinces verify user identity and COVID-19 status

  • FOI requests: provinces provided almost no documentation of privacy protections

  • Health Canada evaluation: provinces not adequately consulted, “one-way exchange”

Logical Method: Deductive from legal requirements. MOU mandates identity verification → verification requires information collection → information collection is by definition “personal information” → federal claim of “no collection” is false.

Gaps:

  • Only 3 provinces responded meaningfully to FOI—small evidence base

  • No documentation of actual privacy harms from provincial collection

  • Assumes identity verification = significant privacy risk but doesn’t demonstrate harm

  • Missing: comparative analysis with other countries’ COVID apps

Assessment: Procedurally meticulous, substantively thin. The logical deduction (verification requires collection) is sound. But the privacy risk is minimal—basic identity confirmation for health service delivery is routine. The real finding is institutional: federal-provincial coordination failure meant nobody took responsibility for comprehensive privacy analysis. This is concerning for governance but the actual privacy harm is unclear.

Conclusion: Democracy as an Artifact (Rone & Biancalana)

Core Claim: Democracy’s future depends on collective agency—treating data as collective resource through bottom-up activism and democratized data sovereignty rather than state-centric or corporate control.

Evidence Presented:

  • Multiple forms of data activism: Make Amazon Pay, Collectivize Facebook campaigns

  • Reactive (countersurveillance) vs proactive (using data for advocacy) activism

  • EU digital sovereignty compatible with individualized data protection

Logical Method: Synthesis of preceding chapters with activist framework. Identifies cross-cutting themes (transparency, accountability, sovereignty) then proposes civil society empowerment as solution.

Gaps:

  • Data activism examples provided but effectiveness not assessed

  • “Collective ownership” proposed but institutional design absent

  • How to reconcile collective data governance with individual privacy rights? Not addressed

  • State digital sovereignty vs popular digital sovereignty tension identified but not resolved

Assessment: Aspirational rather than operational. The identification of data activism as counterpower is valuable. The call for “democratizing digital sovereignty” is correct in spirit but lacks specificity. What does collective ownership of campaign data actually mean? Who decides? Through what mechanisms? The conclusion raises the right questions but doesn’t answer them.

BRIDGE SECTION: Synthesizing the Logical Structure

This volume organizes around three analytical dimensions—polity, politics, policy—to examine how AI and big data affect democracy. The structure reveals both intellectual coherence and logical gaps.

Polity Section (Regulatory Frameworks): Establishes that current legal regimes fail because they treat data as individual rather than collective resource (Trudel), enable epistemic degradation through closed communications (Blais), and lack technical specifications to prevent authoritarian misuse (Pellegrini). The logical progression: individual consent frameworks → inadequate for aggregate data → require collective governance + technical safeguards.

Gap: No single author specifies what collective governance looks like institutionally. Trudel says data are collective resource. Pellegrini says systems must not scale. But who decides what gets collected? Through what democratic process?

Politics Section (Electoral Practices): Demonstrates political parties resist regulation (Bennett), use lifestyle data for targeting (Ouellet & Dufresne), but implementation often fails due to volunteer resistance and cultural context (Biancalana). The progression: parties want data → they collude to avoid regulation → but actual targeting less effective than rhetoric suggests.

Gap: If targeting is ineffective (Biancalana’s findings), why resist regulation so strenuously (Bennett’s findings)? Either (a) parties overestimate effectiveness, (b) resistance is ideological not strategic, or (c) Biancalana’s single case study is unrepresentative. Authors don’t reconcile this.

Policy Section (Pandemic Surveillance): Shows COVID-19 enabled massive surveillance expansion (Lyon), challenged social contract assumptions (Lyon), and revealed federal-provincial coordination failures (Déziel). The progression: emergency → surveillance surge → permanent infrastructure → need for “reset.”

Gap: What triggers the reset? Lyon calls for it, Déziel documents its absence, but neither specifies conditions under which democracies would implement stronger protections post-crisis.

Unifying Tension: All three sections identify similar problem—individual-rights frameworks inadequate for collective-level phenomena—but propose different solutions. Trudel: state data sovereignty. Pellegrini: technical restrictions. Bennett: regulatory enforcement. Lyon: social contract reset. Conclusion: civil society activism. These aren’t necessarily contradictory but they aren’t integrated.

PART 2: Comprehensive Literary Review Essay

Opening: The Provable Verdict

The central question is well-defined: Can democracies characterized by polyarchy—elected officials, universal suffrage, free expression—survive when the information environment is controlled by algorithmic systems designed to fragment publics, extract behavioral surplus, and optimize for engagement over truth?

The volume’s answer: Not without fundamental reconstruction of data governance frameworks. The editors prove this through systematic examination of failures across regulatory (polity), electoral (politics), and governmental (policy) domains. What emerges is not a single catastrophic failure but a pattern: individualized consent frameworks collide with collective-level data phenomena, producing a tragedy of the commons where nobody takes responsibility for democratic outcomes.

The evidence: 48 of 104 democracies declining while AI adoption accelerates. Canadian federal parties spending millions on digital advertising while refusing privacy oversight. Provinces implementing COVID apps without privacy documentation. Italian volunteers turning campaign bags inside-out because they’re embarrassed to be associated with data collection practices.

This is a book about institutional failure in the face of technological transformation. The failure is threefold: legal systems designed for individual privacy can’t regulate collective data resources; political parties prioritize competitive advantage over democratic deliberation; and emergency surveillance becomes permanent infrastructure.

Subject Emerges: Scope, Methodology, Scale

The volume analyzes democracy and big data across three dimensions:

Polity (Regulatory Architecture): Three chapters establish that current frameworks fail fundamentally. Blais shows closed communications undermine epistemic requirements of democracy—rationality and impartiality—by creating echo chambers where “the goal is not to inform but to convince.” Trudel demonstrates personal information laws based on individual consent cannot regulate aggregate data that function as collective resource. Pellegrini proposes “democratic specifications”—formal technical requirements to prevent authoritarian misuse of surveillance systems.

The regulatory section’s core insight: treating data as individual property requiring individual consent is ontologically wrong when data are aggregated. As Trudel argues, “Like the air we all breathe, data are personal when connected to an individual. When aggregated, they become collective resource.” The legal implication: states have sovereign right to regulate data generated within borders, regardless of individual consent.

Politics (Electoral Practices): Three chapters examine how parties use data for microtargeting. Bennett documents Canadian federal parties’ unified resistance to privacy regulation through litigation, parliamentary lobbying, and constitutional challenges—cartel-like behavior protecting access to voter data. Ouellet & Dufresne show lifestyle characteristics (music preferences, consumption choices) predict voting behavior as traditional demographics decline—and these characteristics manifest as digital traces. Biancalana’s ethnography of Italian campaign reveals gap between sophisticated data strategy and failed implementation—volunteers refused data collection, citizens mistrusted strangers.

The political section’s finding: microtargeting rhetoric exceeds reality. Parties invest heavily in data analytics (Bennett: millions in digital spending). But effectiveness disputed: Blais notes “experts disagree,” Biancalana shows volunteers sabotaging data collection. Yet resistance to regulation remains uniform across parties.

Policy (Pandemic Surveillance): Two chapters analyze COVID-19 as surveillance acceleration. Lyon catalogues expansion: contact tracing, work monitoring (Prodoscore), exam proctoring (Examity), universal tracking through platforms. Argues this undermines “social contract” through government-corporate partnerships that exclude citizens from negotiation. Déziel’s investigation of COVID Alert reveals federal-provincial coordination failure—provinces collected identity data despite federal claim of “no personal information collection.”

The policy section’s revelation: emergency surveillance proceeds without democratic deliberation. Lyon: “largest surveillance surge to date.” Déziel: provinces implemented without privacy documentation. Neither government nor platforms accountable to citizens.

The methodological range is notable: philosophical analysis (Blais), legal theory (Trudel, Pellegrini, Déziel), institutional analysis (Bennett), survey research (Ouellet & Dufresne), ethnography (Biancalana), sociological critique (Lyon). This interdisciplinary approach is the volume’s strength—each discipline illuminates different failure mode.

Scale: The book analyzes Canadian and Italian contexts deliberately, correcting US/UK bias in existing literature. The Canadian focus reveals federal-provincial tensions (Déziel) and cartel dynamics (Bennett) invisible in unitary systems. The Italian case (Biancalana) shows cultural resistance to imported American campaign techniques.

The Rigorous Digression: What Is Data, Actually?

The volume’s most important theoretical contribution, developed across Trudel, Lyon, and the Conclusion, is the ontological argument about data’s dual nature.

The Problem: Data about individuals can be understood two ways: (1) as attributes of individuals (personal information requiring consent), or (2) as collective resource generated by community activity. Current law treats data exclusively as (1). But when aggregated, data function as (2).

Trudel’s air analogy: “Like air we breathe, data are personal when connected to individual. When expelled, molecules become environment, resource of concern to community.” The legal error is applying individual-property frameworks to collective resources.

This matters because the ontological classification determines governance. If data are individual property, individuals consent to uses. If collective resource, states regulate in public interest. The shift from (1) to (2) occurs through aggregation—but at what threshold? One person’s location data is personal. Ten thousand people’s location data reveal traffic patterns (collective resource). But 100 people? 1,000?

The Mechanism: Lyon, drawing on Zubof, explains how surveillance capitalism exploits this ontological ambiguity. Platforms obtain “free and informed consent” for individual data collection, then aggregate data to create “behavioral surplus”—predictive models of collective behavior. Value is extracted at collective level while consent obtained at individual level. This is the expropriation Trudel identifies.

The Implication for Democracy: Three logically distinct claims emerge:

  1. Epistemic claim (Blais): Microtargeting fragments deliberative space. When parties can send contradictory messages to different groups, public reason is impossible. The requirement that arguments be “public” (Rawls) is violated by design.

  2. Sovereignty claim (Trudel): Aggregated data are collective resource over which states have legitimate regulatory authority. This provides foundation for requiring platforms/parties to serve public interest, not just obtain individual consent.

  3. Resistance claim (Pellegrini): Centralized data systems enable tyranny by allowing small number of people to surveil many. Democratic specifications must prevent “scaling up” to preserve distributed resistance capacity.

These three claims are logically independent but mutually reinforcing. Blais focuses on information quality, Trudel on resource governance, Pellegrini on power distribution. Together they construct an argument: democracies require public deliberation (Blais), collective governance of information resources (Trudel), and technically constrained surveillance to preserve resistance capacity (Pellegrini).

The Unresolved Tension: If data are collective resource requiring state governance (Trudel), and states may become tyrannical (Pellegrini), who protects collective resource from state misuse? Pellegrini’s answer: technical specifications preventing scale. But this assumes technical constraints can outlast political will to circumvent them. Historical examples (genetic databases expanding despite restrictions, encryption backdoors despite prohibitions) suggest otherwise.

The Conclusion proposes “data activism” and “popular digital sovereignty” as solution—collective ownership through civil society rather than state. But this creates new problem: how do decentralized collectives govern resource that requires coordination at scale? The book identifies the problem more clearly than the solution.

Return & Synthesis: The Pattern of Institutional Failure

Returning to specific evidence with refined analysis:

Bennett’s cartel thesis gains explanatory power when read alongside Ouellet & Dufresne. If lifestyle data predicts voting, and lifestyle data are digital traces easily collected, then privacy regulation directly threatens parties’ competitive advantage. The unified resistance Bennett documents becomes rational strategic behavior, not merely ideological opposition. Parties may not know if microtargeting works (Blais’s point about disputed effectiveness), but they know competitors are using it, creating prisoner’s dilemma: regulate together or defect individually.

Biancalana’s implementation failure illuminates limits of rational-actor models. NST designed sophisticated data operation but volunteers sabotaged it through passive resistance (stopping data collection, turning bags inside-out). This suggests cultural/contextual factors matter more than strategic design. The implication: microtargeting may self-limit through social resistance even without regulation.

Déziel’s coordination failure between federal and provincial governments parallels the coordination failure between normative arguments in the volume. Federal government: “no personal information.” Provinces: collect identity data. Nobody integrates the analyses. Similarly, Trudel argues collective governance, Pellegrini argues technical constraints, Lyon argues social contract reset, Conclusion argues civil society activism—but nobody shows how these fit together.

Lyon’s surveillance capitalism thesis provides explanatory framework but lacks prescriptive power. The diagnosis is clear: platforms extract value through “behavioral surplus,” governments enable this through partnerships, citizens disempowered. The prescription: “social contract reset.” But what does reset mean operationally? The chapter calls for transparency, accountability, trust—all correct—but doesn’t specify institutional mechanisms.

The pattern across all domains: analysis exceeds synthesis. Each chapter diagnoses failure mode in its domain. None construct institutional architecture for success.

Closing: What Is Proven, What Remains Open

What This Volume Proves:

  1. Legal-ontological mismatch: Personal information frameworks cannot regulate aggregate data. This is proven deductively (Trudel’s argument) and empirically (Déziel’s finding that nobody took responsibility for provincial COVID Alert privacy protections).

  2. Epistemic degradation risk: Microtargeting threatens public deliberation by enabling contradictory messaging, echo chambers, and personalized manipulation. Proven normatively (Blais’s argument from public reason) though not empirically (effects on actual deliberation unmeasured).

  3. Implementation gap: Sophisticated data strategies often fail in practice. Proven ethnographically (Biancalana) though generalization limited.

  4. Cartel behavior: Canadian federal parties collude to resist regulation. Proven through behavioral observation (Bennett) though motive unclear given disputed effectiveness.

  5. Surveillance expansion: COVID-19 enabled unprecedented surveillance growth. Proven by accumulation of instances (Lyon) though not quantified.

What Remains Unresolved:

  1. Collective governance mechanism: If data are collective resource, who decides uses? Through what process? At what scale (municipal, provincial, federal, international)? Not specified.

  2. Effectiveness question: Does microtargeting actually work? Blais says disputed. Ouellet & Dufresne show lifestyle predicts voting (suggesting potential effectiveness). Biancalana shows implementation failure (suggesting ineffectiveness). Bennett shows parties act as if effective (massive resistance to regulation). No reconciliation.

  3. Technical vs political constraints: Pellegrini proposes technical specifications preventing surveillance scale-up. But can technical constraints survive political pressure? Genetic database expansion (80,000 to 5.2 million entries) suggests no. Alternative approaches needed but not developed.

  4. Social contract reset: What would it look like institutionally? Lyon calls for it, others echo it, nobody designs it. Beyond “transparency, accountability, trust” (which everyone favors), what are the specific institutional reforms?

  5. Exit strategy: How do emergency measures (vaccine passports, contact tracing, work monitoring) end? Pellegrini warns against ratchet effects—liberticidal measures rarely repealed. True, but what political economy would enable repeal? Not addressed.

The volume’s contribution is diagnostic. It proves current frameworks fail across multiple dimensions. The prescriptive contribution is weaker. The editors acknowledge this: “It is up to our collective agency to make sure ‘artificial democracy’ ends up meaning a democracy we all make collectively... rather than a deep-fake.” True. But collective agency requires institutional channels. What are they?

The Question to Answer: The book establishes that individual-consent frameworks can’t govern collective-level data phenomena. It establishes that technical constraints alone can’t prevent authoritarian misuse. It establishes that parties resist regulation, implementations fail, and emergencies enable surveillance expansion. Given all this, what institutional architecture would enable democratic governance of data as collective resource while preserving individual rights and preventing authoritarian capture?

The book doesn’t answer this question. It proves the question must be answered.

ISE Assessment

Strengths:

  • Interdisciplinary rigor across philosophy, law, sociology, ethnography, political science

  • Canadian/Italian focus corrects Anglo-American bias

  • Evidence-based: legal analysis, ethnography, survey data, FOI requests

  • Honest about limits: implementation gaps, disputed effectiveness, small samples acknowledged

Weaknesses:

  • Prescriptive poverty: diagnoses failure, doesn’t design solutions

  • Internal contradictions unreconciled: state sovereignty (Trudel) vs anti-state specifications (Pellegrini) vs civil society activism (Conclusion)

  • Effectiveness question avoided: if microtargeting doesn’t work, why the alarm? If it does work, why did NST fail?

  • Measurement gaps: “largest surveillance surge” unmeasured, epistemic degradation unquantified, cartel collusion inferred not documented

The Central Unresolved Question:

If individual consent can’t regulate collective resources (proven), and state control risks authoritarianism (proven), and technical constraints fail under political pressure (suggested), and civil society activism lacks coordination mechanisms (acknowledged), then what institutional architecture enables democratic data governance?

The book earns the right to ask this question through rigorous documentation of current failures. It doesn’t earn the right to claim it’s answered it.

Verdict: Essential reading for understanding how data transforms democracy. Insufficient for understanding how democracy should govern data.
