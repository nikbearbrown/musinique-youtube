The arms dealer’s oldest dream was a weapon that could think. What nobody imagined or perhaps what everyone chose not to was the more prosaic nightmare: a weapon that learns to write a more convincing email.

This is where we have arrived. The World Economic Forum’s Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2026, drawn from surveys of 804 leaders across 92 countries, reports that 94% of executives now identify artificial intelligence as the primary force reshaping cybersecurity. But here is the detail that should give anyone pause: 87% of those same leaders say vulnerabilities in AI systems represent the fastest-growing threat to their organizations. They are afraid of what they are also deploying. The tool that defends and the tool that attacks are, increasingly, the same tool.

The WEF calls it the dual-use dilemma. Dilemma implies a choice. The reality is messier. Organizations didn’t choose this. They stepped into it the way you step into water that has quietly risen while you weren’t looking.

What the Numbers Say

On the defensive side, AI works. Organizations that deploy it extensively shorten breach response times by roughly 80 days and reduce breach-related costs by $1.9 million on average. Seventy-six percent of security professionals report chronic exhaustion, and AI tools that filter signal from noise are, for many teams, the difference between functioning and collapse.

On the offensive side, AI also works. Voice-based attacks surged 442% in the past year. Eight-point-six million unique malicious domain names were registered in 2024 an 81% annual increase. Global fraud-related losses exceeded $1 trillion. Generative models have compressed the threat lifecycle so completely that an attacker who once needed advanced coding expertise can now instruct a model to build polymorphic malware in an afternoon.

The same capability. Two directions. The distance between them is not technology. It is intent.

This is not a new problem, it is a very old problem wearing a new interface. Gunpowder carried miners through mountains and soldiers through bodies. Nuclear fission powers cities and ended two of them in four days. What changes with AI is accessibility. The Haber-Bosch process required industrial infrastructure. Generative AI requires a subscription. In 2025, 63% of organizations deploying AI had no formal security governance for it whatsoever. They were handing out weapons without asking who was in the room.

The Boardroom Gap

Chief Information Security Officers rank ransomware and supply chain resilience as their top concerns. They are watching the infrastructure. Chief Executive Officers rank cyber-enabled fraud and phishing as their number one threat. They are watching the ledger.

Neither group is wrong. Both groups are missing something.

CEOs rank AI vulnerabilities as their second-highest strategic risk. CISOs do not include AI vulnerabilities in their top three operational concerns at all. The people setting strategy believe AI risk is critical. The people building the defenses are focused elsewhere, not because they are incompetent, but because they are overwhelmed by the alert that is ringing right now.

This gap is not a personnel problem. It is an architecture problem. The slower, structural vulnerability of poorly governed AI doesn’t trigger an alert. It accumulates. Highly resilient organizations close this gap by design: 99% have board-level engagement in cybersecurity strategy versus 87% in under-resourced firms. The gap between resilient and insufficient is not technology. It is governance.

Who Bears the Cost

Seventy-three percent of global respondents say they or someone in their professional network has been personally affected by digital scams in the past year. In Sub-Saharan Africa, 82%. These are not abstract organizational incidents. They are people who lost money they could not afford to lose, workers whose identities were stolen, nonprofits that clicked a phishing link because they didn’t have a security team.

Twenty-three percent of public-sector organizations report insufficient cyber resilience, versus 11% of private-sector firms. Small organizations are two-and-a-half times more likely than large enterprises to fall into that category. The organizations serving the most vulnerable populations are themselves the most vulnerable.

AI did not create this inequality. But it deepens it, making the expensive defense better while leaving the inaccessible defense worse.

Structural Failure, Collective Intelligence

The WEF’s proposed remedy, organize responsibility upstream, to actors best positioned to act at scale, is correct in its logic. Domain registrars enable 87% of all phishing activity. A handful of registrars are the bottleneck through which millions of malicious domains flow. The Global Signal Exchange increased its shared abuse indicators from 40 million to 550 million in under twelve months. That is what collective defense looks like when it functions.

But the deeper problem is this: we are asking the same actors who profit from AI deployment to self-regulate AI risk. We are asking platforms that benefit from volume-driven engagement to police the fraud flowing through their infrastructure. We are asking nation-states simultaneously attacking other nations’ critical infrastructure to sign agreements protecting it. Governance frameworks are necessary. They are not sufficient.

What does this mean for the individual organization? It means waiting for international consensus is not a strategy. The organizations the WEF classifies as highly resilient are not waiting. They are building AI security governance now, integrating cybersecurity and anti-fraud operations now, demanding software bills of materials from vendors now.

The weapon has learned to write convincing emails. The question is whether we are willing to be equally precise about what we know, what we owe, and who we are actually protecting.
