On April 8, 2026, Arm Holdings did something it spent 35 years refusing to do: they announced a chip they will manufacture and sell themselves, placing them in direct competition with customers who license their designs.

To understand why this matters: CPUs (Central Processing Units) and GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) do different jobs. The GPU is like a factory floor with thousands of workers doing the same simple task in parallel perfect for AI training. The CPU is the project manager: handling complex decisions and coordinating everything.

Arm doesn’t make chips they design the architecture (the blueprint) and license it to Apple, NVIDIA, and Amazon who build the processors. Arm’s designs run 99% of smartphones because they’re incredibly power-efficient. Every iPhone, every Android phone runs on Arm architecture.

The AGI CPU (Arm General Infrastructure) breaks that model. Arm is manufacturing and selling the chip directly.

Why? Because a new kind of AI is breaking the old computing model. The AGI CPU is built for agentic AI systems that run 24/7, continuously reasoning and executing tasks autonomously. Traditional AI waits for prompts and stops. Agentic AI is like an autopilot that never lands: writing code, managing workflows, coordinating with other AI systems without human supervision.

Meta and OpenAI co-developed this chip. Meta optimized it for their AI recommendation systems. OpenAI needs it for orchestration routing millions of requests across data centers. These partnerships determine whether this becomes critical infrastructure or an expensive mistake.

The Crisis: Why x86 CPUs Can’t Handle This

Most data centers run on x86 CPUs Intel and AMD’s architecture from the 1980s. x86 is like a Swiss Army knife: it has tools for everything to stay backward-compatible with old software. This makes chips complex and power-hungry.

For traditional AI, that was fine. ChatGPT asks a question, the CPU does housekeeping, the GPU generates the answer, everything goes idle. Data centers used 1 CPU per 4-8 GPUs.

Agentic AI is different. These systems never stop constantly running decision trees, managing memory, coordinating thousands of AI agents. This is sequential work (step A before step B). GPUs can’t do sequential work well. CPUs handle it, but become the bottleneck.

Arm estimates agentic AI increases CPU workload 15x. Suddenly data centers need equal CPUs and GPUs. But x86 CPUs boost performance temporarily for benchmarks, then throttle when overheating. When you run all cores continuously, exactly what agentic AI requires, they slow down or consume enormous power.

This is the “Power Wall”: data centers hitting limits before electricity and cooling costs become impossible.

Arm’s answer: the AGI CPU uses RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computing), a specialized tool, not a Swiss Army knife. It has 136 real, dedicated cores at 300 watts. No tricks. No throttling. Tell it to run 136 tasks simultaneously, it runs 136 tasks all day without slowing down.

The efficiency comes from 35 years designing smartphone chips where battery life is everything. Now applied to data centers: 45,000 Arm cores per rack versus 20,000 x86 cores. Arm claims 2x performance per rack $10 billion in infrastructure savings at massive scale.

Why This Changes Everything: From Architect to Builder

Imagine you’re an architect who designs houses. For 35 years, you’ve sold blueprints to builders. You design amazing homes. Builders construct and sell them for $500,000, paying you $5,000 per house. You make steady money with 95% profit margins. Everyone loves you because you’re not competing just providing great designs.

That’s Arm. They designed the architecture for 99% of smartphones. Apple, Qualcomm, NVIDIA, Amazon license Arm’s designs and build their own chips. Arm collects a few dollars per chip. No factories, no supply chains needed.

But as AI exploded, Arm’s customers started building their own data center chips using Arm’s architecture. Amazon built Graviton. Google built Axion. Microsoft built Cobalt. They’re using Arm’s blueprints but capturing hundreds of dollars per chip. Arm gets the small royalty.

Arm’s bet: manufacture and sell our own chip.

The risk: competing with customers could lose licensing deals. Profit margins drop from 95% to maybe 40-50%. R&D jumped 46%, $512 million quarterly. Morgan Stanley downgraded the stock.

The buffer: Arm still collects royalties on every customer chip, with 70% locked through 2031.

What’s really happening: the rules for who builds AI infrastructure are being rewritten. Being just the architect, even the most successful one started looking like a losing position.

The Bigger Question: Who Controls the Robots?

Building infrastructure for autonomous AI raises a question: who’s responsible when you make the silicon that lets AI systems run 24/7 without human supervision?

Meta’s algorithms continuously optimize your feed, deciding what you see based on real-time tracking. OpenAI’s systems coordinate multiple AIs to solve complex tasks. These don’t wait for permission.

Think of the GPU as the orchestra playing music, the CPU as the conductor keeping everyone in sync. In systems with thousands of AI agents, the CPU prevents chaos.

But the conductor also decides what gets played, how loud, and for how long.

Efficiency isn’t neutral. For hospital logistics or crop predictions, efficiency saves lives. For real-time surveillance or manipulating information flows, efficiency is a weapon. The AGI CPU makes continuous, autonomous execution faster and cheaper at massive scale.

The parallel is electrification. Edison and Westinghouse built grids to deliver power reliably. The grid didn’t care if you lit your home or powered something harmful. Society decided what was acceptable to plug in.

We’re at the same moment. The AGI CPU is the power grid. What we allow to run autonomously, what requires human oversight, that’s not a question for engineers. That’s a question for all of us.

Can They Actually Do This?

The AGI CPU isn’t just a concept, you can order servers with it now. Meta helped design it; their engineers optimized it for their AI systems. When it launches, it’s already tuned for one of the world’s largest AI deployments.

Arm’s CEO targets $25 billion annual revenue by 2031, six times their 2025 revenue of $4 billion.

The optimistic case: AI infrastructure is the biggest tech spending boom in history. If Arm’s 2x efficiency is real, customers save money switching.

The pessimistic case: margins shrink, R&D costs climb, Intel and NVIDIA fight back with competing chips.

The real question: can Arm manufacture, sell, and keep improving these chips fast enough while competing with their own customers?

What This Means

The CPU is the conductor, the data center the orchestra. But there’s a version where the conductor doesn’t just coordinate, they decide what gets played, how loud, how long.

Agentic AI systems don’t ask permission. The AGI CPU makes them faster and cheaper at massive scale. That’s progress. It’s also concentrated power.

The infrastructure Arm is building will determine what AI systems are economically viable and who controls them. Those aren’t neutral technical decisions. They’re choices that will shape what AI can do in the world.

What the AGI CPU conducts, what we allow it to conduct, will determine whether this becomes an era where AI was accountable and worked for everyone, or when we optimized for efficiency without asking what we were making efficient.

Arm has taken the stage. The question is: who wrote the score, and who gets to change it?

If you’re studying engineering, these are questions your generation will answer. What constraints should be built into systems like this? What shouldn’t be automated, even if we can? The technology is being built now. The governance decisions are still up for grabs.
