We are living through one of the most hyped technological moments in history. AI tools are being built into everything: search engines, coding assistants, customer service bots, medical diagnostics, and even autonomous agents that browse the web on your behalf. The narrative is seductive. These systems seem smart, responsive, and almost human.

But there is a gap between how AI feels and how it actually works. And that gap matters a lot.

Here are five things about modern AI that most people don’t know, but probably should.

1. It Makes Things Up, and That’s Not a Bug

If you have used ChatGPT or any large language model (LLM), you have probably noticed it occasionally states something completely wrong with total confidence. It might cite a paper that doesn’t exist, or give you a historical fact that is slightly off. This is called hallucination.

Here is the uncomfortable truth: hallucination is not a flaw that engineers forgot to fix. It is a natural consequence of how these systems work. LLMs are trained to predict the most statistically likely next word given everything that came before it. They are pattern-matching machines operating at massive scale. They do not “look up” facts. They generate text that sounds right based on patterns in their training data.

So when an AI confidently tells you something false, it is not lying. It genuinely has no mechanism to know the difference between a fact and a plausible-sounding fabrication. That distinction requires something LLMs don’t have: grounding in reality.

2. Nobody Can Explain What It’s Thinking, Including the People Who Built It

When a doctor diagnoses a patient, they can walk you through their reasoning. When a judge makes a ruling, they write an opinion explaining it. When an AI system makes a decision, even its creators often cannot tell you exactly why.

This is the black box problem. Modern neural networks learn by adjusting billions of numerical parameters across millions of training examples. The result is a system that works remarkably well, but whose internal logic is opaque even to its designers. There is an entire field called interpretability research dedicated to cracking this open, and it remains one of the hardest unsolved problems in AI.

This matters beyond academic curiosity. When AI is used to decide who gets a loan, who gets flagged at a border, or which patients get prioritized for treatment, “the model said so” is not a good enough explanation. The black box problem is not just technical. It is a question of accountability.

3. Every Conversation Starts From Zero

Here is something that surprises most people. AI assistants do not actually remember you. At all.

When you open a new chat, the model has no memory of anything you said before unless it is explicitly included in the current conversation window. What feels like continuity is an illusion created by feeding the model a transcript of your recent exchanges each time you send a message. Once that window closes, it is gone.

This has real implications. It means the “relationship” you feel you are building with an AI assistant is one-sided. It means that if you are using an AI agent for a long-running task, it is working without any persistent understanding of your goals, preferences, or past decisions. Every session is, in a very real sense, meeting a stranger who has been handed your notes.

4. AI Agents Can Be Tricked by Things You Would Never Fall For

Imagine hiring an assistant and then discovering that a malicious note left on your desk could secretly reprogram their instructions without them telling you. That is essentially the vulnerability at the heart of something called prompt injection.

As AI systems become more agentic, meaning they can browse the web, read documents, send emails, and take actions on your behalf, they become exposed to untrusted content in the environment. A bad actor can embed hidden instructions in a webpage or a document that the AI reads, causing it to behave in ways its original operator never intended.

What makes this especially tricky is that the AI cannot easily tell the difference between instructions from its legitimate operator and instructions smuggled in through the environment. It processes language. All language looks roughly the same to it. This is an active and serious security concern as agentic AI systems become more widely deployed.

5. When Something Goes Wrong, Nobody Is Quite Sure Who Is Responsible

Pull all of this together and you arrive at perhaps the most unsettling question of all: who is actually in charge?

A typical AI deployment today involves a chain of actors. There is the company that trained the base model. There is the developer who built an application on top of it. There is the business that deployed that application. And there is the end user interacting with it. When an AI agent makes a harmful decision, caused by a hallucination, an opaque internal process, a manipulated input, or a misaligned goal, responsibility is distributed across all of these parties and clearly owned by none of them.

This is not just a legal question, though it is certainly that too. It is a question about what it means to deploy systems that act in the world without anyone fully understanding them, remembering what they did, or being able to explain why they did it.

So Where Does That Leave Us?

None of this means AI is useless or that we should stop building it. These tools are genuinely powerful and, used well, genuinely helpful. But the gap between the hype and the reality is wide, and closing that gap starts with honest understanding.

The systems we are building are not intelligent in the way humans are intelligent. They do not know what they know. They do not remember what they did. They can be fooled. They cannot explain themselves. And the question of who steers them remains, for now, uncomfortably open.

That is worth sitting with before we hand them the wheel.
