Introduction

Every Monday morning, over 500 million Spotify users wake up to a fresh playlist of 30 songs they’ve never heard before — and somehow, most of them hit. That’s Discover Weekly, and behind its effortless feel is one of the most sophisticated recommendation engines in all of tech.

As an Information Systems student at Northeastern, I spend a lot of time thinking about how data-driven systems shape user experiences — and the ethical trade-offs embedded in their design. When I started digging into how Spotify actually builds Discover Weekly, I realized the engineering behind it goes way deeper than most people think. What looks like a simple playlist is actually the output of matrix algebra, neural networks, natural language processing, and behavioral psychology — all working together in real time across half a billion users.

But as with any AI system operating at this scale, there are serious questions about bias, fairness, and whether the algorithm is truly serving users — or just optimizing for corporate retention metrics.

This article breaks down the technical architecture behind Spotify’s recommendation system, examines recent shifts in how it operates, and raises critical questions about algorithmic ethics that anyone in information systems should be thinking about.

A Brief History: From Radio to Algorithms

To appreciate how far music recommendation has come, it helps to look at where it started.

In the early 2000s, music discovery was driven by top charts, radio DJs, and early streaming platforms like Pandora and Last.fm. Pandora’s “Music Genome Project” was one of the first serious attempts at algorithmic recommendation — trained musicologists would manually tag songs with hundreds of attributes, and the system would match users to tracks based on those tags.

When Spotify launched in 2008, it didn’t invent music recommendations. But as researcher Thomas Hodgson explains in the CNBC documentary, it was the way Spotify “combined various computational techniques in order to make their recommendations feel more lifelike” that set it apart from the competition.

The real transformation came in 2014, when Spotify acquired a music analytics company called The Echo Nest for approximately $100 million. The Echo Nest had been using machine learning and natural language processing to build massive databases of songs and artists — analyzing not just audio, but the entire cultural conversation around music. That acquisition gave Spotify the technical backbone for the recommendation system we know today.

Since then, the system has evolved into a hybrid engine built on three core pillars. Let’s break each one down.

Pillar 1: Collaborative Filtering — “People Like You Also Liked This”

Collaborative filtering is the backbone of the entire recommendation system, and it’s built on an elegantly simple idea: if you and another user have similar listening histories, you’ll probably enjoy each other’s undiscovered tracks.

How the Matrix Works

Spotify maintains a gigantic matrix where every row represents a user and every column represents a song. With over 500 million users and tens of millions of tracks, this is an astronomical dataset. Every time you play, skip, save, or add a song to a playlist, you’re feeding data into this matrix.

Of course, you can’t do math efficiently on a matrix that massive — most of it is empty space (since no user has listened to every song). So Spotify uses a technique called matrix factorization to compress it. The algorithm breaks the giant matrix into two smaller sets of vectors:

• User vectors — representing each listener’s taste profile

• Song vectors — representing each track’s characteristics based on how users interact with it

These vectors are computed using a process called alternating least squares (ALS), which iteratively optimizes user vectors and song vectors by bouncing back and forth between them until the prediction error is minimized and the results converge.

The Music Map

The result is essentially a spatial “map” of all music on Spotify. Each song is a point on this map, and its position is determined by how users have playlisted and listened to it. Songs that frequently appear together in playlists will cluster near each other. Songs that never co-occur will be far apart.

As shown in the CNBC documentary, Spotify’s VP of Personalization describes this as “building a map of music and podcasts” where “tracks go together according to the way users have playlisted them and listened to them.” The company has analyzed over 700 million user-created playlists to train these collaborative filtering models — an enormous dataset of human music curation behavior, compressed into mathematical relationships.

To recommend you new music, the system finds your position on this map and surfaces nearby songs you haven’t heard yet — effectively saying, “users with taste profiles similar to yours also loved these tracks.”

The Limitation

But collaborative filtering alone isn’t perfect. Here’s a classic example directly from the CNBC documentary: during the holidays, Mariah Carey’s “All I Want for Christmas Is You” gets playlisted frequently alongside “Silent Night.” One is a pop anthem; the other is a traditional Christmas carol. If Spotify relied purely on co-occurrence data, a pop listener might get flooded with carols — not exactly what they had in mind.

Collaborative filtering also struggles with the popularity bias problem — popular tracks generate more data, making them easier to recommend, while niche or new tracks with limited interaction history get left behind. That’s where the next two pillars become essential.

Pillar 2: Natural Language Processing — Reading the Internet’s Mind

The second major limitation of collaborative filtering is the cold start problem: when a brand-new song drops, there’s zero user interaction data. Nobody has playlisted it, saved it, or skipped it yet. So how does Spotify figure out who might enjoy it?

Crawling the Cultural Conversation

Spotify’s NLP models systematically crawl the internet — including blog posts, music reviews, social media discussions, artist interviews, news articles, and user-generated playlist titles — to construct a cultural and semantic profile of every song and artist.

If a new track keeps appearing in playlists titled “late night drives” or “rainy day vibes,” the system learns its mood without anyone manually tagging it. If music blogs are calling an artist “the next Tame Impala,” the NLP models detect that association and can recommend their music to Tame Impala listeners — even before any collaborative filtering data exists.

Lyric Analysis

Spotify also uses NLP to analyze lyrics directly. As the CNBC documentary explains, the system studies lyrics and analyzes “the adjectives used to describe the track in articles and blogs.” A song heavy on words with melancholic connotations gets classified differently from an upbeat party anthem, and those classifications feed directly into the recommendation engine.

Why This Matters

This is what allows Spotify to surface music from completely unknown artists. If there’s enough online buzz — even from niche music blogs, Reddit threads, or a handful of social media posts — the NLP pipeline picks it up and feeds it into the system. It’s also why an artist’s online presence and press coverage can directly influence their algorithmic visibility, even if their stream counts are still low.

Pillar 3: Audio Analysis — Listening to the Music Itself

This is where Spotify’s system gets genuinely impressive from a technical standpoint — and where some of the most interesting ethical questions emerge.

How Machines “Hear” Music

Spotify doesn’t just rely on metadata and user behavior — it actually “listens” to every single song using machine learning models that process the raw audio signal. The system converts audio into spectrograms (visual representations of sound frequencies over time) and feeds them through convolutional neural networks (CNNs) — the same type of deep learning architecture used in image recognition systems.

The 13 Audio Features

According to Spotify’s public API documentation, their audio analysis model evaluates 13 distinct features for every track:

1. Tempo — speed of the track in beats per minute (BPM)

2. Key — the musical key the track is in (C, C#, D, etc.)

3. Mode — whether the track is in a major or minor key

4. Time signature — the rhythmic framework (3/4, 4/4, etc.)

5. Danceability — how suitable a song is for dancing, based on tempo, rhythm stability, and beat strength

6. Energy — the intensity and activity level (death metal scores high, a piano ballad scores low)

7. Valence — how positive or negative the song sounds emotionally

8. Loudness — the average decibel level

9. Speechiness — how much spoken word vs. singing is present

10. Acousticness — the probability that the track uses acoustic instruments

11. Instrumentalness — whether the track is primarily instrumental

12. Liveness — whether the track sounds like a live recording

13. Duration — the length of the track

To put this in perspective, the CNBC documentary shows that Bruno Mars’ “Uptown Funk” scores a 0.856 out of 1.0 on danceability. The system even maps out the temporal structure of each track — the beats, bars, and sections — creating a complete structural blueprint of the song.

Solving Cold Start with Sound

This pillar is especially powerful when combined with NLP for solving the cold start problem. A brand-new track with zero streams can still be recommended to the right listeners if its audio fingerprint — its tempo, energy, key, and mood — matches the sonic profile of tracks those listeners already enjoy. The algorithm identifies songs with similar “sonic DNA” regardless of the artist’s fame or marketing budget.

How the Three Pillars Merge Into One Engine

None of these pillars operates in isolation. Spotify runs a hybrid recommendation engine that fuses the outputs from all three systems into a unified ranking.

The Final Neural Network

At the last stage, a higher-level neural network takes inputs from all three sources — the collaborative filtering vectors, the NLP-derived cultural profile, and the audio feature vectors — along with contextual signals like:

• Time of day — your morning playlist taste likely differs from your late-night preferences

• Device type — mobile users may prefer shorter tracks

• Recent session behavior — what you’ve been listening to in the past hour

• Multiple taste profiles — Spotify tracks different “versions” of you (your workout self vs. your study self vs. your weekend self)

This neural network produces a confidence score for each candidate song — essentially answering: “Given everything we know about this listener, in this context, at this moment, how likely are they to enjoy this track?” The top 30 songs by confidence score become your Monday morning Discover Weekly.

Beyond Discover Weekly

It’s worth noting that this same engine, with slight variations, powers all of Spotify’s algorithmic features: Release Radar (new music from artists you follow), Daily Mixes (genre-clustered playlists), the AI DJ (introduced in 2023 with a synthetic voice), and Radio stations. As Spotify’s own engineers note: “Discover Weekly, Release Radar, Daily Mixes, Radio, and the AI DJ are all powered by the same recommendation engine, each serving a slightly different purpose.”

The 2026 Shift: When Discovery Became Retention

If you’ve noticed your Discover Weekly feeling a bit predictable lately, you’re not imagining it. Over the past two years, Spotify has made a significant philosophical shift in how its algorithm operates — and it has major implications for both listeners and artists.

What Changed

The platform now prioritizes retention metrics over raw stream counts. According to industry analysis based on data from over 1,200 artist campaigns, the key metrics that now drive algorithmic placement in 2026 are:

• Save rate — did the listener add it to their library? (Tracks above 20% see dramatically better performance)

• Stream-to-listener ratio — do people replay it? (A ratio of 2.0+ is the benchmark)

• Skip rate — especially in the first 30 seconds (high early skips send a strong negative signal)

• Playlist adds — are listeners adding it to their personal playlists?

The weight of these retention signals has increased roughly threefold compared to previous years. A track with 1,000 truly engaged listeners (who save, replay, and playlist it) can dramatically outperform a track with 10,000 passive streams.

The Discovery vs. Retention Trade-off

The downside of this shift is real. Many longtime Spotify users report that Discover Weekly now feels like it’s cycling through the same familiar-sounding songs rather than introducing genuinely new discoveries. The algorithm has become more conservative — optimized to keep you listening and reduce skip rates rather than to challenge your musical horizons.

Spotify’s Response: Prompted Playlist

Spotify appears to recognize this tension. In late 2025, the company launched Prompted Playlist, a feature that lets Premium users steer the algorithm using natural language text prompts. Users can type things like “songs from artists who are headlining major tours right now” and get a personalized playlist that combines their taste profile with their stated intent. It’s an acknowledgment that purely algorithmic recommendation has limits, and that human intent still matters.

Spotify’s engineering leadership has described this as “a new phase where listeners take the lead” — a signal that the future of recommendation may involve more collaboration between humans and algorithms rather than purely passive, behavior-driven systems.

The AI Ethics Questions We Should Be Asking

This is where the article shifts from technical review to critical analysis — and where it becomes especially relevant to our department’s focus on AI ethics and computational skepticism.

1. Cultural Bias in Audio Analysis

Researcher Thomas Hodgson, who studies how streaming technology impacts artists, raises a fundamental concern in the CNBC documentary: Spotify’s audio analysis uses Western music theory concepts to categorize all music globally. Key signatures, the equal temperament scale, and concepts like “danceability” are rooted in Western musical traditions.

Hodgson gives a striking example: a North Indian classical track gets algorithmically labeled as being in “E minor” — a classification he argues is entirely inappropriate for that musical tradition, which operates on a completely different tonal system (ragas, not Western keys). “In other parts of the world,” he notes, “they have musical systems and musical cultures that are entirely different.”

The implication is significant: the algorithm may systematically misunderstand and misclassify non-Western music, leading to poor recommendations for listeners of those traditions and reduced visibility for artists from those cultures.

2. Feedback Loops and Representation Bias

The algorithm can create self-reinforcing feedback loops where existing biases in the catalog get amplified over time. Hodgson explains: “This could mean that a particular catalog of music has more male artists than female artists. One of the dangers with machine learning is that as listeners start to engage with that catalog, those biases become magnified.”

The models trained on historically biased data recommend those same biased patterns more frequently, which generates more interaction data, which further entrenches the bias. This is a textbook example of algorithmic amplification — a concept that should be central to how we think about AI system design in information systems.

3. The Classical Music Metadata Problem

Classical music presents a unique metadata challenge that exposes the limitations of Spotify’s one-size-fits-all approach. A Tchaikovsky recording might include the work name, movement, opus number, conductor, orchestra, soloists, and recording venue — all of which are musically significant. But Spotify’s algorithm isn’t optimized to handle this complexity, often treating the conductor or orchestra as the “artist” in ways that don’t map to how classical music listeners actually think about their preferences.

Apple Music recognized this gap and released a dedicated classical music app specifically designed to address it — a tacit acknowledgment that general-purpose recommendation algorithms struggle with specialized musical domains.

4. Who Benefits from the Retention Shift?

The 2026 shift toward retention metrics raises a broader question: who does this algorithm actually serve? When Spotify optimizes for saves and replays over adventurous discovery, it benefits established artists with proven audience engagement while making it harder for emerging artists to break through. It also benefits Spotify’s business metrics (user retention, time on platform) potentially at the expense of the listener’s genuine interest in discovering new music.

This is a classic tension in information systems design: the system’s optimization objectives may not align with the user’s actual goals. Spotify wants you to keep listening. You want to discover something you’ve never heard before. Those aren’t always the same thing.

Why This Matters for Information Systems Students

The math behind Discover Weekly isn’t just a Spotify story. The same core techniques — collaborative filtering, NLP, content-based filtering, and deep neural networks — power recommendations on Netflix (what you watch), YouTube (what you click), Amazon (what you buy), TikTok (what you scroll), and dozens of other platforms.

Recommendation engines are quietly shaping what billions of people see, hear, read, and purchase every single day. And the ethical questions raised by Spotify’s system — cultural bias, feedback loops, misaligned incentives, the tension between engagement and genuine value — apply to virtually every AI-driven recommendation system in existence.

Whether you end up building these systems, auditing them for bias, designing user experiences around them, or making policy decisions about their regulation, understanding both the technical architecture and the ethical implications is foundational to our work as information systems professionals.

Conclusion

The next time your Discover Weekly serves you a perfect track from an artist you’ve never heard of, take a second to appreciate the full stack of what just happened: matrix factorization identified your taste cluster among 500 million users, NLP scanned thousands of blogs and playlists for cultural context, a CNN analyzed the raw audio waveform across 13 distinct features, and a neural network ranked millions of candidates — all to make that one moment of discovery feel completely effortless.

That’s the math behind the magic. But as we’ve seen, the magic comes with trade-offs — in fairness, in cultural representation, and in what “good” recommendation even means. Those are the questions that will define the next generation of AI systems.

And those are the questions we, as future information systems professionals, should be asking.
