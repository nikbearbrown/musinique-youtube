You arrive in Boston in May. The city has just finished being cold. You expected spring in the way your body remembers it from wherever you came from, that gentle arrival of warmth, the slow persuasion of flowers. Boston doesn’t do persuasion. It does rupture. One week it’s 45 degrees and raining. The next week it’s 75 and everyone is on the Esplanade like they’ve been released from something. You’re supposed to know what to do with this. You don’t yet.

This is fine. The first summer is always an education in what you didn’t know to prepare for.

The Library Card as Moral Infrastructure

Get a library card. Not the digital kind, the physical one, the one that lives in your wallet and gets you into places. The Boston Public Library will give you one if you can prove you live here. Bring your passport. Bring proof of address. Go to the Central Library in Copley Square.

This card is not symbolic. It’s how the city works.

With it, you can reserve museum passes that will save you $25 at the Museum of Science, $20 at the New England Aquarium, $10 at the MFA. You’ll do this through the library’s online reservation system, refresh at midnight and early morning, plan two weeks ahead, learn which branches have better availability. This is how you see the city when you are not rich.

The Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum is $5 with a library promo code. This is the single best deal in Boston. You will stand in front of the empty frame where the Vermeer used to be before someone stole it in 1990, and you will think about what it means to preserve the space where a thing used to be. This is worth $5.

What the Weather Will Do to You

The average high in May is 66°F, but that number is a lie. Some days it will be 55 and windy. Other days it will be 78. By July, the heat index will push past 100°F. Air conditioning becomes a survival strategy. You’ll start planning your day around which public spaces have it: the library, the museums, any university building that still lets you in.

You will learn to dress in layers in May, to carry a water bottle everywhere by July, to move slowly in August.

Sailing

Boston is a harbor city pretending to be a college town. You can learn to sail here. You can learn for cheap.

Community Boating charges $99 for a 30-day trial membership. This gets you lessons, boat access, and the sudden realization that wind is not a metaphor but a physical force you have to negotiate with your body.

Here’s what they don’t tell you: learning to sail is learning to fail in front of other people. You will capsize. You will forget to tack. You will look at the wind and have no idea where it’s coming from. And then, one day in July, you will be out on the water by yourself, and the boat will do what you tell it to do, and you will understand why people do this.

If you show up on May 16, 2026, the Open House, they’ll take you out for a free 20-minute sail and knock $50 off the full-year membership. The full-year membership is $359 for students. Unlimited sailing from May to October.

Free Things in May

May 2026: Lilac Sunday at the Arnold Arboretum. The one day a year you’re allowed to picnic on the grass. Sit under the 400 lilac bushes with your friends and your cheap wine and your takeout. Take the Orange Line to Forest Hills and walk ten minutes.

Starting mid-May, the Rose Kennedy Greenway runs free fitness classes, yoga, HIIT, dance cardio. These are “I just spent $35 at Equinox” classes, except they’re free and outside.

How to Move

The MBTA works well enough if you build in buffer time and accept that the Orange Line will be delayed for “signal problems” at least twice a month. The full LinkPass is $80/month and gets you unlimited subway and bus.

But here’s the real move: Bluebikes. $75.50 for students for a full year. Unlimited 45-minute rides. This covers the distance between most campuses and most places you need to go. You will learn to bike in traffic, which is terrifying for exactly three weeks, and then it’s just how you move.

If you’re low-income or on SNAP, the Bluebikes pass is $5 for the year. They want you to have it. Take it.

What 2026 Gives You

This summer is not a normal summer. Boston is hosting World Cup matches in June and Sail Boston, an international gathering of tall ships in July. Both free to attend if you know where to stand.

The FIFA Fan Festival runs June 12–28 at City Hall Plaza. Free admission, but register in advance. Big screens, food trucks, people from every country on earth who are also in Boston for reasons that seemed like good ideas six months ago.

Sail Boston is July 11–16. Parade of Sail on July 13. Watch from the Seaport, from Charlestown, from East Boston. These are 19th-century sailing vessels moving through the harbor like the past is still here and just needed an invitation.

Starting in July, the city runs free outdoor movie nights. Hatch Shell on the Esplanade, Prudential Center, Castle Island. You bring a blanket. You show up an hour early if you want a good spot. The movies are crowd-pleasers Hamilton, Mamma Mia!, Superman.

Castle Island is the best location. It’s on the water. The breeze helps. There’s Sullivan’s, which serves fried clams and has been doing so since 1951. The fort is free. The movie is free. The clams are $18. You’ll buy the clams.

What You’re Actually Learning

This essay is not really about sailing or museum passes or free movies. It’s about what you learn when you arrive in a city that is expensive and old and uninterested in making things easy for you. You learn to read a public library website like it’s a map. You learn that 66 degrees in May requires a jacket but 66 degrees in September does not, and you can’t explain why, you just know.

You learn that a city is not a place you visit. It’s a place you negotiate. The negotiation is: I don’t have much money, but I live here. What do I get?

And Boston says: here’s a library card. Here’s a sailing lesson. Here’s a festival in a public park. Here’s a movie on the Esplanade at sunset. Here’s how to be here.

The first summer is the summer you figure out whether this city is a place you can live or a place you’re just enduring until you can leave. The difference is whether you learned to use what the city gives you, which is not comfort, and not ease, but access. Access to beauty. Access to water. Access to history. Access to the idea that a public institution is not a backup plan for people who can’t afford the private version, it’s the plan.

You’ll learn this, or you won’t. But May is when you start.
