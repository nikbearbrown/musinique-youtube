The Violence of the Obvious

The university’s winter survival guide for international students contains a sentence I keep returning to: the air, it explains, “actually hurts the face.” This is the document getting as close to honesty as institutional prose allows. And if you read the whole thing slowly enough, you begin to see the outline of something it never quite names.

Northeastern’s guide is a bureaucratic act of love — technically rigorous, deeply earnest, and genuinely useful to a student arriving from Chennai or Lagos or Bogotá who has never experienced a wind chill of negative nineteen degrees Fahrenheit. It contains tables. It expresses concern for your wellbeing. It describes Boston winter as an “extreme atmospheric irregularity” — which is one way to say that the air will damage you if you are not prepared for it. The guide prefers “challenging” to “hostile,” “complex” to “dangerous,” and this preference is not dishonesty. It is institutional prose operating at the edge of its register, trying to be honest about a place that is, by the clinical measure of what it does to human bodies unaccustomed to it, genuinely difficult.

What strikes me, reading through 3,000 words of layering systems and tunnel maps and emergency fund tables, is how much effort a major research university has invested in teaching someone how to survive a place they chose to come to. And how much that effort reveals about what is not being said — about the nature of welcome, the architecture of belonging, and the cost, measured in Vitamin D supplements and waterproof boot technology, of education conducted across the fault lines of climate and culture.

The Three-Layer System as Epistemology

The guide’s central technical contribution is its Three-Layer System: base layer for moisture management, mid layer for thermal retention, shell for environmental barrier. The logic is elegant. The body generates heat. The environment strips it. The question is which materials, in which configuration, can mediate between these two facts long enough for you to get to class.

What the guide does not say — cannot say, in the register it has chosen — is that this system is also a metaphor for what international students are being asked to do more broadly. There is a base layer of self, the private warmth you carry from wherever you came from. There is a mid layer of adaptation, the fleece of acquired habit and modified expectation. And there is the shell: the windproof, waterproof surface you present to the environment that will, on a day in February when the temperature is twelve degrees and the wind is coming off the Atlantic at twenty-five miles an hour, determine whether you make it to your 9 AM lecture without incident.

The document is specifically worried about cotton. “Cotton kills” is the industry standard it cites. Cotton traps moisture against the skin, creates a post-commute chill, can lower core body temperature in conditions that are not even extreme. What the guide is describing, in the language of textile science, is the danger of bringing the wrong material into a new climate. The student who arrives wearing what worked at home — not just the fabric, but the assumptions, the rhythms, the social textures of a different place — is the student most at risk.

Every student arrives with a cotton layer. The university’s job, apparently, is to explain why it needs to be replaced.

What Vitamin D Actually Measures

Here is where I want to stop and sit with something. The guide cites a study from Tufts University suggesting that low serum Vitamin D levels — deficient in 90% of Boston-area students during winter — correlate with a 3.87 odds ratio for anxiety and a 75% increase in stress levels compared to sufficient cohorts. These are not casual statistics. An odds ratio of 3.87 means Vitamin D-deficient students are nearly four times more likely to develop anxiety than their sufficient counterparts. The document presents this as a medical finding. It is also a structural one.

The students most likely to be Vitamin D deficient are the students arriving from latitudes closer to the equator — from South Asia, West Africa, Southeast Asia, the Caribbean, Latin America. Students whose bodies evolved for a different sun angle. The guide recommends 2,000 IU of supplemental Vitamin D3 daily, more than three times the standard RDA, and this is good, practical advice. But it is also, if you let yourself think about it for a moment, a remarkable thing to say to someone. Your biology is not calibrated for this place. Here is how to compensate.

The Vitamin D problem — the Seasonal Affective Disorder problem, the 4:11 PM sunset problem — is not equally distributed across the student body. The students most at risk are the students who have traveled the farthest. Who have crossed the most oceans. Who have the most to lose if the winter breaks them before the spring arrives. The document knows this. It does not say it. Instead it offers a table with clinical observations and recommended interventions, and if you read the table slowly, the outline of an injustice — minor, structural, almost invisible — begins to appear.

The guide is trying to help. Its help is real. But it can’t close the gap between institutional information and the loneliness of being biochemically unsuited to the place you need to succeed.

The Tunnel System and the Question of Belonging

Northeastern’s subterranean infrastructure is, according to the guide, “a vital adaptation to the Arctic conditions of a Boston winter.” Sixteen thousand square feet of tunnels connecting eleven major buildings. Color-coded walls. A Dunkin’ Donuts accessible without exposing yourself to the cold. The students who use it call it a lifeline.

I find myself thinking about what it means to build an institution underground. Not metaphorically — literally, to construct pathways beneath the campus so that the people who use it can navigate it without being exposed to the climate in which it exists. This is an engineering solution to a political fact: we have built a global institution in a place that is, for roughly five months of the year, inhospitable to a significant portion of the people we have invited to attend it.

The tunnels do not reach the residence halls. They do not extend to International Village, the dormitory where many international students live, because the Stony Brook Conduit — a massive underground water structure — prevents further excavation. This is a purely geological constraint. It means, in practice, that the students most likely to need the tunnels are the students least able to access them from their front doors. You can read this as an infrastructure problem. You can also read it as an accidental figure for something larger: the ways institutions build systems of accommodation that stop just short of where the accommodation is most needed.

What the Document Cannot Say

The document does not only ask students to survive the climate. It also asks them to love it.

There is a section on the “Beanpot,” the historic hockey tournament between Northeastern, Harvard, BU, and Boston College. “For Northeastern students,” the guide explains, “the Beanpot is a primary cultural touchstone and a major quintessential Boston winter experience.” This is true. It is also, if you have come from Ahmedabad or Lagos or Manila or Bogotá, a sentence that asks you to perform a cultural fluency you may not yet possess. The document is not wrong to mention the Beanpot. College hockey in Boston in February is, genuinely, a thing that will make the winter feel more livable if you let it. But the distance between “primary cultural touchstone” and I understand why this matters is not closed by a table in a survival guide.

What the guide is trying to do — and what it mostly succeeds at doing, within its constraints — is give students the information they need to physically and logistically survive the spring semester. What it cannot do, because no document can, is account for the particular loneliness of being new to a place that is cold in ways you were not prepared for. The 4:11 PM darkness. The weight of the shell layer on your shoulders every time you leave the building. The way the campus empties on weekends and the tunnels echo.

The guide mentions the “loneliness epidemic that peaks during late winter” in the context of a Wellness Week featuring Meditation and Mango Lassi. This is a genuine attempt at cultural specificity — the mango lassi is not incidental — and I want to credit it as such. It is also, if you are being honest about scale, a small warm drink against a large cold fact.

The Thing That Survives

The international students who come to Northeastern in January are deciding that it is worth it. The cold, the darkness, the biochemical adjustment, the cultural recalibration, the replacement of every assumption they brought with them about what a day in late January should feel like — all of it is a price they have calculated and agreed to pay. The guide is useful precisely because it helps them understand what they are paying for. But the decision to pay it, the willingness to be remade by weather and circumstance and the particular demands of a place that was not built for you — that is something the document can describe but cannot create.

Boston does not ask permission to be what it is. February in the Fens is February in the Fens whether or not you have packed correctly. The question the guide is quietly asking, underneath all the layering systems and tunnel maps and emergency fund tables, is whether you know what you are walking into.

Most of them do. Most of them have been walking into things like this their entire lives. What the document can give them is information. What they bring with them is everything else.

That is enough. That has always been enough. The spring comes.
