In 1909, eight engineering students were placed inside four companies, the Boston Consolidated Gas Company, the Boston Elevated Railway, the Boston and Albany Railroad, the Boston and Maine Railroad and told that this was also their education. Frank Palmer Speare, Northeastern’s first president, called it a “bold experiment.” What he’d actually done was recognize something simpler: the city already knew things the classroom didn’t. All you had to do was put the student inside it.

More than a century later, a student boards the Orange Line at Ruggles at 6:47 AM and is already at work. Not metaphorically. They may be heading to a co-op at the MBTA itself, embedded in a $9.6 billion capital investment plan to transform the nation’s oldest public transit system into a functional regional rail network. Their daily commute is also their research site. The delay on the platform is data. That simultaneity: student and worker, learner and participant, inside the system and riding it home is what the co-op model has always been trying to produce. Whether it succeeds depends on something no administrator would put in a brochure: whether the student actually shows up to the city.

The Model and Who It Left Out

Boston’s history as a place of reinvention is also, unavoidably, a history of who gets to reinvent themselves.

The co-op program’s origin story is usually told without that sentence. Women were admitted to Northeastern in 1943 but faced employers who refused to hire them for placements for decades after. Black students encountered the same wall. The program was revolutionary in form and inherited in its exclusions. It took a 1969 petition from women in the Business Administration program, explicitly accusing employers of discrimination, and a 1964 Ford Foundation grant funding a pilot for Black students from the Boston area, to begin closing the gap between what the program claimed to be and what it actually was.

The program learned, slowly, that the city it was plugging students into was not neutral. Neither was the plug.

What remains is an institution still renegotiating that history and still shaped by the city that forced the reckoning. Students who trace the buried path of the Stony Brook River learn why the roads curve where they do. Those who walk past the unmarked site near campus where 60 enslaved people once lived learn that the built environment is never accidental. That literacy is the foundation on which every technical co-op rests. You cannot do honest work in a city you don’t understand.

What Husky Life Actually Looks Like

Standard internships put you near the work. Co-op puts you in the system then asks you to ride it home.

Mission Hill sits between the Longwood Medical and Academic Area and Roxbury. Walking from student housing to a co-op at Brigham and Women’s takes you through brick rowhouses and the Roxbury puddingstone of the Basilica before depositing you in one of the most powerful medical complexes in the world. That transition takes twenty minutes. You feel it. In the labs of the Longwood Medical Area, students investigate irisin an exercise hormone with potential applications for Alzheimer’s at the edge of research that hasn’t yet resolved into clinical practice. The ambiguity is the education.

For international students, more than a quarter of Northeastern’s enrollment, the city offers a particular and sometimes difficult pedagogy. The work culture, as one industrial engineering student from India described it, is “very different.” Not better or worse. Different in its assumptions about directness, hierarchy, and self-advocacy. That translation happens in real time, in real workplaces, with real stakes. No orientation program replaces it.

And those workplaces, right now, are changing faster than the syllabi can track.

What the City Is Becoming

That same Orange Line the student rides to their MBTA co-op is also a climate story. The T has a zero-emissions fleet target for 2040. Getting there requires electrification, fleet scheduling software that tracks each vehicle’s state of charge before dispatch, and optimization models that have to actually work not just demo well. Students embedded in that modernization are doing data and AI work in service of a climate problem. The two things are not separate tracks. They are the same track.

That connection: between computational rigor and environmental consequence is what Boston’s next phase of co-op work keeps forcing into view. The most useful insight about AI isn’t that it’s powerful. It’s that a deployment either does what it claims to do, or it produces an expensive and convincing simulation of doing it. The same test applies to climate interventions. Students have documented heat island disparities of up to 15 degrees between traditionally redlined Boston neighborhoods and their greener neighbors. The data makes abstract policy concrete in about thirty seconds. It also demands the same diagnostic honesty that good AI work requires: is this actually working, or does it just look like it’s working?

The Institute for Experiential AI and the Roux Institute have built a framework around exactly that question. Does this project address a real business goal, or is it technology enthusiasm looking for a problem? Is the data trustworthy? At the Roux, organizations bring live challenges to graduate students, L.L.Bean on supply-chain analytics, regional banks on recommendation systems. It’s the same model that began with the Boston railroads in 1909. The problems are different. The discipline is the same: put the student inside something real and see what they learn.

The students arriving in the next cohort will be less observers of that transformation and more participants in it. That’s not marketing language. It’s a description of what the placements actually require.

There is a moment, usually quiet and unannounced, when this kind of education crystallizes. It’s not the offer letter. It’s not the thesis defense. It’s the moment you realize the city has been teaching you something you didn’t know you were learning: on the Orange Line, in a hospital corridor at six in the morning, in a heat-stressed Roxbury block in August.

Boston is a palimpsest. The old writing is always beneath the new. What Northeastern’s model bets on is that a student who reads both layers comes out knowing something a classroom couldn’t have taught. Not because the classroom is insufficient. Because the city is irreplaceable.

That is what the living lab actually is. Not a pedagogy. A city that refuses to let you stay theoretical.
