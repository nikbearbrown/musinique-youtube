The Northeastern MGEN resume workshop teaches students to write bullet points in a specific formula: past-tense verb, quantifiable action, measurable result. Not “led a team” but “managed 20 engineers whose combined output reached 112% of target.” This is training in how to make yourself legible to employers who need to process hundreds of applications efficiently. By the time you land your co-op, you’ll have learned to argue for your own employability in a language that works.

Here’s what most students miss: the summer before your co-op search is not dead time. It’s the period when you build the portfolio that makes the search successful. The MGEN programs: Information Systems, Software Engineering, Data Architecture, Engineering Management, Cyber-Physical Systems, and Telecommunication Networks, provide the infrastructure. What you do with it determines whether your resume gets past the first screen.

This guide is for students who want to use their summer strategically.

Build the Portfolio Project That Actually Matters

The co-op search requires evidence of competence, not just coursework. Course assignments are designed to teach concepts. Portfolio projects are designed to prove you can ship.

For MSIS and MSSE students: Build a full-stack application with the MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React, Node.js). This is stack-standard, which is the point. Employers hiring from a pool of 200 MGEN applicants aren’t looking for novelty, they’re looking for fluency. Your project should prove you can work inside existing paradigms, not invent new ones. Include these components: frontend design with a framework like Tailwind CSS or Shadcn, backend API handling with JWT authentication, and database management with dynamic content through an admin panel. Host it on GitHub with a comprehensive README that explains what problem it solves, how you built it, and what technical decisions you made. Deploy it live using services like Vercel, Netlify, or AWS Amplify.

For DAAM students: Build an end-to-end data pipeline that demonstrates you can move data from collection to insight. Process streaming data using Kafka and Spark, public datasets like NYC taxi data, weather APIs, or social media sentiment streams work well as starting points. Store and query the results in a cloud data warehouse like BigQuery, Snowflake, or AWS Redshift. Create an interactive dashboard using Tableau, Power BI, or Apache Superset that visualizes the insights. Document your data quality checks, transformation logic, and performance optimizations. This proves you understand the complete data engineering lifecycle, not just individual tools.

For MSEM students: Document a real optimization project using quantitative methods. If you’ve worked in operations, supply chain, or project management, model a resource allocation problem using operations research techniques, linear programming, integer programming, or simulation. Show the business problem, the mathematical model formulation, the solution approach, and the measurable result. Include your code (Python with PuLP or OR-Tools, Excel with Solver, or specialized software like AMPL) and visualize the before/after impact. Employers hiring engineering managers need evidence you can translate business problems into quantitative solutions and communicate the results to non-technical stakeholders.

For CPS students: Build something that demonstrates hardware-software integration. Edge computing projects using Raspberry Pi or Arduino, IoT security audits that identify vulnerabilities in connected devices, or embedded systems work that shows you can program microcontrollers and interface with sensors all signal the cross-disciplinary competence that cyber-physical roles demand. Document your circuit diagrams, code, and test results. If you’re working with security, demonstrate the vulnerability, explain the exploit, and document the mitigation.

The summer is when you have time to build something substantial. Don’t waste it on tutorials alone, follow one tutorial to learn the tools, then build something original that becomes the centerpiece of your portfolio.

Get the Certifications That Actually Signal Competence

Certifications validate what you’ve learned and provide external proof that survives the resume screening filter. They also shorten the path from “qualified” to “hired.”

For management-focused students (MSEM): Northeastern’s Engineering Management program is certified by the American Society for Engineering Management (ASEM). Graduates have a streamlined path to becoming a Certified Associate in Engineering Management (CAEM) or, with additional experience, a Professional Engineering Manager (PEM). These credentials signal adherence to the Engineering Management Body of Knowledge and are recognized globally. Study for the CAEM this summer while you’re not juggling coursework, the exam covers project management, organizational leadership, engineering economics, and operations management.

For software-focused students (MSIS, MSSE): The IEEE Computer Society offers certifications that validate software engineering competency. Research which certifications align with your target roles, whether that’s software development, systems architecture, or quality assurance. Additionally, consider cloud and DevOps certifications like AWS Certified Developer or HashiCorp Terraform Associate if you’re targeting infrastructure or platform engineering positions.

For cloud and infrastructure roles: AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate (SAA-C03) or Microsoft Azure Fundamentals (AZ-900) followed by Azure Administrator (AZ-104) validate architectural design skills. These are increasingly table stakes for cloud engineering positions. The exam prep typically takes 40–60 hours of study time. Summer is when you have that time. Use official practice exams, hands-on labs in AWS Free Tier or Azure free credits, and study resources from A Cloud Guru, Udemy, or Linux Academy.

For data governance and architecture roles (DAAM, MSIS): The Data Management Association (DAMA) offers the Certified Data Management Professional (CDMP) credential, which validates expertise in data governance, data quality, and metadata management. If you’re targeting data architecture or data engineering positions at enterprises with mature data programs, this signals you understand governance frameworks and compliance requirements, not just technical execution.

Certifications cost money ($150–$300 per exam typically) and time. But they provide something course grades cannot: external validation that moves your resume from the “maybe” pile to the “interview” pile when employers are hiring from a large pool of MGEN graduates.

Use the University’s Summer Resources Strategically

Most MGEN students ignore the free learning platforms that could fill resume gaps in 10–20 hours. Here’s what’s actually worth your time.

LinkedIn Learning: Northeastern provides unlimited access to thousands of courses. Use the summer to fill specific technical gaps that your coursework hasn’t covered yet. Recommended high-value courses: “Docker Essential Training” (if you haven’t containerized applications), “Learning Kubernetes” (critical for cloud-native development), “Advanced Python” (if you’re comfortable with basics but need to level up), “AWS Essential Training” (if you’re preparing for Solutions Architect certification), and “DevOps Foundations: CI/CD” (essential for modern software engineering). Each course provides a certificate you can add to your LinkedIn profile, not as valuable as official certifications, but better than nothing when you’re listing skills.

Gartner Research: Your Northeastern credentials give you access to Gartner’s technology research database. Use this strategically to understand the industries you’re targeting. If you’re interviewing with fintech companies in the fall, spend summer reading Gartner’s reports on embedded finance, payment infrastructure, and digital banking platforms. If you’re targeting healthcare tech, read up on FHIR standards, EHR interoperability, and healthcare cloud adoption trends. Come to co-op interviews knowing the difference between strategic priorities and tactical implementations. When you can reference a Gartner Magic Quadrant during a technical discussion, it signals you understand business context, not just code.

Summer Skills Sessions: The College of Science offers workshops for incoming students covering research proposal writing, technical presentation skills, and career panels with industry professionals. Even if you’re not in COS, many of these sessions are open to graduate students across programs. Attend them, the skills transfer directly to co-op applications and interviews.

Coding practice platforms: Use HackerRank, LeetCode, or CodeSignal to practice technical interview questions. Aim for 2–3 problems per week throughout summer. Focus on medium-difficulty problems in data structures (arrays, linked lists, trees, hash tables) and algorithms (sorting, searching, dynamic programming). Many co-op technical interviews will include at least one live coding component.

Prepare for the Interview Ecosystem Before You’re In It

The co-op interview process is multifaceted: behavioral, technical, and sometimes case-based. Summer is when you prepare the frameworks that make interviews manageable instead of overwhelming.

Prepare 4–7 STAR stories. Situation, Task, Action, Result, this is the backbone of behavioral interviews. The questions are predictable: “Tell me about a time you led a team.” “Tell me about a time you failed and what you learned.” “Tell me about a time you solved a complex problem under pressure.” “Tell me about a conflict with a teammate and how you resolved it.” Write out your stories now, while you have time to think through the details. Each story should be 90–120 seconds when spoken aloud. Practice delivering them until they feel natural, not memorized. Record yourself and listen back, you’ll catch verbal tics, filler words, and places where you’re rushing or losing the thread.

Practice technical explanations. You’ll be asked to walk through your portfolio projects in high-level detail. Practice explaining your MERN stack app, your data pipeline, or your optimization model as if you’re talking to a non-technical stakeholder: What problem does it solve? Why did you choose this architecture? What were the tradeoffs? What issues arose during development and how did you debug them? Practice this until you can do it without drowning the listener in jargon or skipping over the interesting decisions. Have a friend or classmate interview you their confused facial expressions will tell you where your explanation lost clarity.

Prepare for case interviews. Engineering management and product-focused co-ops sometimes include case questions: “How would you optimize warehouse logistics for an e-commerce company expanding to same-day delivery?” or “Estimate the number of servers needed to support a new feature that we expect will double our user traffic.” The skill being tested is structured thinking under pressure, not perfect accuracy. Practice frameworks: define the problem clearly, break it into manageable components, state your assumptions explicitly, solve step-by-step showing your work, and sanity-check your final answer. Use the “Market Sizing and Guesstimation” resources from Case Interview Prep platforms, or practice with classmates using real business scenarios.

Research companies before applying. Don’t wait until you get an interview invitation. Spend 15–20 minutes researching each company when you submit your application. Read their engineering blog if they have one. Check their GitHub organization for open-source projects. Look up their tech stack on StackShare or by reading job descriptions for senior engineers. When you get the interview call, you’ll already know what technologies they use, what problems they’re solving, and what questions to ask.

Build the Professional Network That Opens Doors

The co-op search is not just about applications. It’s about referrals. Referrals bypass the ATS filter and land you directly in front of hiring managers. Summer is when you build the network infrastructure that generates those referrals in fall.

Reach 500+ LinkedIn connections before fall. The number matters because LinkedIn’s algorithm treats 500+ profiles differently, you appear more prominently in search results, your posts reach a wider network, and recruiters are more likely to find you. It’s not vanity, it’s how the platform determines who gets visibility. Connect with: current classmates (your immediate cohort across all MGEN programs), professors and teaching assistants from your courses, co-op coordinators and career advisors, alumni working at companies you’re targeting (use LinkedIn’s alumni search filtered by company and graduation year), and people you meet at events. Personalize every connection request: “Hi [Name], I’m an MGEN student in the [track] program at Northeastern. I saw you work at [Company] in [role], I’m really interested in [specific area] and would love to learn about your experience.” A personalized request has a 3× higher acceptance rate than a blank one.

Attend networking events even when they feel optional. Northeastern’s global campus network hosts site visits, alumni panels, and industry mixers in Boston, Seattle, Silicon Valley, Toronto, and Vancouver throughout the summer. Go to them. Prepare 2–3 good questions before you arrive: not “What’s it like to work there?” but “I noticed your team recently launched [feature], what were the biggest technical challenges?” or “How does your team balance technical debt with new feature development?” Ask questions that show you’ve done research. Collect business cards or LinkedIn profiles. Follow up with thank-you emails within 24 hours, referencing something specific from your conversation. This is how you get referrals, not by asking for them directly, but by building relationships where people want to refer you because they remember you as the student who asked good questions.

Join one student organization and contribute actively. Northeastern has several MGEN-relevant student organizations. Choose based on your interests and technical focus, then actually participate, don’t just add it to your resume. Attend meetings. Volunteer for projects. If the organization is building something (a workshop, a hackathon, a technical talk series), help organize it. This gives you leadership experience to reference in STAR stories, expands your peer network across MGEN tracks, and sometimes creates direct connections to industry sponsors who fund or mentor these organizations.

Engage with professional societies. Organizations like INFORMS (Institute for Operations Research and Management Sciences), ASEM (American Society for Engineering Management), IISE (Institute of Industrial and Systems Engineers), and GWISE (Graduate Women in Science and Engineering) host webinars, conferences, and networking events throughout the year. Many have student membership rates ($20–$50/year). Join one that aligns with your track. Attend their virtual events, these often include industry panels, technical talks, and networking sessions with working professionals who are explicitly looking to mentor students. These connections sometimes lead directly to co-op opportunities or referrals.

Prioritization guidance: If you only have time for one networking activity, prioritize LinkedIn connections, it’s the lowest-effort, highest-return activity that you can do in 10-minute increments. If you have time for two, add attending at least two in-person networking events before fall semester starts. Everything else is valuable but optional depending on your available time.

The Strategic Roadmap: Your Summer Checklist

Students who treat summer as downtime enter the fall scrambling. Students who treat summer as build time enter the fall with a portfolio, a network, and a resume that gets responses. Here’s the difference:

The summer checklist:

  1. Build one substantial portfolio project that demonstrates end-to-end competence in your focus area, full-stack app for MSIS/MSSE, data pipeline for DAAM, optimization model for MSEM, hardware-software integration for CPS.

  2. Earn one certification that validates your technical or management skills, CAEM for MSEM, AWS/Azure for cloud roles, relevant technical certifications for your specialization.

  3. Complete 3–5 targeted skill-building courses on LinkedIn Learning or other platforms that fill specific gaps in your technical stack.

  4. Prepare 4–7 STAR stories for behavioral interviews and practice technical explanations of your portfolio projects until they’re conversational.

  5. Reach 500+ LinkedIn connections by the end of summer and attend at least two networking events (alumni panels, site visits, or industry meetups).

  6. Join one student organization or professional society and contribute actively to at least one project or event before fall.

This is the preparation that makes your co-op search successful. The university provides the infrastructure. The certifications provide the signals. The co-op provides the opportunity. This summer is when you assemble the pieces. Do the work now, and when the co-op search opens in the fall, you’ll be ready.
