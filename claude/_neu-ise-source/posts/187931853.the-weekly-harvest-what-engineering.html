The Features in the Data Most Students Will Ignore

The dataset includes fields most students won’t examine:

Companies with GDPR compliance have legal/privacy infrastructure that extends to other regulatory domains (CCPA, SOC2, HIPAA). language field: Currently shows “en” for all jobs in this sample, but in a full dataset you’d see multilingual postings. Jobs that require bilingual skills (English + Spanish, English + Japanese, English + Mandarin) often pay 10-15% premiums and have smaller candidate pools. If you’re bilingual, this is a filter-in criterion—jobs that explicitly require your language combination have lower competition.

education field: Some jobs have “education_required” flags. This tells you which roles have hard credential requirements (typically roles in regulated industries—biotech, pharma, finance). If you have the degree they require, you’re competing in a smaller pool. If you don’t, don’t waste the application.

requisition_id vs job_id: These are different. The job_id is Greenhouse’s internal identifier. The requisition_id is the company’s internal headcount request number. If you see multiple jobs with the same requisition_id, that’s the same headcount allocation posted multiple times (possibly in different locations or with different title variations). Applying to both is redundant.

The CSV vs JSON Question Actually Matters

The data is available in both formats. Which should you use?

CSV if: You’re doing analysis in Excel, Google Sheets, or pandas and you want simple filtering/sorting. CSV is human-readable, easy to share, and compatible with every data tool. For quick filters (”show me all remote backend engineering jobs”), CSV is faster.

JSON if: You’re building a programmatic pipeline and need to preserve nested metadata structures. The metadata field likely contains structured data (application questions, department tags, custom fields) that flatten poorly into CSV. If you’re writing a script that processes job descriptions, extracts skills with NLP, or integrates with other APIs, JSON preserves data types and nesting.

For most students, start with CSV. If you need the metadata complexity later, switch to JSON. But don’t ignore the metadata field entirely—it often contains signals (hiring manager name, team size, reporting structure) that help you tailor applications.

The Sponsorship Probability Model You Should Build

The dataset doesn’t tell you which companies sponsor H-1B visas, but you can build a model to estimate probability:

Feature 1: Company size (job count as proxy). Companies with 100+ jobs in this dataset are large enough to have legal teams and HR infrastructure. H-1B sponsorship probability: 75-90%. Companies with 1-5 jobs are pre-infrastructure. Sponsorship probability: 10-25%.

Feature 2: Prior H-1B filings (requires external data source). The DOL publishes H-1B disclosure data. Cross-reference company_name from this dataset with USCIS H-1B petition records from 2023-2025. If the company filed 50+ H-1B petitions in the last 3 years, they have established processes. If they filed 0, they either don’t sponsor or only sponsor exceptionally.

Feature 3: Role type. Software engineering roles have higher sponsorship rates than marketing/sales roles because technical skills are more globally scarce. ML/AI roles have even higher rates (specialized expertise, global talent pool). Entry-level customer support roles have near-zero sponsorship rates (easy to fill domestically).

Feature 4: Location requirements. Roles that specify “must be located in [specific state]” for tax/legal reasons often won’t sponsor because they need you to already have work authorization in that state. Roles that say “Remote - US” with no qualifier are more likely to sponsor because they’ve already accepted geographic flexibility.

Combine these features into a weighted score. Filter for sponsorship_probability > 0.6. Now you’re applying to 1,800-2,400 jobs instead of 6,718, and your application effort is concentrated on companies where the visa question is most likely to have a yes answer.

Hour 6: Apply to your top 20 jobs from Priority 1 list. These are companies with high job counts, documented sponsorship history, and roles that match your skills. You’re aiming for quality applications (customized resume, thoughtful cover letter that references specific company details), not bulk submissions. 20 applications in one hour is aggressive but achievable if your templates are good.

Next week: download the new dataset, run the diff, apply to new jobs immediately, continue working through Priority 1 list, adjust strategy based on interview feedback. You have 10-11 more weeks to iterate.

The Question the Data Can’t Answer

This dataset tells you where venture-backed companies are hiring, what roles they need, and how the hiring landscape changes weekly. It doesn’t tell you whether you specifically will get hired. That depends on variables the data doesn’t capture: your interview performance, your code quality, your communication skills, your ability to demonstrate culture fit, your references, your portfolio, your GitHub contributions, your competitive situation against other candidates with similar backgrounds.

The data optimizes the search. It doesn’t guarantee the outcome. But optimize the search correctly and you shift probability in your favor. The student who applies to 100 random jobs has a low expected value per application. The student who applies to 100 filtered jobs where role match + company fit + sponsorship probability are all high has 5-10x higher expected value per application.

For engineering students with 80 days and programming skills, this dataset is not just good—it’s the difference between guessing and calculating. The question is whether you use it like a job board (scroll, click, apply) or like a database (query, filter, optimize, automate).

The scraper runs weekly. The data accumulates. The students who treat this as a data engineering problem rather than a browsing problem are the ones who find the signal in the noise before the 80 days run out.
