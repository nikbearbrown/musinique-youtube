Chapter 3: Mathematical Disorder

Core Claim: Galileo, Cavalieri, and Torricelli developed infinitesimal methods as “bottom-up mathematics” grounded in material intuition, directly contradicting Jesuit top-down rationalism.

Evidence Presented:

Galileo’s contributions:

  • Aristotle’s Wheel paradox (1638): continuous line = infinite indivisible points + infinite vacuums

  • Law of falling bodies: triangle area = infinite parallel lines representing instantaneous speeds

  • Material basis: continuum modeled on ropes (threads), wood (fibers), marble (atoms + vacuums)

Cavalieri’s method (1621–1635):

  • Planes = “cloths woven of parallel threads”; solids = “books of parallel pages”

  • Archimedean spiral proof: transforms curved indivisibles into parabola, shows area = ⅓ circle

  • Defensive posture: claimed method works regardless of whether continuum actually composed of indivisibles

  • Restriction: only compare “all the lines” parallel to single regula (to avoid paradoxes)

Torricelli’s radicalization (1638–1647):

  • Opera Geometrica (1644): 21 proofs of parabola area—first 11 classical (method of exhaustion), last 10 using indivisibles

  • Key innovation: indivisibles have different widths—short lines are wider in proportion to their shortness

  • Embraced paradoxes as investigative tools rather than obstacles

  • Calculated tangent slopes for “infinite parabolas” (y^m = kx^n)

Logical Method: Demonstrate progression from Galileo’s speculation → Cavalieri’s systematization → Torricelli’s full exploitation, showing increasing confidence in paradoxical foundations.

Methodological Soundness: Strong, but relies on readers accepting that effective results matter more than logical consistency.

Logical Gaps:

  • Cavalieri’s claim that his method is independent of continuum’s actual composition is never convincingly defended—Alexander acknowledges contemporaries didn’t believe him

  • Torricelli’s assertion that indivisibles have variable width seems to multiply paradoxes rather than resolve them

Chapter 7: Thomas Hobbes, Geometer

Hobbes’s geometrical reform:

Chapter 9: Mathematics for a New World

Core Claim: Wallis created “experimental mathematics” using infinitesimals and induction, consciously modeling Royal Society experimentalism to make mathematics politically acceptable.

Infinitesimals:

  • Proposition 1: “Exhibit the thing to a certain extent, observe ratios, so at length general proposition becomes known by induction”

  • Pragmatic justification: method works, produces correct results

PART TWO: COMPREHENSIVE LITERARY REVIEW ESSAY

Length: 2,200 words

Opening: The Logical Puzzle

Consider a question that appears purely technical: Can a continuous line be composed of indivisible points? In 1632, five Jesuit fathers met in Rome and declared this proposition “repugnant to Aristotle... improbable... forbidden in our society.” Twenty years later, an English political philosopher would denounce the same idea as threatening civilization itself. Within two decades after that, the question’s resolution would correlate with England’s emergence as an industrial power and Italy’s descent into backwardness lasting centuries.

Amir Alexander’s Infinitesimal makes a startling claim: this abstract mathematical dispute determined the trajectories of European modernity. The argument rests on a specific mechanism: hierarchical political orders require epistemological certainty (provided by Euclidean geometry), while pluralist orders tolerate ambiguity (enabled by infinitesimal mathematics). Where infinitesimals were suppressed (Italy), absolutism and stagnation followed. Where they prevailed (England), pluralism and progress emerged.

The correlation is undeniable—documented in meeting records, published tracts, career destructions, and institutional transformations. Whether correlation proves causation is the question that haunts every page of this ambitious work.

Subject Emerges: A 150-Year War Over Mathematical Atoms

Alexander’s scope is precisely defined: the years 1500–1700, the territories Italy and England, the combatants Jesuits vs. Galileans (Italy) and Hobbes vs. Wallis (England). The mathematical question is deceptively simple: If you divide a line indefinitely, what remains? Ancient paradoxes (Zeno, Pythagoras) suggested indivisibles lead to contradiction—infinite zeroes still sum to zero, yet infinite positive magnitudes sum to infinity. Archimedes (c. 287–212 BCE) provisionally used them, then reproved results classically. The method vanished for 1,800 years.

The 17th-century revival began with Galileo’s Discourses (1638): the continuum contains “infinite indivisible atoms separated by infinite vacuums”—modeled on ropes (threads), wood (fibers), marble (atoms). Bonaventura Cavalieri systematized this as the “method of indivisibles” (Geometria Indivisibilibus, 1635): planes = cloths of parallel threads, solids = books of parallel pages. His Archimedean spiral proof demonstrates power: transform curved indivisibles into straight ones, revealing the spiral encloses exactly one-third its circle. Evangelista Torricelli radicalized the approach (Opera Geometrica, 1644): indivisibles have different widths proportional to their context, enabling tangent calculations for generalized parabolas.

The method’s effectiveness was spectacular. Torricelli offered 21 proofs of a single theorem—the first 11 using the cumbersome classical “method of exhaustion” (requiring foreknowledge of results), the last 10 using indivisibles (direct, intuitive, revealing why results hold). Yet effectiveness came at a price: logical paradoxes multiplied rather than resolved.

The Jesuit response was systematic across three fronts. Legally: Revisers General issued 15+ condemnations (1606–1649), culminating in the Ordinatio (1651)—a permanent ban listing 4 prohibited theses on continuum composition among 65 total banned doctrines. Mathematically: Paul Guldin (1641) proved no number of zero-width lines creates a surface; Mario Bettini (1648) called indivisibles “hallucinations”; Andrea Tacquet (1651) declared “one must destroy the other or be destroyed.” Institutionally: Luca Valerio forced from Lincean Academy (1616), Gregory St. Vincent’s publication blocked 20 years, Cavalieri denied Parma chair (1626), Torricelli silent throughout 1630s in Rome.

The campaign’s brutality peaked with the suppression of the entire Jesuate order (1668)—Cavalieri’s and Angeli’s institutional home. From that moment, no Italian mathematician published on infinitesimals. Viviani, Ricci, Nardi, Mengoli—all talented, all silenced. Italy’s mathematical tradition died in a single generation.

The Deep Dive: Two Geometries, Two Political Orders

The technical dispute masks a profound philosophical divide. To understand why indivisibles threatened order, we must grasp what Euclidean geometry meant to 17th-century authoritarians.

Christopher Clavius (1538–1612) transformed mathematics from peripheral to central in Jesuit education by demonstrating geometry could compel assent. The Gregorian calendar reform (1582) proved this empirically: Protestant princes resisted—England delayed adoption until 1752, Russia until 1918—but ultimately could not maintain an erroneous calendar. Mathematics, unlike theology, forces itself upon its audience “whether they like it or not.” This made it the ideal weapon against Reformation chaos, where “every truth challenged and every certainty gone.”

Clavius’s Prolegomena (1574) makes the ideological function explicit: “The mathematical disciplines have first place among all others... they engender true knowledge in the minds of hearers and completely remove doubt.” Contrast with non-mathematical fields where “the intellect deals with multitude of opinions”—precisely the condition Jesuits sought to eliminate. Geometry offered a template: absolute truths, hierarchical structure (each theorem depends on previous), no room for dissent. The dream was to make all knowledge geometrical, ending religious and political strife through the same irresistible certainty that makes the Pythagorean theorem undeniable.

But Euclidean geometry has essential characteristics:

  1. Top-down construction: Begin with self-evident axioms, build complex results step-by-step

  2. Rational transparency: Every object constructed from first principles, fully known

  3. Logical purity: No contradictions, no ambiguities, no unsolved problems

  4. Universal necessity: Results true everywhere, always, for all rational beings

Indivisibles violated every single one:

  1. Bottom-up investigation: Begin with material intuition (cloth = threads), generalize upward

  2. Mysterious objects: Figures exist “out there” to be discovered, not constructed

  3. Embrace paradox: Infinite zeroes somehow sum to finite magnitudes; indivisibles have variable widths

  4. Provisional results: Effective but not logically proven; validated by consensus, not pure reason

Guldin’s critique captures this perfectly: “Who will judge truth of geometrical construction—the hand, the eye, or the intellect?” For Jesuits, only intellect (pure reason) was acceptable. But Cavalieri’s method explicitly relied on hand and eye—material intuition of how cloth is woven, books are compiled. Tacquet’s warning follows logically: if mathematics abandons rational construction for sensory intuition, it ceases to guarantee order. “One must destroy the other.”

Thomas Hobbes arrived at identical conclusions through different path. His Leviathan (1651) proposed that reason compels men to surrender individual will to a sovereign who embodies their collective will—”mortal God” whose decrees have force of geometrical theorems. The frontispiece visualizes this: giant king composed of tiny subjects, each powerless individually but omnipotent collectively. Opposition to sovereign = opposing one’s own will = logical impossibility, like denying Euclidean axiom.

For this to work, geometry must be complete—no unsolved problems, no mysteries. The existence of the three classical problems (squaring circle, trisecting angle, doubling cube) suggested geometry has “dark corners where light of reason does not shine.” If geometry isn’t fully knowable, how can commonwealth be? Hobbes had to solve them to secure his philosophical foundations.

His reform: redefine geometrical objects as material bodies (points with size, lines with width) moving through space, treating Cavalieri’s indivisibles as “minuscule bodies considered zero.” This made geometry constructive and material—supposedly combining rigor with reality.

It failed catastrophically. Chapter 20 of De Corpore (1655) contains three quadratures: (1) “false quadrature from false hypothesis,” (2) mere approximation, (3) “true quadrature”... followed by disclaimer to take it “problematically.” Wallis obtained unpublished sheets from the printer, reconstructed the sequence of errors and retractions, published devastating refutation months after Hobbes’s book appeared. For 23 years Hobbes continued proposing quadratures; for 23 years Wallis demolished them.

The impossibility was proven in 1882: π is transcendental (not root of any algebraic equation), so line of length π cannot be constructed by compass and straightedge. Hobbes pursued a logically impossible goal because his political philosophy required geometry to be perfectly knowable.

Return & Synthesis: The Experimental Alternative

Wallis’s mathematics offered the escape route—not from the mathematical problem (he also couldn’t square the circle) but from the political trap of requiring absolute certainty.

His Arithmetica Infinitorum (1656) opens by declaring alliance with Baconian experimentalism: “Exhibit thing to certain extent, observe ratios, general proposition becomes known by induction.” The method:

  1. Try simple case (series 0,1 → ratio 1/2)

  2. Try more complex cases (0,1,2,3 → ratio 1/2; 0,1,2,3,4 → ratio 1/2)

  3. Conclude: holds for all cases, “for there will be no reason to distinguish”

Modern mathematicians recoil—this isn’t proof, it’s pattern-matching. Fermat objected immediately: induction “allows proposing rule good for several particulars, nevertheless false in effect and not universal.” Wallis was unmoved: “Induction is very good method of investigation... need not any further demonstration.”

The audacity becomes clear when compared to Hobbes:

AspectHobbes (Euclidean)Wallis (Infinitesimal)ObjectsConstructed from first principles, fully knownExist in world, investigated like natural phenomenaMethodDeduction from axiomsInduction from particular casesCertaintyAbsolute, compels assentProvisional, validated by consensusValidationPure reasonAgreement among competent mathematiciansPolitical analogueTotalitarian: one truth, no dissentPluralist: competing views reach agreement

Wallis treated geometrical objects like Robert Boyle treated air—existing entities to be experimentally probed. His triangle proof: divide into infinite parallelograms (height = 1/∞), sum their areas using arithmetic series formula, cancel ∞/∞, arrive at ½ base × height. Logically dubious (∞/∞ is undefined), but produces correct result—validated not by deduction but by checking the answer.

This wasn’t accident or incompetence. Wallis designed mathematics to embody Royal Society values. Thomas Sprat’s History of the Royal Society (1667)—reviewed by Boyle and Oldenburg—explicitly contrasts experimentalism (breeds humility, cooperation, civic peace) with rationalist systems like Descartes and Hobbes (breed pride, dogmatism, sedition). Mathematics had been rationalism’s exemplar; Wallis made it experimental.

The effectiveness was undeniable. Isaac Newton testified the Arithmetica Infinitorum inspired his calculus (1665). Within a generation, infinitesimal methods transformed mathematics. Leibniz formalized them (1680s), 18th-century luminaries (Bernoulli, Euler, Lagrange) developed analysis, and by 1800 the approach dominated mathematical physics. Every subsequent technological advance—steam engines (thermodynamics), electrical systems (Maxwell equations), aviation (fluid dynamics)—depended on calculus.

Closing: Did Mathematics Determine Politics, or Vice Versa?

Alexander’s argument confronts an attribution problem he openly acknowledges: “It would be an exaggeration to attribute all these developments to the defeat of the infinitely small in Italy.” Italy’s decline had “many causes, political, economic, intellectual, and religious.” Yet he insists the struggle “played an important role” and was “a key site in which the path of Italian modernity was fought over and decided.”

Three pieces of evidence support causation beyond correlation:

1. The combatants’ own testimony. They explicitly linked mathematics to politics:

  • Sorbière (1664): dispute over “indivisible line of mathematicians” defined Wallis-Hobbes conflict

  • Clavius (Prolegomena, 1574): geometry “imposes truth and vanquishes error,” model for ending religious strife

  • Wallis (1659 letter): attacked Hobbes’s mathematics because “this Leviathan relied so much on mathematics... necessary some mathematician show how little he understands”

  • Sprat (History, 1667): experimentalism produces “obedience to civil government,” rationalism produces sedition

They believed mathematical method and political order were connected. This doesn’t prove they were right, but it proves the connection wasn’t Alexander’s anachronistic imposition.

2. Institutional synchronization. Mathematical and political campaigns moved in lockstep:

  • April 1615: Revisers ban indivisibles same month Bellarmine issues opinion against Copernicanism

  • 1632: Breitenfeld battle shifts Roman power → Jesuits return to dominance → Galileo’s trial and renewed infinitesimal ban

  • 1668: Unable to silence Angeli politically, Jesuits suppress his entire order

The timing suggests integrated strategy, not coincidence.

3. Demonstrated mechanism. Alexander shows how mathematics functioned politically:

  • In Jesuit system: Geometry taught students that truth is hierarchical, flows from authority, compels assent—then theology claimed same characteristics

  • In Royal Society: Experimental math taught members to tolerate ambiguity, reach consensus despite disagreement—model transferred to political disputes

  • In Hobbes’s philosophy: Geometrical certainty was not metaphor but foundation—political conclusions claimed to be “as certain as Pythagorean theorem”

Yet the reverse causation remains plausible. Perhaps political conditions enabled certain mathematics rather than mathematical methods determining politics:

  • Jesuit hierarchy needed Euclidean certainty → selected for mathematicians like Clavius who provided it

  • Royal Society pluralism needed flexible methods → Wallis invented experimental mathematics to fit

  • England’s parliamentary tradition created demand for Wallis’s approach; Italy’s autocracy created demand for Jesuit rigidity

The honest answer: bidirectional causation. Mathematics and politics co-evolved, each shaping the other. The infinitesimal debate was neither purely technical nor purely political but a site where intellectual and political commitments reinforced each other through institutional selection pressures.

What can be proven: In 17th-century Europe, mathematical method and political order were understood as inseparable by the combatants themselves. Whether one caused the other or both reflected deeper cultural configurations, the outcome was consequential. Italy suppressed infinitesimals and stagnated. England accepted them and flourished.

The Methodological Question: Can History Prove Causation?

Alexander’s evidence is formidable but faces historiographical limits:

Strengths:

  • Archives consulted: Jesuit Revisers’ records (Vatican), Wallis-Hobbes correspondence, Royal Society minutes

  • Temporal precision: dates of condemnations, publications, political events aligned to months

  • Mechanism identified: specific individuals (Clavius, Cavalieri, Guldin, Tacquet, Hobbes, Wallis) with documented motivations

  • Combatants’ testimony: they believed mathematics and politics connected

  • Outcome correlation: mathematical suppression in Italy coincides with broader stagnation; acceptance in England with pluralist flourishing

Weaknesses:

  • Sample size n=2: Only two cases (Italy, England), insufficient for causal inference

  • Confounding variables: Italy’s decline also correlates with papal temporal power, foreign invasions, loss of trade routes, Counter-Reformation generally

  • England’s advantages: island geography, coal deposits, strong navy, Protestant work ethic, earlier parliamentary tradition—any might explain divergent modernities

  • Counterfactual speculation: “If Galileans had won in Italy...” cannot be tested

  • Alternative explanation: Political conditions selected for compatible mathematics, not vice versa

The most honest assessment: Alexander proves the correlation, demonstrates combatants believed in connection, identifies a plausible mechanism (institutional reinforcement), but cannot rule out reverse causation or confounding variables. He admits this: “It would be an exaggeration to attribute all these developments to defeat of infinitely small.” Then argues it anyway, because the correlation’s magnitude—mathematical death coinciding with centuries of backwardness—strains coincidence.

Where the Book’s Logic Is Strongest: The Combatants’ Worldviews

Alexander’s most compelling sections trace how specific individuals connected mathematical and political commitments:

Christopher Clavius (1538–1612):
A “slow-witted beast” (critics’ term) who never made original contributions but reorganized Jesuit education around Euclidean geometry. His Prolegomena argues mathematics “reject not only false but even merely probable,” admitting only “most certain demonstrations.” Why? Because theological/philosophical disputes “deal with multitude of opinions,” leaving “mind confused.” Geometry ends debate. The Gregorian calendar proved it—Protestants resisted but ultimately submitted to mathematical necessity.

Clavius never wanted original mathematics: “The theorems of Euclid still today, as for many years past, retain their true purity, real certitude, strong demonstrations.” Mathematics worth studying because it never changes—offering “stability, order, unchanging eternal truths.” Innovation would be “worse than useless... dangerous, as it would compromise foundations of truth it was meant to buttress.”

Bonaventura Cavalieri (1598–1647):
The cautious revolutionary who invented the method but hedged his bets. To Galileo (1639): praises “immense ocean of indivisibles” then immediately retreats—”I did not dare affirm continuum is composed of indivisibles.” His defense against Guldin: method works regardless of continuum’s actual composition. This is “disingenuous” (Alexander’s term)—the cloth/book metaphors make his position clear—but strategically effective. Later mathematicians cited Cavalieri’s “learned volumes” when attacked, knowing critics hadn’t read the nearly indecipherable Latin.

His critical admission against Guldin: “Not necessary to actually construct figures, sufficient to assume them mentally, and nothing contradictory follows if we assume they’ve been already constructed.” Here is the divide: Jesuits demand rational construction from first principles; Cavalieri begins with material intuition of existing objects.

Evangelista Torricelli (1608–1647):
Unlike cautious Cavalieri, Torricelli “reveled in paradoxes.” His Opera Geometrica lists dozens as investigative tools—pushing logic to extremes reveals continuum’s true structure, like laboratory experiments push natural phenomena to extremes. The parallelogram paradox: equal triangles composed of unequal lines → solution: short lines are wider by exact proportion they’re shorter, with width given by slope ratio. Absurd? Yes. Effective? Undeniably—enabled tangent calculations for generalized parabolas (y^m = kx^n).

Where Cavalieri avoided the composition question, Torricelli embraced it: indivisibles have positive but variable magnitude. Where ancient mathematicians saw paradoxes as dead ends, Torricelli made them the method. This approach “constantly skirting edges of error” was “flexible and remarkably effective”—spread to France (Roberval, Fermat), England (Wallis, Barrow), ultimately became calculus.

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679):
The materialist who needed geometry to be perfectly knowable to ground his totalitarian commonwealth. His Leviathan (1651) argues rational men escaping “state of nature” (war of all against all) must surrender will to sovereign. This requires logical compulsion—”geometry is demonstrable, for lines and figures are drawn by ourselves; civil philosophy is demonstrable because we make commonwealth ourselves.”

But classical geometry contained unsolved problems (squaring circle, trisecting angle), suggesting unknowable mysteries—fatal for a philosophy claiming “skill of making and maintaining commonwealths consists in certain rules, as does arithmetic and geometry.” Hobbes had to solve them.

His reform: geometrical objects are material bodies in motion. Points have size (considered zero), lines have width (considered zero). Adopted Cavalieri’s indivisibles as “minuscule bodies which for calculation are considered zero.” His friend Sorbière: Hobbes “allows of some very little breadth of no account.” This supposedly made geometry more rigorous by grounding it in matter/motion—only things that actually exist.

The attempt failed because the problems are impossible—squaring circle requires constructing line of length π, proven transcendental (not constructible by compass/straightedge) in 1882. Hobbes didn’t know this, kept trying for 23 years, produced dozens of “quadratures” all refuted by Wallis. Died convinced he’d succeeded, listed it first among life’s accomplishments.

John Wallis (1616–1703):
The Presbyterian minister turned mathematician who reinvented the field to serve Royal Society ideology. His Truth Tried (1643): “speculative knowledge... found in devils”—even demons can follow logical arguments. Higher knowledge is “experimental... we taste and see it, not in power of will to reject.”

Appointed Savilian Professor (1649) for political reasons with minimal qualifications, he taught himself by reading Galileo, Torricelli, Descartes, Roberval. Within six years published De Sectionibus Conicis and Arithmetica Infinitorum (1655–56)—both radiating unconventionality.

Opening sentence, De Sectionibus: “I suppose, according to Cavalieri’s geometry of indivisibles, any plane made up of infinite parallel lines, or rather... infinite parallelograms of equal height, altitude of each = 1/∞.” Invented symbol ∞ for this purpose. Then proved triangle area = ½ base × height by summing arithmetic progression (total length = ∞/2 × base), multiplying by thickness (1/∞ × altitude), canceling ∞/∞.

Fermat objected: cannot sum lines unless area already known (circular); induction proposes rules “good for several particulars, nevertheless false in effect.” Wallis dismissed him as pedant: “Most mathematicians... after such induction are satisfied to conclude universally... hitherto thought conclusive argument.”

The Arithmetica Infinitorum systematizes induction: test ratio for two-term series (0,1), three-term (0,1,2), four-term (0,1,2,3)—all yield 1/2. Conclude: holds for infinite series “for there will be no reason to distinguish.” Same for squares (→ 1/3), cubes (→ 1/4), generalize: ∑(n^m)/n^m → 1/(m+1). Each “theorem” proven by single sentence: “Clear from what has gone before.”

Methodologically outrageous—treated ∞ as ordinary number, divided ∞/∞, assumed finite rules apply to infinite cases—yet worked. Inspired Newton’s calculus, transformed mathematics.

Why did Royal Society embrace this? Because Wallis’s method embodied their political values:

  • Objects exist independently (not constructed by authority)

  • Knowledge gathered experimentally (not deduced from axioms)

  • Results validated by consensus (not logical compulsion)

  • Tolerates ambiguity (∞/∞ undefined but useful)

Sprat’s History makes connection explicit: experimentalism “teaches humility, acquaints them with errors”—removes “haughtiness of mind.” Rationalist systems breed men who “suppose themselves infallible... most fruitful parent of sedition is pride.” Mathematics had been rationalism’s tool; Wallis made it experimental.

The Verdict: Correlation Proven, Causation Plausible

Alexander has proven beyond doubt:

  1. The combatants linked mathematics and politics. Sorbière, Clavius, Hobbes, Wallis, Sprat—all explicitly connected mathematical method to political order in their own writings.

  2. Institutional actors coordinated. Jesuit Revisers, Superior Generals, and mathematical faculty pursued integrated strategy across legal, intellectual, and institutional fronts.

  3. Career consequences were real. Valerio, St. Vincent, Cavalieri (blocked from Parma), Torricelli (silent in Rome), Viviani, Ricci, Nardi, Mengoli (all silenced)—pattern too consistent for coincidence.

  4. Outcomes diverged precisely as predicted. Italy (infinitesimals suppressed) → mathematical/political stagnation. England (infinitesimals accepted) → mathematical/political flourishing.

What remains unproven: the direction of causation. Did suppressing infinitesimals cause Italian stagnation, or did stagnant political culture select for anti-infinitesimal mathematics? Alexander’s most defensible claim: mutual reinforcement. Jesuit hierarchy needed Euclidean certainty; Euclidean mathematics legitimized hierarchy. Royal Society pluralism enabled experimental mathematics; experimental mathematics modeled pluralism. Over decades, this feedback loop amplified initial differences into divergent modernities.

The weakest link: attributing Italy’s broader decline to a mathematical dispute. Papal temporal power, Spanish/Austrian domination, loss of Mediterranean trade, Counter-Reformation generally—all contributed. The infinitesimal war might be symptom more than cause.

But on narrower question—did mathematical culture affect intellectual trajectory?—the case is overwhelming. Italy’s mathematical death was immediate and total. From Galileo/Cavalieri/Torricelli (European leaders, 1630s) to nobody (1670s). England’s rise was equally dramatic: no tradition (1630s) to Newton/calculus/Royal Society dominance (1680s). The mathematics-politics connection, even if not determinative of everything, clearly mattered for scientific development.

Final Assessment: A Brilliant Thesis With Honest Limitations

Infinitesimal succeeds magnificently as intellectual history—reconstructing combatants’ worldviews, showing they believed mathematical and political order were inseparable, documenting systematic suppression. It proves they thought infinitesimals threatened hierarchy, and that belief shaped their actions with measurable consequences.

It succeeds adequately as causal history—demonstrating plausible mechanism (institutional reinforcement), compelling temporal correlations, and outcomes matching predictions. But it cannot eliminate alternative explanations or prove infinitesimals caused rather than correlated with divergent modernities.

Alexander’s thesis: “The struggle over infinitely small was contest between opposite visions of what modernity would be.” Proven.

His stronger claim: “Victory of one side left its imprint on world for centuries to come.” Plausible but not proven—too many confounding variables, insufficient cases, no controlled comparison.

What remains after rigorous skepticism: In 17th-century Europe, abstract mathematical disputes were understood as having concrete political stakes. Whether that understanding was correct—whether banning a mathematical method truly can doom a civilization—remains an open question. But the combatants wagered their careers, their institutions, and their visions of the future on the answer.

They believed geometry and politics were inseparable. Their actions, victories, and defeats shaped the world we inherited. Whether they were right about the mechanism or merely caught in a correlation they mistook for causation, the pattern holds: where infinitesimals died, so did pluralism. Where they flourished, so did freedom.

The verdict is not absolute certainty—that, after all, was the Jesuits’ failed dream. But it is sufficient proof for the business at hand: showing that an “obscure mathematical concept” could indeed, through the beliefs and actions of powerful institutions, help shape modernity’s face.
