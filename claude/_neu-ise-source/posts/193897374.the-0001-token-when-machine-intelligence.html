In March 2026, NVIDIA announced hardware that cuts the cost of machine reasoning by 100x. That number doesn’t mean AI gets better at writing emails. It means we’re about to find out what the world looks like when intelligence is no longer a scarce resource when letting an AI think costs less than storing its answer.

The platform is called Rubin. The implications aren’t about faster chatbots. They’re about what happens when machines can reason continuously, for pennies, without human oversight.

The Economic Inversion

Here’s the shift: generating a token on the Rubin platform costs approximately $0.001, one-tenth of a cent. A novel-length response (100,000 tokens) costs $100. A full day of continuous agent reasoning (10 million tokens) costs $10,000.

Compare this to 2023’s GPT-4 infrastructure, where the same workload would have cost $1 million. Or the 2021 GPT-3 era, where it would have been economically impossible at any price. This isn’t incremental improvement. This is a 100x cost collapse in three years, achieved not by making models smarter but by making the infrastructure radically more efficient.

When reasoning becomes this cheap, you stop rationing it. You stop asking whether the AI needs to think about a problem you let it think, because the marginal cost is negligible. This is the transition from AI as a service you invoke occasionally to AI as a persistent presence, reasoning continuously in the background.

The question is no longer “can we afford to use AI?” It’s “what do we build when intelligence is abundant?”

Why the Previous Generation Couldn’t Do This

The AI systems you use today: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini operate on a reactive model: you ask, they answer, they stop. This architecture exists not because it’s optimal but because it’s all the hardware could afford.

The bottleneck was the “memory wall.” Every time a model generates a token, it must load a massive “key-value cache” into memory, a data structure storing everything the model has processed so far. For trillion-parameter models with million-token contexts, this cache can exceed a terabyte. Moving that much data millions of times per second creates a situation where expensive processors spend most of their time waiting for data rather than computing with it.

Why does asking GPT-4 a complex question sometimes take 10 seconds? Not because the model is thinking hard, it’s because the system is spending most of that time moving data. The bottleneck isn’t the math. It’s the plumbing.

The previous generation NVIDIA’s Blackwell platform could handle this for short bursts. It couldn’t handle it continuously, cheaply, or at the scale required for autonomous agents that run unattended, managing workflows, learning from mistakes, and iterating without asking permission at every step.

Rubin was designed to solve that constraint.

Six Breakthroughs That Made It Possible

1. HBM4: The 22 TB/s Memory Revolution

The Rubin R100 GPU integrates up to 12 stacks of HBM4 (High Bandwidth Memory), delivering 22 terabytes per second of bandwidth. The previous generation topped out at 8 TB/s. This isn’t incremental, it’s a tripling in 18 months.

What does 22 TB/s mean? A trillion-parameter model with a million-token context window can generate tokens in real time without the GPU ever stalling. The memory system delivers data faster than the compute units can process it. The bottleneck has shifted back to where it belongs: the math, not the plumbing.

2. The Groq Acquisition: Heterogeneous Compute

In late 2025, NVIDIA acquired Groq’s Language Processing Unit (LPU) technology in a $20 billion deal. This was NVIDIA’s admission that GPUs, for all their power, are the wrong tool for certain jobs.

GPUs excel at massive parallelism. But token generation is serial you can’t compute token 47 until you’ve computed token 46. Groq’s LPU solved this by using SRAM (static RAM) instead of the slower DRAM that GPUs rely on. SRAM is expensive and space-constrained, but for inference, where you’re just reading pre-trained weights it’s 10x faster.

The Groq 3 LPU delivers 150 TB/s of bandwidth from 500 MB of SRAM, generating tokens at 200–400 per second. This is physically impossible for any HBM-based GPU because DRAM latency is a hard limit set by physics.

The result: Rubin GPUs handle “prefill” (reading your prompt), Groq LPUs handle “decode” (generating the answer). For trillion-parameter models, this division of labor reduces latency by 10x and cost by a similar margin.

3. The Vera CPU: Built for Autonomous Agents

The Vera CPU is NVIDIA’s first fully custom ARM processor, designed not for general computing but for managing thousands of concurrent AI agents. Its Olympus core is a 10-wide superscalar processor with a neural branch predictor, critical for agent workloads where code execution involves constant branching decisions.

The real innovation is Spatial Multithreading: physically partitioning core resources rather than time-slicing them. This eliminates unpredictable latency and ensures every agent sandbox runs at guaranteed performance.

A single Vera CPU rack can sustain 22,500 concurrent agent sandboxes, the infrastructure enabling “AI employees” that run 24/7 without human oversight.

4. NVLink 6: The Rack as Computer

NVLink 6 unifies 72 GPUs into a single shared-memory domain with 260 TB/s of aggregate bandwidth. From software’s perspective, 72 GPUs look like one very large GPU with 20 terabytes of combined memory.

This is what enables trillion-parameter models to run at all. Models too large for a single GPU are automatically sharded across multiple GPUs, and because NVLink’s latency is measured in nanoseconds, the performance penalty is negligible.

5. SCADA Storage: 100 Million IOPS

Traditional storage requires the GPU to ask the CPU to fetch data, a roundtrip that takes milliseconds. NVIDIA’s SCADA framework lets GPUs directly initiate storage requests, achieving 100 million IOPS, a 100x improvement achieved not by faster flash but by removing the CPU bottleneck.

This is essential for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), where models query databases before answering. If storage latency is too high, queries time out. SCADA ensures they don’t.

6. The 600 kW Rack: Power as Constraint

Rubin racks consume 600 kilowatts, 20x more than typical 2024 servers. This density is only possible through single-phase direct liquid cooling (eliminating chillers) and 800 VDC power delivery (reducing conversion losses by 50%).

The result: a data center approaching PUE of 1.0, where essentially zero energy is wasted on anything other than compute. When 30% of your power goes to cooling, you’re paying for waste. When 2% does, you’re paying for intelligence.

What We’re Actually Building

NVIDIA is no longer selling chips. It’s selling factories, AI Factories where data and electricity go in and machine intelligence comes out at industrial scale. The company projects $1 trillion in AI infrastructure revenue between 2025 and 2027, double what they forecast a year earlier.

This upward revision reflects not speculation but deployment: hyperscaler data centers, sovereign AI labs, and corporate campuses across six continents are already assembling this infrastructure. The decisions about access, accountability, and economic distribution are being made this year, while most people still think this is about chatbots.

The software layer, NemoClaw provides the security model that makes autonomous agents deployable in regulated industries. It sandboxes every agent execution, logging every action and enforcing policies at the OS level. This is the difference between a research prototype and an enterprise system you can deploy in healthcare, finance, or defense without triggering an audit.

The Question That Remains

There’s a particular kind of arrogance in naming a computing platform after Vera Rubin, the astronomer who proved that most of the universe is invisible, that 85% of gravitational mass is dark matter we can’t detect except by its effects.

The Rubin platform operates on the same premise: the intelligence we interact with, the chatbots, the assistants is only 15% of what’s happening. The other 85% is infrastructure: memory bandwidth, networking fabric, cooling systems, power delivery, sandboxing runtimes. We don’t see it. We shouldn’t have to. But it’s what makes the visible part possible.

The infrastructure is already being built. The question is not whether this matters but what we choose to run on it, whether systems capable of autonomous reasoning at near-zero marginal cost become public goods or proprietary moats, whether they amplify human agency or replace it, and whether we’re early enough to shape what comes next.
