Most students, junior developers, bootcamp grads, and career switchers obsess over their portfolio website. Hiring managers often don’t. What they’re actually looking for — across GitHub, LinkedIn, and the work itself — is evidence that you’re not a risk. Here’s what that evidence looks like, and how to show it.

I learned this the hard way. When I first started applying for tech roles as a Northeastern ISE student, I had a handful of projects and no formal technical background. Co-op application season has a way of making that feel urgent. Every article I found said the same thing: build a portfolio website. And the standout examples all had a “thing” — dark and light mode toggles, glossy animations, some clever gimmick to make it memorable. I came to believe that a great portfolio meant a highly polished personal site.

The actual goal: reduce uncertainty

For most companies, a portfolio is rarely a strict requirement. What it can be, though, is a risk-reduction signal. With fewer junior positions available today — and co-op and internship slots at a premium — hiring someone early-career is a higher-stakes decision. Concrete evidence of ability is worth far more than personal branding.

A good portfolio should help clearly answer four questions: Can this person actually build things? Can they work with others? Can they communicate technical ideas clearly? Are they likely to grow into the role?

If your portfolio answers those four questions clearly, it’s doing its job — regardless of how it looks.

What is a tech portfolio, really?

A tech portfolio is a place to show who you are and what you can build. The key word is show. Unlike a resume, a portfolio doesn’t tell hiring teams you have skills — it demonstrates them directly. That contrast matters enormously for candidates with little formal experience.

But “portfolio” doesn’t mean one website. Based on conversations with engineers and hiring managers across the industry, what actually gets evaluated is a broader set of materials:

  • Your GitHub activity and code quality

  • The types of projects you’ve worked on

  • How you document and explain your work

  • Your professional presence

A personal website is one way to present these — but it’s rarely the whole picture.

Where should you build your portfolio?

The honest answer: all three of the following. Each reaches a different part of the hiring ecosystem.

GitHub is where engineering managers go first. It’s the closest thing to a technical audition you’ll get before the interview.

LinkedIn is how non-technical recruiters and talent partners find you — often before you’re even actively applying.

A personal website carries the most weight at startups and frontend-heavy roles. Everywhere else, it’s a nice-to-have.

A solid GitHub and LinkedIn alone can get you far. Add a personal website and you cover most bases, including the startup segment that weighs it most heavily.

One note on personal sites: a startup founder I spoke with gave me the most memorable advice of my entire job search. Many juniors fall into a trap of trying to be too unique — to a point where the information hiring managers actually want becomes buried under CSS experimentation. If a visitor can’t find your work easily, the creativity has the opposite effect intended. Save the flashiness for after your first job. At this stage, professionalism communicates more than experimental styling.

What should your portfolio contain?

At minimum, every platform should have:

  • A clear headshot — puts a face to the name and signals professionalism

  • A short personal bio — context for who you are beyond the work

  • Your main tech stack — lets the right people find and evaluate you faster

  • Examples of your work — the core signal; everything else supports this

  • Contact information — make it effortless to reach you

For junior engineers, that’s a perfectly complete starting point. More content doesn’t automatically mean more value — and all of it should be easy to find immediately.

What about projects?

Three or fewer is a solid baseline. One genuinely strong project beats five tutorial reproductions. Quality matters more than volume — but presentation matters too.

Whatever you feature, each project should have: a live or publicly accessible version, a public code repository, a well-structured README, and supplementary visuals like screenshots or a short demo video.

Types of projects worth highlighting

Collaborative projects — anything with teammates demonstrates Git workflows, code review, and team communication. These skills are often more important to hiring managers than the technical complexity of the work itself.

Real-world problem solving — doesn’t need to be revolutionary. A simple but practical solution with a clear use case makes for a compelling interview story later.

Passion projects — work tied to genuine personal interests tends to stand out and is easier to talk about naturally in interviews.

Games (optional) — not required, but some engineering managers appreciate them specifically for demonstrating fundamentals, logic, and raw coding ability.

Even better than personal projects: real work

If you lack professional experience, actual production work — even unpaid — carries significantly more weight than personal projects. For Northeastern students, this is also where the co-op program has a real edge: a six-month co-op placement is exactly this kind of experience. If you’ve completed one, feature it prominently. If you haven’t yet, the options below can bridge the gap.

Volunteering with a real development team (NGOs, nonprofits, open-source projects, student orgs) provides live system experience, team collaboration, and mentorship from senior engineers. That last one is invaluable at this stage. Northeastern has no shortage of clubs and orgs worth exploring — and even organizations that don’t explicitly advertise technical openings are worth reaching out to directly.

Freelance work can add meaningful signals around client communication, project ownership, and solo production experience. Quality and conditions vary — understanding what you’re willing to take on matters — but even short engagements can give you genuine professional experience to talk about. For bootcamp grads and career switchers especially, this can be one of the fastest ways to build a credible track record.

The actual goal

Hiring teams evaluate portfolios holistically. A personal website is nice. But obsessing over aesthetics misses the point.

A tech portfolio isn’t about looking impressive. It’s about reducing uncertainty — giving a hiring team enough concrete evidence to feel confident taking a chance on someone early in their career.

For junior engineers, sometimes that’s all it takes.
