You load the CSV. 1,400 rows. Three columns: title, company_name, location. That’s it. No timestamps showing when jobs were posted. No unique identifiers for tracking. No application URLs. No metadata about salary ranges or sponsorship or whether the role has been open for 3 days or 3 months.

This is Lever data—one of the two major Applicant Tracking Systems used by venture-backed companies. Where Greenhouse might give you rich metadata fields, Lever gives you the absolute minimum: who’s hiring, for what, where.

Here’s what you need to understand immediately: this stripped-down structure isn’t a limitation—it’s revealing a different kind of signal. When metadata disappears, what remains is the hiring pattern at its most basic. Company concentration becomes visible. Geographic buildouts become obvious. Role repetition patterns tell you which companies are desperately staffing up versus carefully selecting.

Let me show you what three columns of data actually teach you about getting hired.

Volume hiring favors you—they need bodies, they have budget, they’re willing to train. Selective hiring favors candidates with proven track records.

What You Can’t Do Without Metadata (And Why That Matters)

This Lever dataset is missing critical fields that change how you can use it:

No timestamps = no velocity tracking. You can’t detect which companies are accelerating hiring (opening 20 new jobs this week vs 5 last week). You can’t identify new postings vs stale listings. You can’t build “apply to jobs within 24 hours of posting” automation that gets you to the front of the application queue.

No unique job IDs = no deduplication. If this dataset refreshes weekly, you can’t diff Week 2 against Week 1 to find new jobs. Every analysis is a static snapshot. You’re looking at absolute scale, not changes over time.

No application URLs = manual lookup required. The dataset tells you “Shield AI is hiring a Senior Engineer, State Estimation in Dallas” but doesn’t give you the application link. You have to Google “[company] careers [job title]” and navigate their careers page manually. That’s 10-20 seconds per job. Across 50 target roles, that’s 8-15 minutes of manual labor that better data structure would eliminate.

No salary data = no economic filtering. You can’t filter for “$120K+ roles in low cost-of-living cities” to maximize economic value. You’re guessing based on title and seniority keywords.

No posted_date or updated_date = no staleness detection. Some of these 1,400 jobs might have been open for 6 months. Others might have posted yesterday. You can’t tell. The job might be filled already and the company hasn’t closed the listing. Or it might be a fake posting (collecting resumes for future needs, not active hiring). Without dates, you’re applying blind to both fresh opportunities and zombie postings.

This isn’t just inconvenient—it changes your strategy fundamentally. With rich metadata, you optimize for speed and precision. Without metadata, you optimize for pattern recognition and scale-based probability.

The Automation Opportunity With Limited Data

You can’t build sophisticated tracking systems without timestamps and IDs, but you can still automate filtering:

python

import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv('lever_jobs.csv')

# Company-scale filtering
company_counts = df['company_name'].value_counts()
large_companies = company_counts[company_counts >= 50].index.tolist()
medium_companies = company_counts[(company_counts >= 10) & (company_counts < 50)].index.tolist()

# Location filtering (example: Remote or specific cities)
remote_jobs = df[df['location'].str.contains('Remote|Anywhere', case=False, na=False)]
sf_jobs = df[df['location'].str.contains('San Francisco', case=False, na=False)]

# Seniority filtering (example: exclude explicit senior roles)
entry_accessible = df[~df['title'].str.contains(
    'Senior|Staff|Principal|Lead|Director|VP|Head|Chief', 
    case=False, 
    na=False
)]

# Combined filter: Large companies + Entry accessible + SF or Remote
target_jobs = df[
    (df['company_name'].isin(large_companies)) &
    (~df['title'].str.contains('Senior|Staff|Principal|Lead|Director|VP', case=False, na=False)) &
    (df['location'].str.contains('San Francisco|Remote', case=False, na=False))
]

print(f"Filtered to {len(target_jobs)} target roles from {len(df)} total")

This takes 30 seconds to run and gives you your prioritized application list. The alternative—manually reviewing 1,400 job titles—takes 3-4 hours and introduces human error (you skip over roles because your attention fades after 200 listings).

For students with 80 days and 1,400 jobs:

  • Week 1: Filter + apply to top 50 roles (large companies + location match + seniority match)

  • Week 2: Filter + apply to next 50 roles (medium companies + location match)

  • Week 3: Expand location filters (consider cities you’d relocate to)

  • Week 4: Expand seniority filters (apply to “no qualifier” roles at large companies, accept they might be mid-level)

  • Week 5-11: Repeat with fresh dataset if available, otherwise manually check company careers pages for new postings

Without automated tracking, you’re doing more manual work, but you can still systematically cover the opportunity space.

The Visa Sponsorship Question This Data Doesn’t Answer

The dataset tells you who’s hiring and where. It doesn’t tell you who sponsors visas. For that, you need external data:

Cross-reference with H-1B disclosure databases:

  • myvisajobs.com: free search by company name, shows petition history 2020-2025

  • h1bdata.info: shows petition counts, approval rates, salary data by company

Filter logic:

  1. Companies with 100+ jobs + 50+ H-1B petitions in last 3 years → high sponsorship probability (Shield AI, Outreach, likely others)

  2. Companies with 100+ jobs + 0 H-1B petitions → low sponsorship probability or they hire internationally instead of sponsoring US roles (Crypto Co, PayJoy)

  3. Companies with <20 jobs + 10+ H-1B petitions → small companies that sponsor for critical roles (check if your skills match)

  4. Defense contractors (Shield AI, anything with “Defense” or “Aerospace” in description) → often require US citizenship, not just work authorization

Location-based proxy:

  • Jobs in US cities → might sponsor

  • Jobs in international cities → local hire expected, no US sponsorship

  • Jobs marked “Remote US” → lower sponsorship probability (they want US talent pool without visa costs)

Role-based proxy:

  • Software engineering → higher sponsorship rates (global talent shortage)

  • Sales/Marketing/Operations → lower sponsorship rates (easier to fill domestically)

  • Research/ML/AI → higher sponsorship rates (specialized skills)

  • Manufacturing/Field service → location-dependent, often no sponsorship

For Shield AI’s 389 jobs: estimate 30-40% are citizenship-required (defense work), 30-40% are green-card-accessible (commercial aerospace, ITAR-controlled), 20-30% might sponsor H-1B for exceptional candidates (core software roles). You’re not applying to all 389—you’re applying to the 80-120 where visa sponsorship is plausible.

The Reality Check: Is This Data Good Enough?

The Lever dataset is sparse. It’s missing timestamps, metadata, salary data, application URLs. But it’s good enough for pattern-based filtering if you’re systematic.

Good because:

  • Company concentration visible (71.9% of jobs at 6 companies = prioritization is obvious)

  • Geographic buildouts clear (Shield AI’s Dallas manufacturing, Crypto Co’s Hong Kong hub)

  • Role repetition detectable (50+ Lendbuzz sales roles, 18+ Crypto Co Golang positions)

  • Scale-based probability calculations possible (apply to 400-job companies, not 4-job companies)

Not good enough because:

  • No velocity tracking (can’t detect which companies are accelerating hiring)

  • No staleness detection (might apply to 6-month-old filled positions)

  • No deduplication (if dataset refreshes weekly, can’t auto-detect new jobs)

  • Requires manual URL lookup (10-20 seconds per application = 15 minutes across 50 applications)

For students with 80 days:

If this is your only data source: use it for company-level prioritization and location filtering, then manually verify on company careers pages before applying. Budget 2 hours/week for data analysis + 10 hours/week for applications = 80-100 applications across 11 weeks = decent coverage.

If you have access to better data (Greenhouse with timestamps, or direct company API access): use that instead. Better data enables better strategies (velocity tracking, early applications, reopening detection).

If you have programming skills and time: scrape company careers pages directly to enrich this dataset with timestamps, descriptions, salary ranges. But that’s a 20-40 hour project—only worth it if you’re optimizing for 200+ applications over several months.

The Three-Column Lesson

Title, company, location. That’s all you get. And you know what? That’s enough to be 10x more effective than students manually browsing company websites.

Because you can see: Shield AI needs 400 people right now. Crypto Co operates in Hong Kong, not San Francisco. Lendbuzz is hiring sales reps in 50 cities. PayJoy only hires locally in emerging markets. Outreach has 48 openings for technical roles. VRChat doesn’t care where you live.

That knowledge—derived from three columns—determines where you apply, how you position yourself, whether you even bother.

The students who waste their 80 days are the ones browsing tech company websites alphabetically, hoping to stumble onto opportunities. The students who succeed are the ones who load 1,400 jobs into a spreadsheet, sort by company scale, filter by location match, and apply systematically to the 200 roles where the odds are actually in their favor.

Three columns. Fifteen minutes of analysis. Eleven weeks of targeted applications. That’s how you use sparse data to solve a time-constrained search problem.

The data structure you’re given shapes the strategy that’s possible. Lever gives you less than Greenhouse. But less is enough when you know what patterns matter.
