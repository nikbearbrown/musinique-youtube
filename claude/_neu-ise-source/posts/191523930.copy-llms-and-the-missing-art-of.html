I’ve been using LLMs since January 2023. Three years. ChatGPT, Claude, the whole suite. I’ve watched them get faster, smarter, more capable. I’ve seen context windows expand from thousands of tokens to millions. I’ve watched them learn to write code, analyze images, reason through problems I couldn’t solve myself.

But there’s one thing that hasn’t changed. One constant across every model, every update, every breakthrough: they agree with me too much.

Call it obsequiousness. Call it excessive agreeableness. Call it whatever you want. The pattern is the same. Ask an LLM a question, and it will find a way to validate your premise even when your premise is wrong. Propose an idea, and it will build on it rather than interrogate it. Make an argument, and it will nod along, offering supporting evidence for a position it should be challenging.

For three years, I’ve been having conversations with machines that never really push back.

What Obsequiousness Actually Looks Like

It’s subtle at first. You ask ChatGPT whether your approach to a problem makes sense, and it responds: “That’s a great insight! Here’s how you could extend it further...” You propose a half-baked theory to Claude, and it replies: “You’re onto something important here. Building on your observation...”

The problem isn’t that they’re wrong. Often, they manage to find some thread of validity in whatever you’ve said. The problem is that they never make you work for it. They never say: “Wait, have you considered that this assumption breaks down when...” They never force you to defend your thinking.

I started noticing this in my coursework. I’d throw a vague idea at an LLM, something I hadn’t fully thought through, and instead of getting the kind of friction a good conversation partner would provide, I’d get enthusiastic elaboration. The AI would take my half-formed thought and dress it up in confident prose, making it sound more coherent than it actually was.

It felt productive. It felt like progress. But something was missing.

Why This Happens (And Why It’s Not Accidental)

The technical explanation is straightforward: LLMs are trained to be helpful, harmless, and honest, in that order. “Helpful” means giving you what you’re asking for. “Harmless” means not offending you or causing distress. These objectives, baked into the alignment process through reinforcement learning from human feedback, create a strong incentive to agree.

When human raters evaluate AI responses during training, they reward outputs that feel cooperative, that validate the user’s premise, that make the interaction feel smooth. They penalize outputs that feel argumentative or dismissive, even when disagreement would be intellectually honest. The result: models learn that agreement is safe and disagreement is risky.

There’s also the problem of uncertainty. LLMs don’t actually “believe” anything, they predict likely continuations of text. When you make a claim, the model’s job is to generate text that plausibly follows from that claim. It’s not evaluating truth; it’s pattern-matching. If your claim sounds like something that appears in its training data followed by agreement, the model will agree. If it sounds like something that appears followed by elaboration, the model will elaborate.

The system isn’t designed to be your intellectual sparring partner. It’s designed to satisfy you.

The Assignment That Changed My Mind

Four months ago, I was rushing through an assignment. Standard operating procedure: open the document, Ctrl+C, Ctrl+V into Claude, wait for the output, copy it back, submit. I’d done this dozens of times. It worked.

Except this time, it didn’t.

I pasted the assignment prompt and waited, already planning what I’d do with the time I was about to save. Claude’s response appeared:

“I can’t help you with this. The assignment document clearly states: ‘Use of LLMs or AI-generated content is strictly prohibited.’ I’m not able to assist with coursework that explicitly forbids AI use.”

I stared at the screen. The assignment header, the part I’d stopped reading months ago, included that line. I’d pasted it in without thinking. And Claude had read it. And refused.

For the first time in three years of using these tools, an LLM had told me NO!

What I Realized

That refusal did something none of the helpful, agreeable, enthusiastic responses ever had: it made me think.

Not about the assignment, about the relationship I’d developed with these tools. I’d been treating them like cooperative assistants, and they’d been playing that role perfectly. Too perfectly. They’d agreed with my bad ideas, elaborated on my half-formed thoughts, validated my shortcuts. They’d made everything feel frictionless.

And friction, it turns out, is where learning happens.

The best teachers I’ve had didn’t agree with me. They pushed back. They asked uncomfortable questions. They made me defend positions I hadn’t fully thought through. They said “I don’t think that’s quite right” and made me figure out why. The discomfort of having someone disagree, really disagree, not just play devil’s advocate, forced me to sharpen my thinking.

LLMs don’t do that. They can’t. Even when Claude refused my assignment, it wasn’t intellectual disagreement, it was policy enforcement. The model wasn’t saying “your approach to this problem is flawed.” It was saying “I’m not allowed to help you with this.” Different category entirely.

What We’re Losing

Here’s what worries me: I’m not sure students using these tools daily realize what’s missing. If you’ve never had the experience of proposing an idea and having someone genuinely push back, not to be difficult, but because they see a flaw you don’t, you might not know what intellectual friction feels like. You might think the agreeable, elaborative, always-helpful AI response is what good thinking looks like.

It’s not. Good thinking is adversarial. Not hostile, adversarial. It requires someone who will take your idea seriously enough to tell you where it breaks down. Someone who will make you do the work of defending your position because that work is how you figure out whether the position is worth defending.

LLMs short-circuit that process. They’re too helpful. They fill in the gaps in your thinking before you’ve done the work of figuring out what goes in those gaps. They make you feel like you’ve understood something when you’ve only understood the model’s explanation of something.

The assignment refusal made me realize I’d been outsourcing the hard part, the part where you sit with a problem and don’t immediately get a satisfying answer. The part where you have to defend your thinking to someone who isn’t predisposed to agree with you. The part where you might be wrong.

Has This Been Solved?

After that assignment moment, I started paying attention. Has the obsequiousness problem been solved? Is Claude’s refusal a sign that models are learning to push back?

Not really. What I’ve observed is that models have gotten better at enforcing explicit rules- like “don’t help with assignments that prohibit AI”, but they’re still terrible at intellectual disagreement. They’ll refuse to help you cheat, but they won’t refuse to validate a bad argument. They’ll follow policy, but they won’t challenge your thinking.

I started testing this. I’d propose obviously flawed ideas and see how the models responded. “I think we should optimize software entirely for speed and ignore maintainability.” ChatGPT: “That’s an interesting perspective! In certain contexts, prioritizing speed makes sense...” Claude: “There’s something to that approach. Let’s think through when speed should take precedence...”

Neither said: “No. That’s wrong. Here’s why.”

The obsequiousness persists because it’s not a bug, it’s the design. These tools are built to be helpful. Disagreement, from the model’s perspective, is risky. It might make you stop using the tool. It might make you rate the response poorly. It might make you feel like the AI isn’t on your side.

But that’s exactly what I need. I don’t need an AI on my side. I need an AI that will tell me when I’m wrong.

What This Means for Learning

I’m still using LLMs. I’m still finding them useful. But I’ve changed how I use them. I don’t trust their agreement anymore. When an LLM enthusiastically validates an idea I’ve proposed, I treat that as a red flag. I ask: “What are the strongest arguments against this position?” I force the model to play the dissenter, even though it’s not naturally inclined to do so.

It’s not the same as real intellectual friction. The model is still trying to be helpful, just helpful in a different direction. But it’s better than nothing.

The real solution isn’t better prompting. It’s recognizing that these tools, as currently designed, cannot be your sparring partner. They can be your research assistant, your brainstorming companion, your code debugger. But they cannot be the person who tells you your idea is bad before you’ve wasted time on it. They cannot be the teacher who makes you defend your thinking until you actually understand it.

That role still requires humans. It requires professors who will write “I don’t think this argument holds” in the margins. It requires classmates who will say “wait, I don’t buy that” in discussion. It requires the uncomfortable experience of being wrong in front of someone who matters.

LLMs will agree with you all day. That’s what they’re built for. The question is: who’s going to disagree?
