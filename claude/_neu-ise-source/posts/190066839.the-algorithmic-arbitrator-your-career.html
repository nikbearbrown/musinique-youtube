There is a number that follows you. Not the number on your credit report — that one, at least, you can check. This number lives inside the servers of talent intelligence platforms that power hiring at some of the largest employers in the world. It was derived from your career history, your LinkedIn profile, your application behavior, and possibly data scraped from your social media without your knowledge. It updates when you are rejected. It may compound those rejections into a pattern the system interprets as evidence of low hireability. And you have no legal right to see it.

We are not yet accustomed to thinking of our careers as credit scores. We should be. The architecture is closer than most people realize — and the legal battles now unfolding around platforms like Eightfold AI are forcing that comparison into courtrooms where the consequences will be real.

The Machinery of Matching

Eightfold AI calls itself a “system of intelligence,” not a hiring tool. The distinction is load-bearing. A hiring tool assists a human decision-maker. A system of intelligence is the decision-maker, rendering judgment before any human enters the process. The platform ingests data from existing HR systems — Workday, Oracle, SAP — and layers it over a proprietary Global Talent Network containing over 1.6 billion profiles and 1.5 billion career trajectories. From this data, it produces Match Scores ranging from 0 to 5, in increments of 0.5, that rank candidates for every role they apply to.

The technical mechanism is genuinely sophisticated. Traditional applicant tracking systems worked through keyword matching — if your resume did not contain the phrase “project management,” you did not surface for a project management role, regardless of how much managing you had done. Eightfold uses deep semantic embeddings: both candidate profiles and job descriptions are mapped into a high-dimensional vector space, and the distance between those vectors determines fit. A data engineer applying for an ML Ops role is recognized as a plausible candidate even without that specific title in their history. The model understands contextual equivalence in ways that keyword matching cannot.

Layered on top of the semantic matching is trajectory modeling. Recurrent neural networks analyze hundreds of millions of career sequences — the ordering of titles, the duration of tenures, the types of companies in sequence — to predict what a candidate’s likely next title will be. If that predicted next title aligns with the role being filled, the Match Score rises. Eightfold markets this capability as the identification of “potential”: the ability to surface candidates who are ready for the next step before their resume explicitly reflects it. Internal studies show that candidates scoring above 4.0 are promoted 50% more frequently and retained at rates 5 percentage points higher at 12 months than lower-scoring candidates. In retail, the retention gap reaches 18% within four months.

These numbers justify the system to employers. They say nothing about whether the system is justified to candidates.

Three Numbers and What They Have in Common

I want to take seriously a comparison that sounds, at first, like overreach: the Eightfold Match Score, the Spotify Popularity Index, and the consumer credit score are not the same thing. They operate in different domains, serve different functions, and carry different legal weight. But they share an architecture worth examining, because that architecture is what the Kistler lawsuit is actually about.

All three reduce complex human behavior into a single numerical output that determines access or visibility. All three weight recency heavily — Spotify’s index prioritizes streams from the last 28 days over historical performance; Eightfold’s model weights skills used in recent roles over skills from early career; credit scores treat recent payment behavior as more predictive than old debt. All three treat behavioral signals as reputation signals: a song frequently skipped loses algorithmic placement; a candidate frequently rejected accumulates a record that may lower their visibility for future roles; a borrower who applies for too many credit lines in a short period sees their score drop from the inquiry volume alone.

That last parallel — the penalty for high application frequency — is where the Eightfold comparison becomes most consequential and least discussed. Spotify’s algorithm rewards high volume: more streams means a higher popularity score. Credit systems penalize high volume of certain activities: multiple hard inquiries signal financial stress. Eightfold, by the account of candidates and the allegations in the Kistler lawsuit, may operate more like a credit system than a streaming platform. When a candidate applies to dozens of roles across companies using Eightfold, the system may interpret the pattern as “indiscriminate applying” — a signal of poor fit or what some analysts have called a “desperation signal.” And because Eightfold’s platform retains rejection data and uses it to evaluate candidates for future roles, those rejections compound. The candidate who has been rejected a hundred times across the network may be carrying a shadow record that filters them out before a human ever considers their qualifications.

Professional forums document this experience in volume. Candidates with strong credentials report rejections arriving within minutes of submission — a timeline that no human recruiter could produce, which means the decision was made by the algorithm alone. The speed of the rejection is not evidence of discrimination in the legal sense. It is evidence of a gate that closed before any person opened it.

The Audit That Passed

To address fairness concerns, Eightfold submits to annual bias audits under New York City’s Local Law 144, which requires employers using automated employment decision tools to publish audit results and notify candidates of the tool’s use. The March 2025 audit returned a passing rating. All demographic groups scored above the Four-Fifths Rule threshold, the standard that asks whether any group clears less than 80% of the highest-scoring reference group.

A passing audit and a fair system are not the same thing.

The audit excluded over 60 million applications — roughly one in four reviewed — where race or gender was unknown. The findings for the groups that were measured: Hispanic or Latino candidates scored at 0.916 relative to White candidates. Female candidates scored at 0.960 relative to male candidates. Both figures clear the legal floor. Neither figure is evidence that the system treats all candidates equally. The audit measures aggregate scoring rates across demographic groups. It does not measure whether an individual candidate’s profile contains inaccurate data scraped from the web. It does not measure whether the cross-company rejection feedback loop is suppressing specific candidates. It does not measure whether the “company similarity” variable — which scores candidates more favorably when their employer history clusters near the target company in vector space — is encoding prestige bias into the Match Score by design.

The audit exists because the law required it. What the law required is not sufficient.

What the Lawsuits Are Actually Fighting About

Two cases are reshaping the legal landscape for algorithmic hiring. They are related but distinct, and the distinction matters.

Mobley v. Workday, filed in 2023 and granted conditional collective certification for workers over 40 in May 2025, argues that Workday’s AI screening tools discriminate against protected classes — older workers, people with disabilities, candidates of specific races. The theory is one of disparate impact: the algorithm produces discriminatory outcomes, and Workday, as the vendor performing the screening function, is liable as an agent of the employer. This is an anti-discrimination argument. It asks whether the algorithm produces unfair outcomes.

Kistler v. Eightfold AI, filed in January 2026, asks a different question. It argues that Eightfold functions as a Consumer Reporting Agency under the Fair Credit Reporting Act — that the dossiers it compiles and the scores it produces are legally equivalent to consumer credit reports, triggering the full apparatus of FCRA disclosure rights. The plaintiffs allege that Eightfold scrapes personal data from social media, location tracking, and other unverified sources; that this data is assembled into “secret dossiers” processed by a proprietary model; that the resulting scores determine employment eligibility without candidate knowledge; and that no mechanism exists for candidates to see, verify, or dispute the information being used against them.

If the Kistler theory succeeds, the implications extend well beyond Eightfold. Every AI vendor producing a score used to determine employment eligibility would be required to disclose that score to candidates, provide access to the underlying data, and establish dispute mechanisms. The credit reporting industry was built on exactly this framework after decades of documented harm from inaccurate and inaccessible consumer files. The question before the court is whether the labor market’s version of the same harm requires the same remedy.

Eightfold will argue that a Match Score is a tool, not a report — that employers retain final judgment and the algorithm assists rather than decides. This argument has force. It also has a logical limit. When a Match Score of 1.5 means a candidate is filtered from the recruiter’s view before any human sees their file, the distinction between “assists” and “decides” is thinner than the legal brief will acknowledge.

The Regulatory Gap

New York City’s Local Law 144 is the most prominent regulatory response to algorithmic hiring in the United States. Its requirements — annual independent audit, published impact ratios, candidate notice of tool use — represent a genuine attempt to impose transparency on a process that had none. A 2025 audit by the New York State Comptroller found that out of 32 company websites reviewed for compliance, 17 showed potential non-compliance. The city’s enforcement agency had identified one.

The gap between what the law requires and what is happening reflects two problems. First, enforcement of disclosure requirements is administratively difficult — regulators cannot easily verify whether every Eightfold-powered company in the five boroughs is notifying every candidate that an automated tool is being used. Second, and more fundamentally, LL144 addresses aggregate fairness rather than individual accuracy. It asks whether the system discriminates against demographic groups. It does not ask whether the system’s judgment of a specific person is correct, whether the data underlying that judgment is accurate, or whether a candidate has any recourse when the judgment is wrong.

This is the gap the Kistler lawsuit is designed to bridge. The FCRA’s architecture — see your report, dispute inaccuracies, be notified when a report is used against you — was built precisely for the case where an institution makes a consequential decision about an individual based on data that individual cannot access. The labor market has produced exactly that case, at enormous scale, without the legal framework to address it.

The Reckoning We Are Approaching

The history of consumer data regulation in the United States is a history of harm occurring at scale, visible harm being documented and litigated, and legal frameworks arriving late. The Fair Credit Reporting Act was passed in 1970, decades after credit bureaus had been compiling files on consumers with no obligation to accuracy or disclosure. The damage done in those decades — to people denied housing, credit, and employment based on wrong or biased information they never knew existed — was not undone by the law. The law established a floor for what came after.

We are in the pre-floor period for algorithmic hiring. The damage is accumulating. Candidates are being filtered out of economic opportunity by systems they cannot see, based on data they did not submit, generating scores they have no right to challenge. The lawsuits will determine whether the existing framework of consumer protection law is adequate to address this harm, or whether new legislation is required. Neither outcome is fast.

Until the floor is established, job seekers operate in a system that was not designed with their interests in mind. Optimizing a resume for semantic matching — ensuring that relevant skills appear in recent job descriptions, not merely in a skills section; that career trajectory is presented as a logical sequence toward the target role; that LinkedIn profiles are synchronized and keyword-rich — is not a guarantee. It is an accommodation to a system whose full logic is proprietary and whose effects on any individual candidate are invisible.

The credit score analogy keeps returning because it is the most accurate one we have. We have spent fifty years building a legal architecture around the principle that people deserve to know the numbers that govern their economic lives. That architecture is imperfect, routinely gamed, and unevenly enforced. It is also vastly better than nothing.

The labor market is building the same problem from scratch. The question is whether we are willing to wait fifty years for the same inadequate answer.
