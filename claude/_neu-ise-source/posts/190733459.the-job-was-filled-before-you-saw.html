There is a particular cruelty in the verb to miss. It implies you were present for the event, that you were in the vicinity, that the failure was yours — a lapse of timing or attention or effort. You missed the train. You missed the deadline. You missed the job.

But what if you were never given the chance to be on time? What if the system through which you were searching was designed, structurally and deliberately, to show you the world as it looked three days ago?

The Architecture of Delay

Abhinav Pandey and a team of Humanitarians AI fellows spent months mapping what they call the freshness gap — the distance between when a job is published and when it becomes discoverable to a typical job seeker. The gap is not a glitch. It is infrastructure.

Here is how it works. A recruiter at a well-funded startup clicks “publish” on a backend engineering role. The job is created inside an applicant tracking system — Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby, one of a dozen platforms. From that moment, the listing is technically public. It exists in a database. It has a timestamp. It could be found.

What happens next is where the delay accumulates. Most major job boards — Indeed, LinkedIn — do not pull from ATS platforms directly. They receive XML feeds. Indeed-hosted FTP feeds refresh every two hours; externally hosted feeds every six. LinkedIn ingests ATS partner feeds every six hours and job board partner feeds every twenty-four. Google for Jobs takes a different route entirely — it crawls web pages for structured data, and without the Google Indexing API, a new listing takes three to seven days to appear. Each intake method adds a hop. Each hop adds latency. By the time a job appears on the platform where most people search, one to three days have passed. The role may have already received a hundred applications. The recruiter may have already moved to phone screens.

By the time you see it and apply, you are not late in any sense you could have controlled. You are late in the only sense that matters to the system: your application arrives after the initial surge, after the algorithmic sorting has already done its preliminary work. You missed the train. The train left before the schedule was posted.

Pandey’s discovery — the thing that became Jobsekr — is that the five largest ATS platforms expose their complete job databases through free, unauthenticated public APIs. No key. No OAuth handshake. A plain HTTP request returns every published role, with salary ranges, timestamps to the millisecond, remote status, and department. The data has always been there. The intermediaries — the XML pipelines, the crawlers, the distribution networks — are a layer of profitable latency inserted between the source and the seeker.

Who the System Was Built For

This is worth pausing on. A system that delays job data by one to three days is not neutral in its effects. The delay is regressive. It penalizes people who most need speed.

Consider who is most vulnerable to timing in a job search. The worker who was laid off last week and has bills due next month. The recent graduate who has ninety days to find visa sponsorship before their OPT authorization expires. The hourly employee looking for a second job whose schedule allows searching only on weekends. For these people, the difference between a Monday posting and a Thursday discovery is not inconvenience. It is a closed door.

The mathematics of the OPT countdown make this precise rather than impressionistic. Under the F-1 visa’s Optional Practical Training program, graduates are permitted ninety cumulative days of unemployment. The clock starts when their employment authorization begins. A seventy-two-hour freshness gap means every delay cycle consumes 3.3 percent of their total legal buffer. When aggregated across a full search — dozens of roles discovered late, dozens of applications landing after the initial surge — the cumulative latency can shorten a seeker’s viable window by weeks. Only 44.6 percent of international students successfully navigate this transition. The system does not explain why. The architecture does.

Consider who is least harmed by the delay. The person who has a warm introduction from a former colleague. The person whose recruiter sent them the job the afternoon it was posted. The person whose network reaches inside the company before the listing is even public. For them, the job board exists as backup — a place to check if nothing else is working, not the primary channel through which opportunities arrive.

The XML pipeline, the crawl latency, the programmatic distribution layer — these do not slow everyone down equally. They slow down the people for whom the job board is the primary mechanism of access. They advantage people for whom it is the last resort.

This is architecture producing outcomes nobody planned. The platforms are built to serve employers, who pay for the service, and the intermediaries who aggregate listings, who also pay for distribution. Job seekers are the product moving through the pipe, not the customer the pipe was built for.

What Open APIs Actually Mean

The Jobsekr team built parsers for fourteen ATS platforms. They normalized salary data that arrived as “$150k–$180k” in one format and “€120,000” in another. They handled a BambooHR bug where location came back as a dictionary instead of a string and crashed the entire batch. That single API quirk cost a full day of work. The intermediaries that created the freshness gap would never have noticed — it wasn’t their problem to solve.

They wrote deduplication logic for a market where the average job ad is reposted two to five times and ghost postings — roles companies aren’t actively filling, listed for pipeline-building or optics — represent an estimated eighteen to twenty-seven percent of all listings.

The full technical account is documented in Pandey’s Substack essays, and it deserves to be read in detail. But the finding that matters most is simpler than any of its implementation challenges: the data was always public. The freshness gap exists not because fresh data is inaccessible but because the intermediary layer has no incentive to close it.

This pattern appears in other domains. Financial data that is technically public but practically inaccessible without expensive terminals. Medical research locked behind paywalls despite being funded by public grants. Court records that are nominally open but practically opaque without a lawyer to navigate them. In each case, the information is “available” in the narrow sense that it is not encrypted. In the practical sense — the sense that matters for the person who needs it — it is hidden inside a system optimized for someone else’s convenience.

What Pandey built is not a technical solution to a technical problem. It is an act of redistribution. The public API data was always there. Someone needed to point it at the people for whom it matters most.

The Deeper Problem

The platforms most important to visa-dependent workers — Workday, iCIMS — are the ones with no public APIs. Workday is used by most Fortune 500 companies. iCIMS is similarly locked down. These are precisely the large, stable employers most likely to sponsor visas, to offer benefits, to provide the kind of employment continuity that matters for people navigating immigration systems. Their career sites are JavaScript-rendered applications requiring session cookies and CSRF tokens — accessible only through headless browsers and constant maintenance against frontend changes.

The closed APIs are, by accident, a map of where the most durable employment lives.

This is not an accident anyone designed. It is a consequence of which companies chose which platforms. The startups on Greenhouse and Lever built in a culture that values developer tooling and chose accordingly. The enterprise employers on Workday chose a platform built to serve their procurement workflows and HR departments — a platform with no particular interest in making its data accessible to anyone other than its paying customers.

The result: the most accessible job data belongs to employers least likely to sponsor visas, most likely to hire on a project basis. The seekers who most need the large, stable employers are the ones least served by what the open APIs can currently reach. Jobsekr’s coverage today — sixty thousand active jobs from sixteen hundred companies — is real and significant. It is also not yet the full picture of where the most consequential opportunities live.

What This Reveals

The job board that exists is not neutral.

It was built for speed that benefits those already advantaged. The job board that should exist would be built for the ninety-day countdown, for the person refreshing the search at midnight, for the seeker to whom a three-day delay is not inconvenience but catastrophe.

Pandey’s summary of the technical approach is as precise as it is pointed: faster because it skips the pipeline entirely and goes straight to the source. That sounds like a statement about engineering. It is actually a statement about power.

Most of what makes a system inaccessible to the people it theoretically serves is not technical complexity. It is layers. Each layer was added for a reason — efficiency, monetization, moderation, legal compliance. Each layer introduced latency. Each layer created a new stakeholder with an interest in maintaining itself. By the time a job posting has passed through the employer’s ATS, the XML feed generator, the board’s normalization queue, the ranking algorithm, and the display interface, the simple fact of a job existing has been transformed into a product managed by half a dozen intermediaries none of whom made the hiring decision.

What open APIs reveal is that the source is closer than it looks. The team at Humanitarians AI did not have a technical advantage over Indeed. They had a different set of questions. They asked: who is this search failing, specifically, and what would it take to build something faster for them?

The answer was: skip the intermediaries. Hit the APIs. Show the results before the pipeline has diluted them.

The next phase closes the remaining gap. The team is cross-referencing live job data with 4.8 million historical H-1B records from the Department of Labor — mapping which employers sponsor visas, at what frequency, at what wage levels, and how recently. The goal is a search that surfaces not just whether a job exists but whether applying to it is worth the clock. A role at a company that has never filed an H-1B petition is not a job lead for an OPT student. It is ninety days evaporating.

Jobsekr is free and updated three times daily. If you’re in an active search — or know someone navigating a visa countdown — start there. If you want to follow the research behind it, Abhinav Pandey’s writing is at 80 Days to Stay on Substack.

The data was always there. It took someone willing to go straight to the source to build the thing that was missing.
