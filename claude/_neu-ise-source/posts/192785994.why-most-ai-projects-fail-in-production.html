The Gap Between Lab and Production

Here’s what they don’t teach you in computer science:

What happens when your system has to process thousands of inputs under time constraints.

What happens when real-world data looks nothing like the datasets you trained on.

What happens when users depend on your system to respond quickly and reliably.

A model can perform well in controlled environments while the surrounding system struggles with latency, failure handling, or integration issues.

An approach that works on curated datasets may not generalize cleanly to noisy, real-world inputs.

A pipeline that is accurate but slow or brittle becomes difficult to use at scale.

These are not model problems. They are system problems.

And the insight that follows is simple, but not obvious:

A model that works in isolation is a curiosity. A system that works in production is a tool.

What Production Demands

APIs fail regularly. Not sometimes. Regularly. If your pipeline can’t handle this without crashing the entire system, you don’t have a production system. You have a lab demo with a critical flaw.

Pipelines break. The data format changes. The dependency updates. The cloud service you rely on has an outage. Your system must degrade gracefully, not catastrophically.

User behavior is unpredictable. They will use your accessibility tool on websites you never tested. They will upload video in formats you never trained on. They will deploy your clinical triage system in conditions you never simulated. Your system must be robust to the unanticipated, or it will fail the moment it encounters reality.

Performance matters under load. A system that works for one user and breaks for one hundred users is not a system it is a demonstration with a critical flaw.

Working within these constraints forced a different kind of thinking. Not “does this work?” but “does this keep working?” Not “can I prove this is correct?” but “can someone depend on this when I am not in the room?”
