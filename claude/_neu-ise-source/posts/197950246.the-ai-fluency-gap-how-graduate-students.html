The Four Models You Actually Need

You need to understand four tools: GPT-4o for speed, Claude 3.5 Sonnet for tone, Gemini 1.5 Pro for synthesis, o1-Preview for logic.

GPT-4o is two times faster than Claude, 56 tokens per second versus 28. Use it for iterative coding tasks, debugging, and rapid drafting. If you’re generating five variations of a LinkedIn headline or adding docstrings to Python, this is your tool. The weakness: its prose can sound like it’s selling you something.

Claude 3.5 Sonnet is the specialist in nuance. Cover letters. Cold outreach emails. LinkedIn posts where you’re trying to sound like yourself, not like a brochure. In agentic coding evaluations, Claude solved 64% of tasks autonomously. If you’re writing to a human who will judge whether you sound like a peer, use Claude.

Gemini 1.5 Pro can hold your entire dissertation, a year of lab notes, and a competitor’s patent filing in memory at once, two million tokens. That’s 1.5 million words. Upload your entire academic career and ask, “What’s missing from this body of work that McKinsey’s data science practice would care about?” The framing will be correct, and that’s what matters for strategic decisions.

o1-Preview is OpenAI’s “long thinking” model designed for logic-intensive tasks. It scores 76.6% on zero-shot chain-of-thought math benchmarks. Use it for quantitative case interviews or debugging recursive algorithms that fail in ways you don’t understand. It’s slower because it’s “thinking” before responding, but the output is more rigorous.

The Prompt Isn’t a Question, It’s a Contract

The model isn’t lazy, it’s literal.

If you say “Help me with my resume,” you’ll get a generic response. If you say, “Act as a senior technical recruiter at Goldman Sachs. Review my resume for a Quantitative Strategist role, focusing on whether my bullets demonstrate leadership and quantitative rigor. Limit feedback to the top three changes that would increase my chances of passing ATS screening,” you’ll get something usable.

This is the Role-Goal-Context framework: Role defines the AI’s expertise, Goal specifies the deliverable, Context provides constraints, Format dictates structure.

Chain-of-Thought prompting is the single most effective technique. Instead of “Solve this market-sizing problem,” ask: “Walk through the steps you’d take to solve this market-sizing problem, identifying assumptions and sanity-checking each stage.” You get logic you can defend under scrutiny.

Step-Back prompting establishes principles first. Don’t ask “Evaluate my resume for this Data Scientist role.” Ask: “What are the core competencies required for a Senior Data Scientist in 2026?” Then: “Evaluate my resume against these competencies.” The critique becomes grounded in industry standards, not the model’s general training data.

The Echo Chamber: Why AI-Written Resumes Pass AI Screeners

Here’s what nobody wants to say out loud, if you’re not using AI to write your resume, you’re at a structural disadvantage. Research from the University of Maryland and Ohio State University found that AI-written resumes are 82% more likely to survive automated screening tools than human-written ones.

There’s a self-preference bias: models favor content generated by similar architectures. A resume written using GPT-4o is 23% to 60% more likely to pass an ATS using GPT-4o than a human-written resume with identical qualifications.

The process is straightforward:

  1. Paste the job description into Claude or GPT-4o

  2. Ask it to identify the “unspoken priorities”, competencies the company values but didn’t explicitly list

  3. Rewrite your resume bullets using the job description’s own vocabulary

This isn’t deception. It’s semantic alignment. Tools like Rezi (ATS optimization), Jobscan (match percentage scoring), Teal (job tracking), and Resume Worded (language clarity grading) automate parts of this process. The consensus: AI should handle formatting and keywords, but the final document must retain “human personality markers” specific anecdotes, idiosyncratic phrasing, evidence of judgment.

The Hidden Job Market: LinkedIn as Strategic Infrastructure

Your LinkedIn headline is “prime real estate” for recruiter keywords. Instead of “PhD Candidate in Computational Biology,” try “Computational Biologist | ML for Drug Discovery | Python, R, Genomics.”

For cold mail outreach: identify a problem the person’s work addresses, ask a specific question that demonstrates homework, request a 15-minute call rather than a job. Train the AI’s voice by pasting 2-3 examples of your own emails first. Otherwise, the tone will be too formal or too obviously generated.

Ethical Guardrails: The Four-Fifths Rule

As AI embeds in hiring, regulations tighten. NYC Local Law 144 requires annual bias audits. The EU AI Act mandates transparency. The Four-Fifths Rule: advancement rates for any group should be at least 80% of the highest-advancing group. If an AI tool advances Group A at 40% and Group B at 15%, it fails 15% is less than 32%.

What this means: Omit PII when using third-party AI tools. Disclose AI use if required by local law. Always maintain human-in-the-loop, never submit 100% AI-generated documents. Read every message aloud to ensure it sounds like a human peer, not a brochure.
