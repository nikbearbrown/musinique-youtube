You opened the NUworks portal. You scrolled through dozens of listings. And almost every single one said the same thing: “1–2 years of experience preferred.”

If you’re a first or second year Information and Software Engineering student staring at that screen wondering how you’re supposed to get experience without experience, you’re not alone. This is the paradox that greets nearly every Northeastern student at the start of their co-op journey. The good news? It’s completely solvable. Here’s how.

1. Reframe What “Experience” Actually Means

Here’s the first mindset shift you need to make: experience doesn’t only mean paid work.

Recruiters reviewing entry-level and co-op applications are not expecting a polished resume packed with prior internships. What they are looking for is evidence that you can think, build, and contribute. That evidence can come from:

  • Class projects: Did you build something in a systems or software course? That belongs on your resume with a clear description of what you made and what technologies you used.

  • Personal projects: A small web app, a data scraper, a Discord bot. Anything you built on your own time demonstrates initiative and genuine interest.

  • Hackathons: Even if you didn’t win, participating shows you can work under pressure, collaborate, and ship something fast.

  • Open source contributions: Even fixing documentation or filing a well-reasoned bug report on GitHub is worth mentioning.

The key is to document everything. If you built it, write it down. If you collaborated on it, describe your specific role. Vague resume bullets get skipped; specific ones get read.

2. Build a “Proof of Work” Portfolio Before You Apply

The single most powerful thing a zero-experience applicant can do is show up with something tangible. A portfolio, even a simple one, immediately separates you from the pile of identical resumes.

Your portfolio doesn’t need to be fancy. A clean GitHub profile with 3 to 5 well-documented repositories, or a personal website built with something as simple as GitHub Pages, is more than enough to make an impression. For each project, make sure you include:

  • What it does: Explain it like you’re pitching it to someone non-technical.

  • Why you built it: Motivation reveals character. Did you solve a problem you personally experienced? That’s compelling.

  • What you learned: Showing self-awareness about your growth is something junior developers rarely do, and hiring managers notice it.

Think of your portfolio as a living document. Start it now, add to it continuously, and link it in every application you send.

3. Target the Right Opportunities First

Not all co-op listings are created equal. When you have no prior experience, applying to highly competitive postings at FAANG-adjacent companies out of the gate is a strategy that leads to discouragement, not offers.

Instead, be strategic about where you aim first:

  • Startups and early-stage companies: They often value raw enthusiasm and the ability to learn quickly over a polished track record. You’ll also likely get broader, more meaningful responsibilities.

  • Nonprofit and mission-driven organizations: Programs like Humanitarians AI, which is connected directly to our own department, are excellent entry points. They give you real technical work, a strong resume line, and a network of purpose-driven professionals.

  • On-campus and university-affiliated roles: Research assistant positions, IT support roles, and departmental projects are legitimate co-op and internship experience, and they come with built-in mentorship.

  • Local Boston tech scene: Boston has a thriving ecosystem of biotech, fintech, and edtech companies that regularly recruit from Northeastern. Many specifically seek co-ops because they want to train talent from the ground up.

Cast a wide net, but cast it intelligently.

4. Make Your Application Materials Do the Heavy Lifting

When experience is thin, your resume and cover letter need to work harder. A few principles that make a real difference:

On your resume:

  • Lead with a strong skills section. If you know Python, SQL, React, Git, or any relevant tools, list them prominently.

  • Use action verbs that convey impact: designed, implemented, automated, analyzed, deployed. Not just “worked on” or “helped with.”

  • Keep it to one page. Ruthlessly cut anything that doesn’t strengthen your candidacy for the specific role.

In your cover letter:

  • Don’t summarize your resume. Instead, tell a short story: What drew you to this field? What problem do you want to solve? Why this company specifically?

  • Mention one or two things from your portfolio or coursework that are directly relevant to the role.

  • Show that you’ve done your research. A single specific reference to the company’s product, team, or mission signals genuine interest and sets you apart from generic applicants.

5. Treat Networking as a Skill, Not a Chore

Most students avoid networking because it feels awkward or transactional. Reframe it: networking is just having genuine conversations with people who are doing work you find interesting.

Some concrete ways to do this as an ISE student:

  • Attend the ISE Speaker Series. Every speaker is a potential connection. Ask a thoughtful question after their talk and follow up on LinkedIn that same evening.

  • Use Northeastern’s alumni network. The Northeastern alumni base is enormous and famously loyal to other Huskies. A cold LinkedIn message that leads with “I’m a current ISE student at Northeastern” gets opened.

  • Go to hackathons and tech meetups in Boston. In-person events create the kind of rapport that online applications never can.

You don’t need a large network. You need a few real relationships with people who know your name and what you’re working toward.

6. Don’t Wait Until You’re “Ready”

The most common mistake first-year students make is waiting until they feel ready to start applying. They tell themselves they’ll apply after they finish one more project, learn one more framework, get a little more comfortable.

The truth is that readiness is built through the process of applying, not before it. Every application teaches you something. Every recruiter screen sharpens your communication. Every rejection narrows down what you actually need to work on.

Start applying early. Apply broadly. Treat every interview as practice and every rejection as data. The students who land their first co-op are rarely the most technically advanced. They’re the ones who started first and kept going.

The Bottom Line

Landing a co-op with no prior experience is not a matter of luck or connections. It’s a matter of strategy, consistency, and being willing to show your work before anyone asks you to. Build something. Document it. Put it in front of people. Repeat.

Your first co-op won’t be your dream job. It will be something better: proof that you can do it.
