When I started the MGEN program, I had a background in electronics engineering, two years of software development experience in India, and a vague but persistent feeling that I wanted to work in AI. I didn’t have a clear plan. I didn’t know anyone in Boston. And I definitely didn’t know that the next two years would reshape how I think about building, learning, and showing up.

Starting From Zero (And I Mean Zero)

Before Northeastern, I was at Persistent Systems in Pune, India, writing software and slowly realizing I wanted more. AI and machine learning fascinated me, but I had no hands-on experience with either. Moving to the U.S. for grad school meant starting fresh - professionally, socially, everything.

The first few weeks in Boston were humbling. Not just the logistics of figuring out a new country - that’s hard but manageable. The real gut punch was the professional restart. Back home, I had credibility. People knew my work. Here, I was starting from a blank page.

What I’d tell current students: That blank page feeling is normal. It doesn’t mean you’re behind. It means you’re at the beginning, and that’s exactly where you’re supposed to be.

The Coursework That Actually Changed Me

The MGEN program lets you design your own track, and I’d encourage every student to be intentional about that. Don’t just pick what sounds interesting - pick what scares you a little.

Prof. Handan Liu’s High-Performance Parallel ML & AI course had me doing distributed GPU training with CUDA. I had no business being comfortable with that, and I wasn’t - but by the end, those became some of the most marketable skills on my resume. Prof. Srikanth Krishnamurthy’s Big Data Systems class pushed me to build data pipelines at a scale that felt impossible at first. And Prof. Nik Bear Brown’s Advances in Data Science class is where I built MediPedia - a medical chatbot powered entirely by open-source tools because I couldn’t afford paid APIs.

That last one is important. MediPedia wasn’t my most technically sophisticated project. But it was the one where I got scrappy, figured things out with what I had, and built something that worked. It’s also the project that landed me my co-op.

What I’d tell current students: Your capstone project or class project might feel like just another assignment. It’s not. Treat it like a portfolio piece. Build something you’d be proud to demo in an interview, because you might have to.

The Co-op Search (The Part Nobody Warns You About)

Let’s talk about this honestly, because I wish someone had told me: the co-op search is hard. Really hard. There were stretches of silence, rejections after interviews I thought went well, and days where I questioned whether any of this was going to work out.

Prof. Josie Cucciniello’s Career Management for Engineers course (ENCP 6000) helped me stay grounded through it. The mock interviews, the guest speakers who’d been through the same process, the honest conversations about how the U.S. job market works for international students - it all made a difference when things felt uncertain.

I eventually landed an AI Engineer role at Jutly Inc., an early-stage startup in Cambridge. It wasn’t a big-name company, and that turned out to be the best thing for me. I was prototyping AI workflows with LangGraph and LangChain, navigating ambiguity daily, and learning that startups don’t hand you a roadmap - they hand you a problem and trust you to figure it out. I grew more in those few months than I expected.

What I’d tell current students: The co-op search will test you more than any exam. It’s okay to feel lost in the middle of it. Keep a system, refine after every setback, and lean on ENCP 6000 - it exists for exactly this reason.

Hackathons: Where It All Comes Together

If there’s one thing I’d go back and do more of, it’s hackathons. They became the spaces where everything I’d learned collided under pressure - and produced the work I’m proudest of.

At the DreamAI 2025 Hackathon, my team was building a health-tracking app when user interviews told us we were solving the wrong problem. Women didn’t need another tracker - they needed help understanding how to pay for healthcare. I pushed my team to throw away our working code and start over. We built FinFluent, an AI finance assistant, and pitched it to investors as finalists.

At Confluent AI Day, we placed 2nd for SecureStream AI - a real-time privacy detection system built in three hours. At the MIT Women’s Health AI Hackathon, we tackled the fact that women make up half the world’s population but less than 5% of medical research data.

Every hackathon taught me something courses couldn’t: how to listen to users, how to make tough calls under pressure, and how to build with purpose - not just with code. I didn’t win them all, and some of my ideas fell flat. But every single one made me a better builder.

What I’d tell current students: Sign up for hackathons. Even if you don’t feel ready - especially if you don’t feel ready. The outcome matters less than what you learn in the process.

Looking Back From the Other Side

I graduated in December 2025, and I’m still figuring a lot of things out. But the thing I value most from this experience is simpler than any credential: I proved to myself that I could leave everything familiar, start from nothing, and build something I’m proud of - even when I wasn’t sure I could.

MGEN gave me the foundation. The flexibility to design my own path, the faculty who pushed me, the co-op that threw me into the deep end, and a community that made me feel like I belonged - even on the days I wasn’t sure I did.

There’s still so much more ahead. But if you’re reading this at the beginning of your own MGEN journey, I want you to know: the blank page is the best part. Fill it with things that scare you, things that matter to you, and people who make you better.

You’ll be amazed at what you build.
