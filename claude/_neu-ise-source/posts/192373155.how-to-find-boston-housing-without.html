Before you can move into an apartment in Boston, you need three things ready: about $9,000 to $12,000 in upfront costs, documentation proving you’re legally authorized to be here, and the ability to spot the difference between a legitimate lease and one designed to extract money you don’t legally owe.

You can complete the entire apartment search from your home country. Thousands of international students secure legitimate Boston housing remotely every year. The key is knowing which verification steps are non-negotiable.

What You Need Before You Start Looking

Documentation sequence (complete this first):

  • I-20 or DS-2019 form (name must exactly match your passport)

  • I-901 SEVIS fee paid and receipted ($350)

  • Bank statements showing proof of funds (satisfies both immigration and landlords)

Financial reality: Most landlords require first month’s rent + last month’s rent + security deposit (max one month’s rent). For a $3,000/month apartment, that’s $9,000 upfront.

The 2025 savings: Massachusetts eliminated the fourth charge—broker fees that tenants used to pay—dropping typical upfront costs from $12,000 to $9,000. On August 1, 2025, the state enacted “Who Hires, Pays”: if the landlord hires the broker to market their property, the landlord pays the commission. You’re no longer funding someone else’s marketing costs. This is $3,000 you keep.

Northeastern’s resources: The Office of Campus Engagement and Support (OCES) at 151 Speare Hall provides lease review, roommate matching, and access to the verified housing database at https://aptsearch.northeastern.edu/. Use these. They exist because Boston’s rental market assumes you have U.S. credit history, local co-signers, and familiarity with state law—assumptions that don’t match your reality.

Where You Can Actually Afford to Live

Every Boston neighborhood forces a trade: proximity to campus, rent you can afford, or safety you feel secure in. You rarely get all three. Here’s how to choose:

Mission Hill — Walking distance, premium price Rent: $4,250 median (studios $2,529, 3-beds $4,802) Safety: Crime rate 28.91 per 1,000 (property theft primary concern, violent crime low at 5.11 per 1,000) Best for: Students who prioritize zero commute over cost savings

Roxbury (Fort Hill section) — Best value proposition Rent: $2,498 average (nearly $1,750 less than Mission Hill) Safety: Fort Hill area increasingly student-populated, 52% report feeling “pretty safe” Best for: Budget-conscious students comfortable with 15-minute Orange Line commute

Malden or Quincy — Commuter strategy Rent: Below city center pricing ($500-800/month savings) Commute: 22-38 minutes via MBTA Best for: Students trading time for money, comfortable with consistent transit

Fenway/South End — High-end urban Rent: $3,800-$4,800 Best for: Students with higher budgets or 4+ roommate groups

Use Northeastern’s database filters for “international student friendly” and “no fee” listings. Cross-reference with MBTA maps if considering the commuter strategy.

Finding Roommates Who Share Your Context

Northeastern’s roommate-matching platform at https://aptsearch.northeastern.edu/ goes beyond generic filters. You can search for roommates from your home country or specific city.

Why cultural matching matters: When you’re adjusting to a new academic system, a new country, and a new language simultaneously, having roommates who share your cultural reference points reduces total cognitive load. You’ll integrate plenty in classes, co-ops, and campus activities. Your apartment can be the place where you don’t have to explain why you’re video-calling home at 3 AM or why certain holidays matter.

The platform lets you filter by: Home country or specific cities Sleep schedules (early riser vs. night owl) Cleanliness standards Guest policies Noise tolerance Cooking habits (shared meals vs. separate)

Success pattern: Many students start by living with roommates from their region during the first year while adjusting, then branch out to more diverse housing in subsequent years. Others live with people from their home country for their entire degree. Both patterns work. The platform gives you the choice.

Once you find compatible roommates: Use Northeastern’s roommate agreement templates to formalize expectations on rent division, utility responsibility, quiet hours, and conflict resolution. These aren’t legally binding like leases, but they create a written record when “I thought we agreed...” disputes arise.

The Co-Signer Problem: Three Options That Work

Most Boston landlords require a U.S.-based co-signer. You don’t have one. Here are the three options—none are cheap, but all are navigable:

Option 1: Third-Party Guarantor Services Companies like Insurent, TheGuarantors, and Leap act as institutional co-signers. Cost: 70-110% of one month’s rent (one-time, non-refundable) Requirements: Proof of liquid assets equal to 50× monthly rent, or FICO score above 640 Example: $3,000 apartment requires showing $150,000 in accessible funds + paying $2,100-$3,300

Option 2: Direct Negotiation Some landlords waive the co-signer if you provide: I-20 showing full financial certification Bank statements demonstrating ability to pay full lease term Offer to pay 3-6 months upfront

Option 3: Co-Living Arrangements Companies like June Homes offer furnished rooms with flexible co-signer policies explicitly for international students. More expensive per square foot, but eliminates the guarantor hurdle.

Utility Costs: The Hidden Monthly Expense

Rent is only part of the monthly cost. Boston utilities fluctuate with New England’s extreme seasons.

Average monthly utilities (2026): Electricity: $162 Gas heating: Included in rent (best case) or $100-$300/month in winter Electric heating: Avoid if possible—can exceed $500/month in winter Internet: $78 (shop for student discounts—see below) Water/Waste: Usually included in rent

Total monthly utilities: $220-$280 average, but can spike to $500+ in January/February with electric heat.

Internet savings: During Northeastern’s Fall Fest (first week of September), major internet providers set up campus booths offering student-exclusive rates. Xfinity, Verizon Fios, and Starry typically offer 15-30% off standard pricing, some waive installation fees, and promotional rates lock in for 12 months. Wait until Fall Fest before signing up if possible—standard cost is around $78/month; Fall Fest deals bring this to $55-65/month.

Cost-saving strategies: Enroll in “City of Boston Community Choice Electricity” for rates as low as 14.2 cents/kWh Prioritize apartments where “heat and hot water included” appears in listing Many Mission Hill/Fenway brick buildings have central boilers—heat is included, protecting you from winter spikes

What’s Still Illegal (Even If It’s In Your Lease)

Massachusetts law prohibits landlords from charging: Application fees (unless charged by a licensed broker for screening) Administrative fees, holding fees, or “processing fees” Any fee not explicitly: first month’s rent, last month’s rent, security deposit (max one month), or lock/key replacement cost

If a landlord asks for a “processing fee” or “paperwork fee,” that’s illegal. Refuse, cite the law, and if they insist, walk away.

Book a lease review appointment at OCES before signing anything. They’ve seen every version of every problematic clause and can tell you exactly what’s enforceable.
