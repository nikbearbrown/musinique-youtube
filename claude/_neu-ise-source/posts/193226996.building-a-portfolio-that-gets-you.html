Every semester, hundreds of talented ISE students submit polished resumes to co-op postings — and hear nothing back. The problem isn’t their GPA, their coursework, or their ambition. The problem is that a resume only conveys what you claim about yourself. In a field built on verifiable outputs, that’s a surprisingly weak pitch.

The students who consistently land competitive roles share one thing: they show up with proof. Not bullet points — artifacts. Code. Projects. Writing. Things a hiring manager can open and evaluate right now, without scheduling a single conversation. ISE students at Northeastern have used this approach to land co-ops at companies including:

Google · Fidelity Investments · HubSpot · Wayfair · Amazon · MathWorks · Liberty Mutual · + Boston startups

What they share isn’t a perfect GPA or a lucky referral. It’s a portfolio that does the talking before the interview even starts.

Why “proof of work” actually matters

The shift toward portfolio-driven hiring reflects how engineering work is actually evaluated on the job. When your team lead asks you to ship a feature, they don’t want a summary of your capabilities. They want the feature. A strong portfolio signals, before you even walk into a room, that you think in outputs rather than effort — and that’s the mindset every great engineering team is hiring for.

For ISE students, the intersection of software and information systems means your portfolio can span a genuinely wide range: web apps, data pipelines, AI integrations, UX research, technical writing, even well-documented coursework. That breadth is a competitive advantage — if you use it intentionally.

Five things every strong ISE portfolio includes

01 : A deployed project

Something live, even if small. Deployed beats documented beats described — every time.

02 : A clean GitHub profile

Commit history and quality READMEs signal professional habits, not just claimed skill.

03 : A write-up of something hard

Walking through your reasoning process is more impressive than the solution alone.

04 : A co-op story with depth

What was the real problem? What did you ship? What broke? What would you redo?

05 : Evidence of curiosity

Side projects, open-source contributions, a technical article. Build when no one’s watching.

How to build one if you’re starting from zero

The most common mistake is waiting for the perfect project. Don’t. Start with something small and ship it. Rebuild a tool you actually use. Scrape a dataset you’re curious about. Automate something tedious in your own life. The goal in year one isn’t to impress anyone — it’s to build a pattern of shipping that becomes its own credential over time.

Document as you go. Writing up what you built — the decisions you made and why — sharpens your thinking and produces interview material almost automatically. Every time a recruiter asks “tell me about a project you’re proud of,” a well-documented portfolio gives you a story that’s already been rehearsed in writing.

The resume still matters — here’s how they work together

A portfolio doesn’t replace your resume. It amplifies it. The resume gets you through the initial screen; the portfolio closes the deal in the conversation that follows. Think of the resume as the table of contents and the portfolio as the actual book. Both need to exist, and both need to be genuinely good.

The best ISE graduates treat their portfolio as a living document — updated every semester, linked prominently on their resume and LinkedIn, maintained with the same care they’d give a production codebase. That habit, more than any single project, is what separates the students who land the roles they want from the ones who settle for whatever’s left in March.

Start now. Ship something small. Write it up. Link to it everywhere. That’s the whole playbook.
