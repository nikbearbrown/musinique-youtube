There is a particular anxiety embedded in academic institutions when new technology arrives: the anxiety of not knowing whether to study the thing or become it. Universities have historically been better at producing scholarship about tools than producing people who can use them. They write papers on the sociology of the hammer. They do not teach carpentry.

Nik Bear Brown, an Associate Teaching Professor of Engineering at Northeastern University, has chosen the other path. His answer to the AI question isn’t a curriculum or a framework or a think-piece. It’s a web page with hundreds of working projects, a nonprofit called Humanitarians AI, and a philosophy compressed to two words: just do it. When OpenAI invited him to give a video presentation on AI in education, he didn’t deliver a lecture. He did a show and tell.

The distinction matters.

The show and tell covers ground fast—maybe too fast, Brown admits, because he runs out of time before he runs out of material. But the velocity itself is the argument. What he presents in under ten minutes is a working ecosystem: custom GPTs deployed in live classrooms, an AI-assisted textbook spanning hundreds of chapters on cancer and nanomedicine, an intelligent book interface that adjusts its presentation based on who’s reading, a computational skepticism framework that automatically verifies AI-generated claims, agentic systems for protein prediction and wastewater research. None of it is speculative. All of it is already being used by students.

Start with the custom GPTs—not because they’re the most ambitious work in the presentation, but because they’re the most instructive about Brown’s method. Ada tutors students in introductory calculus. Grace handles office hours for his Coursera algorithms course so completely that, after the first week, students stop showing up in person. Nuke teaches introductory physics at Northeastern alongside Professors Shri and Tom Kelly. Each bot took roughly half an hour to an hour to build. Each scores approximately 4.8 out of 5 in student surveys. Each embeds not just information but tone—Ada, under the influence of Professor Abby Williams who has since taken over its prompting, is encouraging even when students are wrong. What you got right. What you got wrong. Good job on this part.

One detail Brown offers in passing deserves to linger: a student told Williams that she’d been afraid to ask her professor questions directly—afraid of seeming stupid. She uses Ada instead. The bot is, in this case, a proxy for intellectual safety. The technology isn’t replacing the relationship between teacher and student; it’s extending the conditions under which that relationship can function.

Here is where I want to slow down, because Brown’s project raises a question that gets asked constantly in AI-and-education circles but rarely answered with this kind of specificity: what exactly does it mean to learn AI by doing AI?

The orthodox answer in most universities is something like: study the theory, understand the mathematics, then apply. You must walk before you run. This is a coherent pedagogical position, and it has a long track record. It also, in the current moment, has a problem. The field moves faster than the curriculum. A student who graduates with thorough grounding in last year’s models and no experience building with this year’s tools is at a systematic disadvantage—not because theory is wrong, but because the gap between knowing and doing has never been wider or faster-moving.

Brown’s Humanitarians AI nonprofit frames its response to this gap in terms of lifelong learning: graduates who left school two years ago are already facing AI tools that simply didn’t exist when they earned their degrees. The solution isn’t to retrofit their education in retrospect. It’s to build a practice of continuous building—a culture where the appropriate response to a new tool is to deploy it on a real problem immediately, not to wait for the certification.

The word “skunkworks” appears in Brown’s framing of this work—a reference to Lockheed Martin’s legendary advanced development division, created in the 1940s precisely to circumvent institutional bureaucracy and get experimental aircraft built. Brown’s AI skunkworks at Northeastern operates on the same logic: the bureaucracy of institutional funding is too slow. A single GPT Plus account can deploy a custom chatbot to hundreds of students. The currency of this operation isn’t budget approval. It’s momentum.

Whether this scales—whether it produces students who understand what they’re building or only students who have built things—is a genuine question. The two are not the same. A student who prompts their way through a data analysis pipeline may have produced valid results without understanding the statistical assumptions underneath. Brown’s computational skepticism framework, Popper, seems to acknowledge this risk: it’s a set of automated tools designed to verify AI outputs, to hold the system accountable to evidence. The name is deliberate. Karl Popper’s philosophy of science rested on falsifiability—on the principle that a claim is only scientific if it can, in principle, be shown wrong. Brown appears to be building that epistemological standard directly into his pedagogy, creating tools that check rather than merely accept.

That combination—build fast, verify rigorously—is not the most common posture in AI education. Most approaches land on one side or the other: either cautious, theory-first, or breathlessly generative. Brown’s bet is that you need both, simultaneously, woven together, taught through practice.

The nanomedicine textbook is the most ambitious thing in the presentation, and Brown treats it almost in passing—another project in a long list—which may itself be the point. The book is enormous in scope: cancer treatment, nanomedicine, a global audience including regions with limited access to academic resources. Aan is leading the writing. AI is helping produce first drafts of chapters. Popper is then combing through the claims—hundreds of assertions per chapter—gathering evidence, checking sources, asking where the proof is.

The project emerges from a collaboration with Srinivas Sridhar, University Distinguished Professor of Physics, Biomedical Engineering, and Chemical Engineering at Northeastern, and Lecturer on Radiation Oncology at Harvard Medical School. Sridhar is not a peripheral figure in this story. He is the Director of Northeastern’s Nanomedicine Innovation Center and the NIH CaNCURE program, a Fellow of the American Physical Society and the National Academy of Inventors, the author of more than 450 journal articles and patents across nanomedicine, neurotechnology, and quantum chaos. His 2003 paper in Nature was named among Science magazine’s Breakthroughs of the Year. He has trained and mentored over 1,200 students across 14 PhD programs. His CaNCURE program specifically targets experiential cancer nanomedicine training for undergraduates, with particular attention to students from minority-serving institutions. The Sridhar Lab’s research spans 26 countries and has drawn $24 million in grant funding from NSF, NIH, and the Department of Defense.

The book, in other words, isn’t a side project. It’s the written record of one of the most productive research and education programs in nanomedicine anywhere. The question Brown is asking—can AI help produce this book in months rather than a decade—lands differently once you understand the scope of what’s being documented.

Brown says plainly: he doesn’t think this book gets written without AI. That’s a significant statement, and it’s worth taking seriously rather than treating it as enthusiasm. Large, interdisciplinary scientific textbooks have always been slow to produce. The incentive structures of academic publishing are misaligned with the pace at which science moves. A book that takes a decade to write on nanotechnology is, by definition, outdated by the time it arrives. If AI-assisted drafting and automated verification can compress that timeline to months without compromising accuracy, the implications extend well beyond one textbook at one university. Sridhar’s global reach—240 presentations in 30 countries, collaborators across six continents—means the audience for this work is genuinely international. The Madavi intelligent textbook interface, which adjusts its presentation based on whether you’re a physician or a high school student, is built precisely for that heterogeneous readership.

The project called Madavi—the intelligent textbook interface—adds another layer. A physician reading the nanomedicine book needs different navigation than a high school student curious about cancer biology. Madavi adjusts the presentation based on who’s reading. This is not a new idea in educational technology, but it’s rarely been implemented at the scale and specificity that current language models make possible.

Brown ends his presentation, somewhat breathlessly, with a list of projects he doesn’t have time to cover: protein prediction agents, reinforcement learning extensions for agentic AI, SERS spectroscopy tools for public health. The timer is running out and he is—genuinely, visibly—a little sad about it.

The sadness is instructive. This isn’t the performance of a person demonstrating a project. It’s the frustration of someone who builds faster than they can explain. The gap between what his lab produces and what he can show in a ten-minute video is not a communication problem. It’s a productivity problem of the best kind.

The question worth sitting with isn’t whether Brown’s approach is replicable at every institution—it isn’t, and he’d probably say so. It’s what this model reveals about the cost of institutional caution. Every university that waited for consensus on how to handle AI education watched another semester pass. Every committee convened to discuss the appropriate role of generative tools produced students who graduated without knowing how to use them. The bureaucratic timeline Brown describes—a year to secure modest funding for an educational project—isn’t hyperbole. It’s a lived constraint he solved by simply not waiting.

The lab that builds by doing is not, finally, just a pedagogical philosophy. It’s a critique of the institution that asked Brown to show it what he’d built, delivered in the form of ten minutes he ran out of.
