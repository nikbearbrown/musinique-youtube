Pedagogical Foundation

Linear regression is not a statistical trick—it is a logical framework for quantifying relationships between variables. Before we begin: this is not about memorizing formulas. This is about understanding why each formula exists, what assumptions justify its use, and when those assumptions break down.

I. The Geometric Foundation: What Problem Are We Solving?

The Setup

You observe n data points: (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), ..., (xₙ, yₙ). You hypothesize that y depends linearly on x. This means you believe:

y = β₀ + β₁x + ε

Where:

  • β₀ is the intercept (predicted y when x = 0)

  • β₁ is the slope (change in y per unit change in x)

  • ε is the error term (everything we cannot predict)

This is not a statistical model yet. This is a geometric claim: points in the (x,y) plane cluster around a line.

The Logical Problem

You don’t know β₀ or β₁. You must estimate them from data. Call your estimates β̂₀ and β̂₁. For any choice of these parameters, you can compute predicted values:

ŷᵢ = β̂₀ + β̂₁xᵢ

The residual for observation i is: eᵢ = yᵢ - ŷᵢ

This is the vertical distance from the observed point to your line. The question becomes: which line minimizes total error?

Why Squared Residuals?

You cannot minimize Σeᵢ directly—positive and negative errors cancel. You could minimize Σ|eᵢ| (absolute deviations), which is geometrically sensible and produces median regression. But we minimize Σeᵢ² for three reasons:

  1. Mathematical tractability: Squares are differentiable everywhere; absolute values are not

  2. Unique solution: The function Σeᵢ² is strictly convex, guaranteeing a single global minimum

  3. Connections to probability: If errors are normally distributed, least squares is the maximum likelihood estimator

The third point is crucial: least squares is optimal only if errors are normal. Without that assumption, it’s merely convenient.

II. Deriving the Estimators: Calculus as Logical Necessity

The Minimization Problem

We want β̂₀ and β̂₁ such that:

RSS = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (yᵢ - β̂₀ - β̂₁xᵢ)² is minimized

This is a calculus problem. Take partial derivatives with respect to β̂₀ and β̂₁, set them to zero:

∂RSS/∂β̂₀ = -2Σ(yᵢ - β̂₀ - β̂₁xᵢ) = 0

∂RSS/∂β̂₁ = -2Σxᵢ(yᵢ - β̂₀ - β̂₁xᵢ) = 0

From the first equation: Σyᵢ = nβ̂₀ + β̂₁Σxᵢ

Divide by n: ȳ = β̂₀ + β̂₁x̄

Therefore: β̂₀ = ȳ - β̂₁x̄

This proves the regression line passes through (x̄, ȳ), the center of mass of the data. This is not an assumption—it’s a logical consequence of minimizing squared vertical distances.

Substitute into the second equation and solve (algebra omitted):

β̂₁ = Σ(xᵢ - x̄)(yᵢ - ȳ) / Σ(xᵢ - x̄)²

This is the sample covariance of x and y divided by the sample variance of x. The slope measures how much y co-varies with x, scaled by the spread in x.

III. What the Error Term Means: Four Assumptions

The model y = β₀ + β₁x + ε requires the error term ε to satisfy four conditions. These are not suggestions—they are logical requirements for the inferential machinery (p-values, confidence intervals) to work.

Assumption 1: E[ε] = 0 (Zero Mean)

If errors have non-zero mean, you’ve systematically misspecified the intercept. Suppose E[ε] = c ≠ 0. Then:

E[y] = β₀ + β₁x + c = (β₀ + c) + β₁x

The true intercept is β₀ + c, not β₀. Your model is wrong.

Consequence of violation: Predictions are systematically biased. If E[ε] = 2, every prediction is too low by 2 units.

Assumption 2: Independence of Errors

Errors must be uncorrelated: Cov(εᵢ, εⱼ) = 0 for i ≠ j. This means knowing one residual tells you nothing about another.

Common violation: Time series data. If today’s error is positive, tomorrow’s is more likely positive (autocorrelation). Standard errors become invalid—you underestimate uncertainty.

Detection: Plot residuals against time or observation order. Look for patterns.

Assumption 3: Homoscedasticity (Constant Variance)

Var(εᵢ) = σ² for all i. The spread of errors doesn’t change with x.

Common violation: Heteroscedasticity—variance increases with x. Example: predicting income from education. High-education individuals show greater income variability.

Consequence: Standard errors are wrong. You might declare a coefficient “significant” when it’s not.

Detection: Plot residuals vs. fitted values. If spread fans out, variance is non-constant.

Assumption 4: Normality of Errors

εᵢ ~ N(0, σ²). This is required for t-statistics and F-statistics to follow their named distributions.

Consequence of violation: p-values are approximate, not exact. For large n, Central Limit Theorem rescues you. For small n, conclusions may be invalid.

Detection: Q-Q plot (quantile-quantile plot). If residuals are normal, points lie on a straight line.

IV. Standard Errors: Quantifying Uncertainty in Estimates

What Standard Errors Measure

β̂₁ is a random variable. If you collected different data, you’d get a different slope. The standard error SE(β̂₁) measures the typical size of this variation.

Under the four assumptions above:

SE(β̂₁)² = σ² / Σ(xᵢ - x̄)²

Where σ² = Var(ε). We estimate σ² from residuals:

σ̂² = Σeᵢ² / (n - 2)

We divide by n - 2 because we’ve estimated two parameters (β̂₀ and β̂₁), losing two degrees of freedom.

Interpretation

SE(β̂₁) is smaller when:

  1. σ² is small: Less noise in the relationship

  2. Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² is large: Greater spread in x values gives more leverage to estimate slope

If all x values are nearly identical, Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² ≈ 0, and SE(β̂₁) → ∞. You cannot estimate a slope from vertical data.

Confidence Intervals

A 95% confidence interval for β₁ is:

β̂₁ ± 2·SE(β̂₁)

(More precisely, use the 97.5th percentile of a t-distribution with n - 2 degrees of freedom, but 2 is a good approximation for n > 30.)

Correct interpretation: If you repeatedly sampled data and constructed intervals this way, 95% would contain the true β₁.

Incorrect interpretation: “There’s a 95% probability β₁ is in this interval.” β₁ is fixed; the interval is random.

V. Hypothesis Testing: Is the Slope Really Zero?

The Null Hypothesis

H₀: β₁ = 0 (no relationship between x and y)

If β₁ = 0, then y = β₀ + ε, and x provides no information. The alternative is:

H₁: β₁ ≠ 0

The t-Statistic

t = β̂₁ / SE(β̂₁)

This measures “how many standard errors is β̂₁ away from zero?” If H₀ is true and errors are normal, t follows a t-distribution with n - 2 degrees of freedom.

Decision rule: If |t| > 2 (approximately), reject H₀ at the 5% significance level. The p-value gives the exact probability of observing |t| this large or larger if H₀ were true.

p < 0.05: Strong evidence against H₀. The observed slope is unlikely to arise from noise alone.

p > 0.05: Weak evidence against H₀. We “fail to reject” (not “accept”) the null.

Common Mistake

A t-statistic of 1 does NOT mean “the effect is 100%.” It means β̂₁ equals its standard error—the signal barely exceeds the noise. A t-statistic of 38.2 / 38.2 = 1 is very weak evidence.

A t-statistic of 38.2 / 3.4 = 11.2 is overwhelming evidence. The estimated slope is 11 times larger than its sampling variability.

VI. R²: Measuring Explanatory Power

Three Sums of Squares

Define:

  • SStotal = Σ(yᵢ - ȳ)²: Total variance in y (if you predicted ȳ for everyone)

  • SSreg = Σ(ŷᵢ - ȳ)²: Variance explained by the model

  • SSres = Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²: Residual variance (what the model misses)

Key identity: SStotal = SSreg + SSres

This is a Pythagorean theorem in n-dimensional space.

Definition of R²

R² = SSreg / SStotal = 1 - SSres / SStotal

R² measures the proportion of variance in y explained by x.

Range: 0 ≤ R² ≤ 1

  • R² = 0: The model explains nothing; you might as well predict ȳ for everyone

  • R² = 1: Perfect fit; all points lie exactly on the line

Interpretation

R² = 0.75 means the model explains 75% of the variance in y. The remaining 25% is due to factors not captured by x (or measurement error, or irreducible randomness).

Critical limitation: R² only measures fit to observed data. It does not measure predictive accuracy on new data. A model can have R² = 0.99 and still predict terribly if it’s overfit.

R² vs. Correlation

For simple linear regression, R² = r², where r is the Pearson correlation between x and y. This is a mathematical identity.

For multiple regression (multiple predictors), R² no longer equals any single correlation. It measures the combined explanatory power of all predictors.

VII. Multiple Regression: Adding Predictors

The Model

y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + ... + βₚxₚ + ε

Each β̂ⱼ now represents the change in y per unit change in xⱼ, holding all other predictors constant. This is crucial.

Example: Advertising Data

Sales = β₀ + β₁(TV) + β₂(Radio) + β₃(Newspaper) + ε

From the notebook results:

  • β̂₁ = 0.046: Each $1,000 in TV ad spending increases sales by 46 units, holding Radio and Newspaper constant

  • β̂₂ = 0.189: Each $1,000 in Radio increases sales by 189 units, holding TV and Newspaper constant

  • β̂₃ = -0.001: Newspaper advertising has a negative coefficient (!)

Interpreting the Newspaper Coefficient

Does newspaper advertising hurt sales? Not necessarily. Three explanations:

  1. Confounding: Newspaper spending correlates negatively with TV/Radio spending. Companies that spend more on Newspaper spend less on TV, and TV is more effective. The coefficient captures this substitution, not a causal effect.

  2. Multicollinearity: If Newspaper and TV are highly correlated, their effects become difficult to separate. Standard errors inflate, coefficients become unstable.

  3. Genuine effect: Perhaps newspaper ads really do confuse or alienate customers. Possible, but requires additional evidence.

Lesson: Regression coefficients are not causal effects. They are conditional associations given the model.

VIII. The F-Test: Testing All Coefficients Simultaneously

The Null Hypothesis

For multiple regression:

H₀: β₁ = β₂ = ... = βₚ = 0 (no predictors matter)

Individual t-tests can mislead. With p = 20 predictors, you expect one p-value < 0.05 purely by chance, even if all βⱼ = 0.

The F-Statistic

F = [(SStotal - SSres) / p] / [SSres / (n - p - 1)]

This compares the improvement in fit from adding p predictors (numerator) to the remaining residual variance (denominator).

If H₀ is true, F follows an F-distribution with (p, n - p - 1) degrees of freedom. Large F values indicate at least one predictor is useful.

Decision rule: If p-value for F < 0.05, reject H₀. At least one βⱼ ≠ 0.

Relationship to R²

F increases with R². Specifically:

F = [(R² / p) / ((1 - R²) / (n - p - 1))]

A high R² produces a high F-statistic, but F also penalizes models with many predictors (large p).

IX. Residual Diagnostics: When the Model Breaks

Why Residual Plots Matter

Summary statistics (R², F-statistic, t-statistics) assume the four error assumptions hold. If they don’t, these statistics are meaningless. Residual plots reveal violations invisible to numerical summaries.

Diagnostic Plot 1: Residuals vs. Fitted Values

Purpose: Check linearity and homoscedasticity.

What to look for:

  • Random scatter around zero: Good. Model captures the relationship.

  • Curved pattern: The relationship is non-linear. Try polynomial terms or transformations.

  • Funnel shape (widening spread): Heteroscedasticity. Variance increases with ŷ. Standard errors are invalid.

Diagnostic Plot 2: Q-Q Plot (Normal Quantile-Quantile)

Purpose: Check normality of residuals.

What to look for:

  • Points lie on a straight line: Residuals are approximately normal.

  • Heavy tails (S-shaped curve): Distribution has outliers.

  • Skewed departure: Residuals are asymmetric.

Diagnostic Plot 3: Residuals vs. Leverage

Purpose: Identify influential observations.

Leverage: Measures how extreme a point’s x-values are. A point has high leverage if its x-values are far from x̄.

Influence: Measures how much a point affects the regression line. Influence = Leverage × Residual. A point is influential if it has both high leverage and a large residual.

Cook’s Distance: Quantifies influence. Dᵢ > 1 suggests observation i is highly influential and deserves investigation.

What to do: If a point has Dᵢ > 1:

  1. Check for data entry errors

  2. Investigate whether the observation is genuinely different

  3. Fit the model with and without the point—if results change dramatically, report both

Do not automatically delete influential points. They may be the most informative observations.

X. Multicollinearity: When Predictors Are Redundant

The Problem

Suppose x₁ and x₂ are highly correlated (r > 0.9). Then the model:

y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + ε

faces a logical indeterminacy: many combinations of β₁ and β₂ fit the data equally well. If x₁ ≈ x₂, then β₁ = 0.5, β₂ = 0.5 produces nearly the same predictions as β₁ = 1, β₂ = 0.

Consequences

  1. Standard errors inflate: SE(β̂₁) and SE(β̂₂) become very large

  2. Coefficients become unstable: Adding or removing one observation drastically changes β̂₁ and β̂₂

  3. Individual t-tests fail: Both p-values may be > 0.05, even though the combination of x₁ and x₂ predicts y well (F-test is significant)

Detection

Compute Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) for each predictor:

VIF(x₁) = 1 / (1 - R²₁)

Where R²₁ is the R² from regressing x₁ on all other predictors. VIF measures how much the variance of β̂₁ is inflated by correlation with other predictors.

Rule of thumb: VIF > 10 indicates severe multicollinearity.

Solutions

  1. Drop one predictor: If x₁ and x₂ are nearly identical, remove one

  2. Combine predictors: Create x₃ = x₁ + x₂ (a composite index)

  3. Regularization: Use ridge regression or lasso, which penalize large coefficients

XI. Interactions: When Effects Depend on Context

The Model

y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + β₃(x₁ · x₂) + ε

The interaction term x₁ · x₂ allows the effect of x₁ on y to depend on the value of x₂.

Interpretation

Rewrite the model:

y = β₀ + (β₁ + β₃x₂)x₁ + β₂x₂ + ε

The slope of x₁ is now β₁ + β₃x₂, not a constant. When x₂ increases by 1, the slope of x₁ increases by β₃.

Example: TV × Radio Interaction

From the notebook:

Sales = β₀ + β₁(TV) + β₂(Radio) + β₃(TV · Radio) + ε

Results:

  • β̂₁ = 0.019

  • β̂₂ = 0.029

  • β̂₃ = 0.0011

Interpretation: Each $1,000 in TV spending increases sales by:

19 + 1.1 × Radio (in thousands)

If Radio = $0, TV effect = 19 units per $1,000. If Radio = $20,000, TV effect = 19 + 1.1 × 20 = 41 units per $1,000.

Synergy: The marginal return to TV spending increases when Radio spending is high. This is why R² jumps from 89.7% (additive model) to 96.8% (interaction model). The interaction term explains 69% of the remaining unexplained variance.

XII. Categorical Predictors: Dummy Variables

The Problem

Suppose you want to include Gender (Male/Female) as a predictor. You cannot enter text into a regression equation. You must encode categories numerically.

Dummy Variable Encoding

Create a binary variable:

Female = 1 if Gender = Female, 0 if Gender = Male

The model becomes:

Balance = β₀ + β₁(Female) + ε

For males (Female = 0): E[Balance] = β₀

For females (Female = 1): E[Balance] = β₀ + β₁

β₁ measures the difference in average balance between females and males.

Multiple Categories

For Ethnicity (Asian, Caucasian, African American), create two dummy variables:

  • Asian = 1 if ethnicity is Asian, 0 otherwise

  • Caucasian = 1 if ethnicity is Caucasian, 0 otherwise

The model:

Balance = β₀ + β₁(Asian) + β₂(Caucasian) + ε

For African Americans (both dummies = 0): E[Balance] = β₀ (baseline)

For Asians: E[Balance] = β₀ + β₁

For Caucasians: E[Balance] = β₀ + β₂

Why not three dummy variables? Perfect multicollinearity. If you know two dummies, you know the third. Always use k - 1 dummies for k categories.

XIII. Model Selection: Choosing Predictors

The Problem

With p potential predictors, there are 2^p possible models. For p = 10, that’s 1,024 models. For p = 40, over one trillion. You cannot fit them all.

Forward Selection

  1. Start with the null model (intercept only)

  2. Fit p simple regressions, one for each predictor

  3. Add the predictor with the lowest p-value (or highest R²)

  4. Fit all two-predictor models that include the chosen predictor

  5. Add the predictor that most improves the model

  6. Repeat until no remaining predictor has p < 0.05

Backward Selection

  1. Start with all p predictors

  2. Remove the predictor with the largest p-value (weakest evidence)

  3. Refit with p - 1 predictors

  4. Remove the predictor with the largest p-value

  5. Repeat until all remaining predictors have p < 0.05

Why These Methods Fail

Both are greedy algorithms—they make locally optimal choices without considering global interactions. A predictor weak alone may be strong in combination with others.

Better Criteria

  • Adjusted R²: Penalizes model complexity

  • AIC (Akaike Information Criterion): Balances fit and parsimony

  • BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion): Penalizes complexity more heavily than AIC

  • Cross-validation: Tests predictive accuracy on held-out data

XIV. Cross-Validation: Testing Predictive Accuracy

The Problem

R² measures fit to training data. But we care about fit to new data. A model can fit training data perfectly (R² = 1) and predict new data terribly (overfitting).

k-Fold Cross-Validation

  1. Randomly partition data into k equal-sized folds (typically k = 5 or 10)

  2. For each fold i:

    • Train the model on the other k - 1 folds

    • Predict on fold i

    • Compute prediction error (e.g., mean squared error)

  3. Average the k prediction errors

This estimates how well the model predicts observations it hasn’t seen.

Why It Works

Cross-validation simulates the process of fitting a model to current data and predicting future data. If CV error is much larger than training error, the model is overfit.

Example: Suppose training R² = 0.95 but CV R² = 0.60. The model has learned noise specific to the training set, not generalizable patterns.

XV. When the Framework Collapses

Anscombe’s Quartet

Four datasets with identical:

  • Mean of x and y

  • Variance of x and y

  • Correlation (r = 0.816)

  • Regression line (y = 3 + 0.5x)

  • R² = 0.67

Yet the relationships are completely different:

  1. Linear relationship (regression appropriate)

  2. Non-linear relationship (should use polynomial)

  3. Linear except one outlier (outlier drives the fit)

  4. Vertical data with one leverage point (relationship is an artifact)

Lesson: Summary statistics conceal structure. Always plot the data.

The Mosteller-Tukey Warning

“A regression coefficient estimates the expected change in y per unit change in x, with all other predictors held fixed. But predictors usually change together!”

Example: Predict number of tackles by a football player from weight (W) and height (H).

Model: Tackles = β₀ + 0.50W - 0.10H + ε

The negative coefficient for height seems absurd—taller players make fewer tackles? No. Weight and height are positively correlated. When both are in the model, height’s coefficient reflects: “For a given weight, does extra height help?” And the answer is no—a 250-pound player who is 6’0” makes more tackles than a 250-pound player who is 6’6”, because the shorter player is more solidly built.

Lesson: Coefficients are conditional effects, not marginal effects. They answer hypothetical questions (”what if we could change x while freezing everything else?”) that may be physically impossible.

XVI. Two Fundamental Limits

George Box

“Essentially, all models are wrong, but some are useful.”

Linear regression assumes relationships are linear, errors are normal, variance is constant, and predictors are independent. These are never exactly true. The question is: how wrong is the model, and does it matter for your purpose?

A model with R² = 0.40 is “wrong”—it misses 60% of the variance. But if it identifies the direction of an effect reliably, it may be useful.

Mosteller and Tukey

“The only way to find out what will happen when a complex system is disturbed is to disturb the system, not merely to observe it passively.”

Regression on observational data cannot establish causation. It can only identify conditional associations. To know whether increasing TV advertising causes sales to rise (rather than both being driven by seasonal demand), you must run an experiment: randomize TV spending and measure the effect.

Without randomization, regression coefficients estimate what varies together, not what causes what.

Conclusion: What You’ve Proven

You now understand:

  1. The geometric problem: Least squares finds the line that minimizes squared vertical distances—a logical solution to a geometric optimization

  2. Where estimators come from: β̂₀ and β̂₁ are derived from calculus, not convention

  3. What standard errors mean: They quantify the sampling variability of your estimates under specific assumptions

  4. What hypothesis tests test: Whether observed slopes are distinguishable from noise

  5. What R² measures: Proportion of variance explained—a descriptive statistic, not a causal claim

  6. What residual plots reveal: Violations of assumptions that summary statistics cannot detect

  7. When the model fails: Multicollinearity, interactions, outliers, and non-linearity break the linear framework in predictable ways

Linear regression is not a magic box. It is a logical structure built on four assumptions. When those assumptions hold approximately, the framework is extraordinarily powerful. When they fail, every conclusion is suspect.

The only honest approach: Check your assumptions. Plot your residuals. Acknowledge your uncertainty. And never claim causation from correlation alone.
