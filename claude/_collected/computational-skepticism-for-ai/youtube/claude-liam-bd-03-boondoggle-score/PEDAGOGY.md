# PEDAGOGY — claude-liam-bd-03-boondoggle-score

## Act structure
7 beats (~300s). Concept-and-application episode (E3/4). Cold open (B00) names the Boondoggle Score's central question ("which work is the human's?") and flags the Prompt D anchor as the episode's output. B01 defines a complete score by its four required structural elements with specific failure modes for each. B02 introduces the solve-verify asymmetry — the course's central concept — with a concrete drug-interaction example and distinguishes "most complex Claude task" from "most expensive human verification." B03 teaches score-reading in three steps: distribution table, highest-risk handoffs, three-question checklist per handoff. BVDT consolidates all four elements plus the asymmetry and names the deliverable. BHTF reads the full /claude prompt aloud and explicitly tasks the student with identifying the Prompt D anchor. BOUT closes.

## Cold open law
Opens on the specific question the Boondoggle Score answers ("which work is the human's?") and names the learning outcome in one sentence: "before you analyze it, learn to read it." The Prompt D anchor is introduced by name so the student knows what to look for throughout the episode.

## Gap formula
Gap: students have an SDD but no framework for what a complete score contains, no conceptual reason why human steps land where they do, and no method for identifying the highest-value verification step. Bridge: four-element completeness checklist + solve-verify asymmetry + three-question reading protocol. Close: complete score in hand and a single named Prompt D anchor.

## Utility lint
The four structural elements of a complete score are enumerated, each with a specific failure mode. The solve-verify asymmetry has a concrete example (drug interaction list) and a direct implication for Prompt D. The three-question handoff checklist is binary per question. The "notice that incompleteness" line is load-bearing: it tells students the skill is not fixing the incompleteness — it is seeing it.

## Vocabulary / register
"Boondoggle Score" defined in B00 as the tool that answers "which work is the human's?" "Supervisory capacity" defined in B01 by example (Domain Verification, Ethical Judgment, Contextual Interpretation, Procedural Oversight, Adversarial Reasoning). "Testable handoff condition" defined in B01 with the exact format: 'Claude delivers X; human accepts when Y, escalates when Z.' "Solve-verify asymmetry" introduced in B02 as "the concept the whole course turns on" — flagged explicitly so students know its weight.

## Honesty
The solve-verify asymmetry claim is accurate: generative AI systems do have asymmetric production/verification costs and that asymmetry is a well-documented failure mode in deployed AI systems. The drug-interaction example is chosen because it is a domain where verification cost is obviously high and causally connected to outcome severity. The "notice the incompleteness" framing is honest about what the assignment is measuring: not remediation, but recognition.

## Length law

No B04 needed; content fits in three body beats. BHTF is the longest beat (65s) because the prompt is specific and the Prompt D anchor instruction needs to be stated clearly.
