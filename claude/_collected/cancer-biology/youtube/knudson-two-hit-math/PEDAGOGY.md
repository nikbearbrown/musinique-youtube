# PEDAGOGY — knudson-two-hit-math

## Learning objective
Students understand the two-hit hypothesis as a statistical inference from population data, can name five syndromes confirmed at the molecular level, and explain why PTEN haploinsufficiency refines rather than undermines the model.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, genetics trainees, clinical geneticists.

## Core concept
Knudson's insight was that the age-of-onset distribution of hereditary versus sporadic retinoblastoma followed different statistical laws. Hereditary cases had an earlier onset and required one inherited event plus one somatic event. Sporadic cases required two somatic events. He modeled this with Poisson statistics and predicted: two hits in the same gene. The gene did not need to be known. The logic was in the epidemiology.

## Pedagogical approach
- The 16-year gap is the hook: math predicted the molecule
- Reconstruction of the argument makes the logic transparent
- Five confirmations show it is not a one-off
- PTEN haploinsufficiency as a refinement, not a refutation

## Key takeaway
Mathematical epidemiology can produce mechanistic predictions. The two-hit model is a statistical argument, not a mechanistic one — and its success demonstrates the power of population-level reasoning.

## Misconception addressed
Students sometimes think that without molecular tools, no biological prediction is possible. This reel shows that distributional reasoning in epidemiology predicted a molecular result that took 16 years to confirm.
