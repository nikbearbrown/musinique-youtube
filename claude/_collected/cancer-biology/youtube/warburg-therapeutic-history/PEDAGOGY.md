# PEDAGOGY — warburg-therapeutic-history

## Learning objective
Students understand why the Warburg effect, despite being a universal cancer phenotype, has been a poor therapeutic target, and why IDH inhibitors succeed where broad metabolic targeting fails.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, metabolism researchers, clinical oncology trainees.

## Core concept
The Warburg effect is aerobic glycolysis — cancer cells preferentially ferment glucose even in the presence of oxygen. This is real, measurable by PET scan, and universal. But universality is the problem: the therapeutic window depends on selectivity between tumor and normal tissue. Bone marrow and gut epithelium also proliferate rapidly and also use glycolysis. IDH inhibitors succeed because they target a tumor-specific metabolic defect (the IDH mutation produces 2HG, which only IDH-mutant tumors have) — not the universal Warburg phenotype.

## Pedagogical approach
- Historical survey of failed attempts grounds the problem
- IDH inhibitors as the exception-that-proves-the-rule is the key teaching moment
- Timeline visualization makes the pattern of failure and exception visible

## Key takeaway
Therapeutic success in cancer metabolism requires a tumor-specific metabolic vulnerability — not a universal cancer phenotype. The Warburg effect is real; targeting it broadly does not work.

## Misconception addressed
Students sometimes assume that because cancer cells use more glucose, blocking glucose would selectively kill them. This reel shows why that assumption fails in practice.
