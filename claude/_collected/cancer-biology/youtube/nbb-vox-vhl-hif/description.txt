When a cell runs out of oxygen, it fires an emergency program. But what happens when that emergency program runs — permanently — in a cell with plenty of oxygen?

Clear cell kidney cancer (ccRCC) behaves as if it is starving for oxygen even in regions with adequate oxygen partial pressure. The culprit is VHL — the Von Hippel-Lindau protein — lost in nearly all ccRCC cases through deletion, mutation, or methylation. VHL is not the oxygen sensor itself; it is the reader. Prolyl hydroxylases (PHDs) tag HIF-1α on schedule when oxygen is present. VHL binds that hydroxylation tag and recruits the ubiquitin ligase machinery that commits HIF-1α to proteasomal destruction. Lose VHL and the tag is placed correctly — but the destruction order never fires. HIF-1α accumulates constitutively and drives the full hypoxic transcriptional program: GLUT1, glycolytic enzymes, VEGF, and everything downstream. One missing protein. The whole program, permanently on. A broken off-switch, not a false alarm.
