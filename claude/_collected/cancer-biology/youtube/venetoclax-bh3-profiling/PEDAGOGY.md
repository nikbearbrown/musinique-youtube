# PEDAGOGY — venetoclax-bh3-profiling

## Learning objective
Students distinguish predictive biomarkers (predict response to specific therapy) from prognostic biomarkers (predict outcome regardless of therapy), and understand why a scientifically validated assay may not be in clinical use.

## Target audience
Oncology students, hematology trainees, clinical pharmacology students.

## Core concept
BH3 profiling measures "mitochondrial priming" — how close a cell is to the apoptotic threshold. Cells that are highly primed respond to BCL-2 inhibition with venetoclax; unprimed cells do not. The assay is a direct functional test of what the drug is targeting. The barrier to clinical use is not scientific; it is logistical.

## Pedagogical approach
- Lead with the clinical question: why can't we predict venetoclax response?
- Introduce BH3 profiling as the scientific answer
- Audit the barriers systematically using Claude
- MCL-1 comparison shows the gap between scientific knowledge and clinical tools

## Key takeaway
Scientific validity and clinical implementation are two different problems. BH3 profiling solves the scientific problem. The implementation problem (logistics, infrastructure) is what prevents standard-of-care adoption.

## Misconception addressed
Students often assume that if an assay works scientifically, it will be adopted clinically. This reel shows the gap: fresh cells, specialized equipment, and rapid turnaround are real barriers independent of the science.
