## Claim-by-Claim Verification

### B01 — Cold open narration
**Claim:** A melanoma cell keeps dividing when BRAF is blocked, and when a second pathway is blocked.
- Verdict: ✓ (editorial framing of the drug resistance observation; the chapter opens with exactly this clinical scenario — "the cancer did not become resistant by evading the drug. It became resistant by changing what it depends on.")
- Source: Chapter 12, opening paragraph.

### B02 — BRAF V600E prevalence
**Claim:** "BRAF V600E is the mutation driving half of all cutaneous melanomas."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "BRAF V600E is the most common mutation in melanoma — present in roughly half of cutaneous melanomas."
- **Claim:** Vemurafenib produces "dramatic tumor shrinkage within weeks."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "BRAF inhibitor monotherapy produces dramatic initial responses in BRAF V600E melanoma — tumor regressions visible within weeks."
- Note: The drug name is spelled "vemurafenib" (not "venurafenib" in narration draft — fix: narration has a typo in planning notes, verified correct spelling is vemurafenib; the audio script will use correct spelling).

### B03 — The Question card
**Claim (card copy):** "BRAF inhibition should shut down the proliferative signal in a BRAF-mutant melanoma. Here BRAF is blocked, yet the cancer continues dividing. Why?"
- Verdict: ✓ — This is the card's stated question, verbatim from candidate 10. Factually accurate framing.

### B05 — Rb holds E2F
**Claim:** "At the boundary between G1 and S phase sits one protein: Rb. When Rb is active, it grips E2F, the transcription factor that opens the door to DNA replication. No E2F, no S phase."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "The Rb–E2F switch: Rb holds E2F inactive until growth signals activate cyclin D–CDK4/6 to phosphorylate Rb and release it." Chapter 11 (prior chapter, referenced): E2F drives S-phase gene transcription.
- Source: Chapter 12, "Six ways to force the gate open."

### B06 — Normal opening signal
**Claim:** "Growth signals phosphorylate Rb via a kinase pair: CDK4 and CDK6, each requiring a cyclin D partner."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "cyclin D–CDK4/6 to phosphorylate Rb." CDK4 and CDK6 are named throughout; cyclin D is the required partner.
- **Claim:** "Phosphorylated Rb releases E2F. E2F drives S-phase gene transcription. The cell copies its DNA."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "cyclin D–CDK4/6 to phosphorylate Rb and release it" → E2F drives S-phase entry. Causal chain is accurate.

### B07 — Section card
No factual claims. Editorial framing only. ✓

### B08 — Six lesions
**Claim (all six mechanisms):**
1. "Rb deleted: the gate is simply gone — E2F is free from the start."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Delete or mutate RB1 and there is no gate to hold. E2F is free from birth."
2. "p16 lost: CDK4 and CDK6 run without a brake, phosphorylating Rb constantly."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "p16 (encoded by CDKN2A) is a CDK inhibitor that binds CDK4 and CDK6 and prevents them from phosphorylating Rb. Without p16, CDK4/6 run constitutively."
3. "Cyclin D amplified: too much kinase complex, overwhelming whatever p16 remains."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "The effect is the same: too much kinase complex, not enough brake, Rb gets phosphorylated." (translocation t(11;14) in mantle cell lymphoma, CCND1 amplification in breast/head-and-neck cited.)
4. "CDK4 or CDK6 amplified."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "CDK4 amplification occurs in well-differentiated liposarcoma and some gliomas; CDK6 amplification in T-cell leukemias and certain lymphomas."
5. "Activating CDK4 mutation that blocks p16 binding."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "the R24C substitution in melanoma is the canonical example — change the kinase structure so that p16 can no longer bind."
6. "Hyperactive upstream signaling — BRAF, KRAS, PI3K — that drives cyclin D continuously."
   - Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "constitutively active Ras–MAPK (from KRAS or BRAF mutations), PI3K–AKT (from PIK3CA mutations or PTEN loss), or Wnt (from APC loss) — drives cyclin D transcription and protein stabilization continuously."

**Claim (graphic label):** "E2F free / S phase" as the single convergent endpoint.
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Six distinct molecular lesions produce this same endpoint."

### B09 — Selection pressure
**Claim:** "Six distinct molecular lesions all force the same result: Rb phosphorylated, E2F free, S phase entered without a growth signal."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Six distinct mechanisms, one net effect: the Rb–E2F gate is permanently open. The convergence is not coincidental — it reflects selection pressure."
- **Claim:** "Natural selection in the tumor finds every molecular path that leads there."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Natural selection in the tumor picks for the gate-open state; there are many molecular paths to get there."

### B10 — Three inputs in melanoma
**Claim:** "BRAF V600E melanomas typically carry three simultaneous alterations at the Rb gate."
- Verdict: ✓ (minor simplification) — Chapter 12: "most BRAF V600E melanomas also carry CDKN2A deletion (p16 loss), and many carry PTEN loss." "Typically" overstates the certainty slightly; "often" or "many" would be more precise. However, the chapter's phrasing "also carry" and "many carry" supports this as a common co-occurrence. Labeled as the illustrative case.
- **Claim:** "BRAF V600E activates MAPK, which drives cyclin D1 transcription continuously."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "BRAF V600E phosphorylates MEK, MEK phosphorylates ERK, ERK enters the nucleus and transcribes cyclin D1."
- **Claim:** "p16 and CDKN2A are deleted in fifty to seventy percent of melanomas."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "CDKN2A deletion or methylation silencing occurs in roughly 30 percent of all cancers and far higher fractions of melanoma (50–70 percent)."
- **Claim:** "PTEN loss activates PI3K and AKT, which stabilizes cyclin D1 protein — a second upstream input through a different pathway."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "PTEN loss activating the PI3K–AKT pathway, which also stabilizes cyclin D1 and drives Rb phosphorylation through a parallel route."

### B11 — BRAF block fails
**Claim:** "Block BRAF alone. The MAPK-driven cyclin D1 falls. But p16 loss still lets CDK4 and CDK6 run without a brake — and PTEN loss still keeps AKT stabilizing whatever cyclin D1 remains."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Even when BRAF is successfully blocked, p16 loss and PI3K activation are still forcing CDK4/6 to run and driving Rb phosphorylation through AKT-stabilized cyclin D. The BRAF inhibitor addresses one of three converging perturbations on the same gate."
- **Claim:** "Two of the three cables are still plugged in."
- Verdict: ✓ — editorial metaphor accurately representing the mechanism; labeled illustrative.

### B12 — Junction still active
**Claim:** "Blocking one input in a convergent pathway does not close a gate that has two other active inputs."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "a drug that blocks one node in a convergent pathway achieves complete arrest only if it is the rate-limiting node and there are no bypasses."
- **Claim:** "The cell doesn't need BRAF. It has p16 loss and PTEN loss doing the same job."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "the BRAF inhibitor addresses one of three converging perturbations on the same gate."

### B13 — CDK4/6 inhibitor example (ILLUSTRATIVE)
**Claim:** "Block BRAF: MAPK-driven cyclin D1 drops. But CDK4 and CDK6 are running without p16 — they keep phosphorylating Rb. And AKT is still stabilizing whatever cyclin D1 remains from other sources."
- Verdict: ✓ — mechanism accurate per chapter.
- **Claim:** "Add a CDK4/6 inhibitor. All three inputs converge at that one kinase. The junction box goes dark. Rb stays unphosphorylated. E2F stays bound. S phase is arrested."
- Verdict: ✓ (illustrative) — Chapter 12: "Add a CDK4/6 inhibitor and you address the convergence point itself — the kinase all three pathways are feeding." S-phase arrest follows from Rb held hypophosphorylated and E2F restrained.
- **Illustrative note:** "Illustrative pathway, real mechanism" — labeled in narration.
- Verdict: appropriate disclaimer. ✓

### B14 — Rational combination
**Claim:** "Rational combination therapy means reading the pathway before writing the prescription. Which inputs are active? Which convergence point do they all share? Block at the convergence, not just at one upstream driver. Each drug added to the combination must close a specific residual active input."
- Verdict: ✓ — Chapter 12: "Each additional drug is justified by a specific residual mechanism that the previous combination leaves active. This is what combination-therapy reasoning looks like when it is mechanistically grounded rather than empirically assembled."

### B15 — Recap endcard
**Claim:** "Why does blocking BRAF fail to stop a BRAF-mutant cancer? Because p16 loss and PTEN loss give the Rb gate two other active inputs — and blocking one upstream driver cannot close a gate that two other pathways are keeping open."
- Verdict: ✓ — Accurate summary of the chapter's mechanism and the film's argument. Matches the card's question from B03.
