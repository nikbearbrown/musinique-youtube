# PEDAGOGY — hallmarks-2022-debate

## Learning objective
Students can distinguish mechanistic hallmarks supported by experimental evidence from descriptive categories supported by correlation, using the Bradford Hill experiment criterion as the dividing line.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, biomedical researchers, clinicians reading oncology literature.

## Core concept
The Hallmarks of Cancer framework is a powerful organizing tool, but its 2022 additions reveal a tension: as a framework grows to absorb anomalies, it risks becoming unfalsifiable. The 2022 additions are plausible and useful for research organization, but they lack the mechanistic experimental foundation of the original six.

## Pedagogical approach
- Lead with the counterintuitive: a growing framework is not necessarily a stronger one
- Use Claude as a research tool to surface the primary literature debate
- Demonstrate the difference between "observational support" and "mechanistic proof"
- The experiment column forces students to think about what would actually resolve the question

## Key takeaway
The question "what experiment would settle this?" is the most important question in evaluating any scientific framework addition. If no one has run that experiment, the addition is a hypothesis.

## Misconception addressed
Students often treat the Hallmarks framework as settled science. This reel shows that even a landmark framework has contested additions, and that critical reading of primary literature reveals the hedging language the authors themselves use.
