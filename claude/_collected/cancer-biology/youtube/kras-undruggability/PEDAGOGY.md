# PEDAGOGY — kras-undruggability

## Learning objective
Students understand why KRAS was classified undruggable for 40 years, what chemical insight enabled G12C inhibition, why G12D requires a different strategy, and why variant-specific testing is required before any KRAS-targeted therapy.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, oncology trainees, drug discovery researchers.

## Core concept
KRAS is undruggable because: (1) it has picomolar affinity for GTP/GDP — no drug can outcompete this, (2) it has a featureless surface with no obvious binding pocket in the active state, (3) it lacks enzymatic activity that could be competitively inhibited. The G12C breakthrough came from a covalent approach targeting a specific cysteine in the switch-II pocket that is only accessible when the protein is GDP-bound (inactive state). G12D replaces the glycine with aspartate — no cysteine, no covalent handle.

## Pedagogical approach
- The 40-year historical sweep is the hook: this is the most common oncogene and it took four decades
- Chemistry-first explanation: the mutation created the drug target, not the biological importance
- G12D comparison makes the accidental nature of G12C's druggability explicit

## Key takeaway
Druggability is a chemical property, not a biological importance ranking. The most clinically important KRAS variant (G12D in pancreatic cancer) is harder to drug than a less common variant (G12C) because of a single nucleotide change that created a chemical handle.

## Misconception addressed
Students often assume the most common cancer mutation would be the easiest to drug. This reel reverses that: the most common variant in pancreatic cancer (G12D) has no approved drug, while a less common variant (G12C) has two.
