# PEDAGOGY — idh-epigenetic-reversal

## Learning objective
Students can trace the complete causal chain from IDH mutation to differentiation block, explain why removing the oncometabolite (not the methylation) is the therapeutic strategy, and identify the clinical evidence confirming the epigenetic mechanism.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, hematology/oncology trainees, epigenetics researchers.

## Core concept
IDH1/2 mutations produce 2-hydroxyglutarate (2HG), which competitively inhibits alpha-ketoglutarate-dependent enzymes including TET2 (DNA demethylase) and KDM histone demethylases. Without active demethylases, methylation marks accumulate on differentiation gene promoters. Blasts cannot read the maturation program. Ivosidenib blocks mutant IDH1, removes the source of 2HG, and the cell's own demethylases restore the epigenome. The key insight: the drug removes the cause, not the effect.

## Pedagogical approach
- Five-node causal chain is the structural backbone: every step has a primary citation
- Contrast with demethylating agents (azacitidine): removes symptoms, not cause
- Differentiation syndrome as clinical confirmation of mechanism (blasts differentiating causes inflammatory response)
- SDH/FH comparison shows generalizability of the oncometabolite concept

## Key takeaway
Epigenetics is pharmacologically reversible when the cause of the methylation is druggable. The IDH/2HG axis is the proof-of-concept. The same logic may apply to other oncometabolites but the clinical evidence is much earlier.

## Misconception addressed
Students often think that demethylating agents (azacitidine, decitabine) work by the same mechanism as IDH inhibitors. This reel makes the distinction clear: global demethylation vs. cause removal.
