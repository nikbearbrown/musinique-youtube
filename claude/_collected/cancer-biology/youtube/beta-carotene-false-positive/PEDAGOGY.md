# PEDAGOGY — beta-carotene-false-positive

## Learning objective
Students understand why consistent, plausible, strong observational associations in cancer epidemiology can be reversed by randomized trials, and apply the Bradford Hill experiment criterion as the decisive test for causality.

## Target audience
Cancer biology students, epidemiology students, public health trainees, anyone reading cancer prevention literature.

## Core concept
Confounding in observational epidemiology can produce any of the Bradford Hill criteria: strong associations, consistent replications, plausible biological mechanisms, dose-response gradients. The only criterion confounding cannot mimic is a well-designed randomized experiment. When the experiment runs, it cuts through all the confounding at once. Beta-carotene in food is a marker of healthy diet; it is not the active protective agent. Supplements deliver beta-carotene without the rest of the healthy diet pattern.

## Pedagogical approach
- Bradford Hill checklist makes the structure of epidemiological reasoning explicit
- Appearing criteria one-by-one shows that five criteria were satisfied — building false confidence
- The Experiment criterion appearing last and reversed is the visual and pedagogical climax
- Vitamin D repetition of the pattern shows this is a structural problem, not a unique error

## Key takeaway
An observational association is a hypothesis, not evidence for causation. The experiment criterion exists for exactly this reason. Before changing behavior based on epidemiological data, find the randomized trial.

## Misconception addressed
Students often trust consistent, replicated, biologically plausible observational data. This reel shows that all those properties can be produced by confounding. The experiment is the only solution.
