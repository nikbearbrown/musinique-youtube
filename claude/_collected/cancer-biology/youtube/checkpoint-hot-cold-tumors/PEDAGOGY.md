# PEDAGOGY — checkpoint-hot-cold-tumors

## Learning objective
Students can rank six cancer types by checkpoint immunotherapy response rate, explain why hot tumors respond and cold tumors do not at the mechanistic level, and identify three independent predictive biomarkers that should be tested before prescribing checkpoint inhibitors.

## Target audience
Oncology trainees, immunology students, cancer biology students.

## Core concept
Hot tumors have: (1) T cells primed against tumor antigens, (2) those T cells present in the tumor, and (3) the only suppression mechanism is PD-1/PD-L1 signaling, which the drug can reverse. Cold tumors may lack any of these conditions. Pancreatic cancer is cold because the desmoplastic stroma physically excludes T cells — no drug that blocks PD-1/PD-L1 can overcome a physical exclusion barrier.

## Pedagogical approach
- Six-cancer bar chart makes the 15-fold gap visually immediate
- Hot/cold/excluded phenotype classification is the key conceptual framework
- Hodgkin lymphoma as the counterintuitive top responder (low TMB, not expected to work by TMB logic) teaches that mechanism matters more than a single biomarker
- Cold-to-hot conversion failure in pancreatic shows the field's limitation as of 2026

## Key takeaway
TMB and PD-L1 are correlates, not universal predictors. The mechanistic question is: are T cells primed, present, and suppressed by a reversible mechanism? Three independent biomarker tests (PD-L1, MSI, TMB) capture non-overlapping subsets of responders.

## Misconception addressed
Students often think high TMB = checkpoint response. Hodgkin lymphoma refutes this: it has low TMB but the highest response rate because the suppression mechanism (PD-L1 amplification) is completely reversible.
