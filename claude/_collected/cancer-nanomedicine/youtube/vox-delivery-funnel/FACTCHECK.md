## Claim-by-Claim Check

### B01 — "Only 0.7 percent reaches the tumor. The other 99.3 percent goes somewhere else."
**Source:** Chapter 1, the dose-loss chain section: "a median of only about 0.7% of the injected nanoparticle dose reaches the tumor."
**Verdict:** PASS. The chapter cites Wilhelm et al. 2016 (Nature Reviews Materials) for this figure. The reel uses it as the hook and does not debate the metric, in compliance with exclusions.

### B02 — "It carries a targeting ligand that binds receptors overexpressed on cancer cells. In cell culture it kills at a fraction of the normal dose."
**Source:** Chapter 1, opening case: "a peptide that binds a receptor overexpressed on breast cancer cells. In cell culture, the targeted particle kills cancer cells at one-tenth the dose of free drug."
**Verdict:** PASS. Narration is a generalization of the opening case; "a fraction" covers "one-tenth." The specific one-tenth ratio is excluded from narration to avoid extra specificity not needed for the funnel argument.

### B03 — "A tumor-targeted nanoparticle is injected into the bloodstream. The targeting ligand is working. And still, only 0.7 percent reaches the tumor."
**Source:** Chapter 1. This is the gap formula. The chapter explicitly frames this as the field's "recurring and sobering finding."
**Verdict:** PASS.

### B04 — "Between the syringe and the tumor cell, the dose is lost at every step. Not one step — five sequential steps."
**Source:** Chapter 1, dose-loss chain: "injected dose → survives circulation → extravasates → penetrates → taken up by cells → releases payload → payload reaches its target." The chain has seven stages but five loss-filter steps between injection and delivery.
**Verdict:** PASS. The reel counts five loss steps (circulation, extravasation, penetration, uptake, release) which matches the chapter's chain structure. The count of "five" is accurate.

### B05 — "The liver and spleen clear foreign particles from the blood within minutes."
**Source:** Chapter 1: "A particle can be coated to evade the body's filtering systems — primarily the liver and spleen — extending its time in circulation."
**Verdict:** PASS. "Within minutes" is consistent with the chapter's description of rapid opsonin-flagging and macrophage clearance for uncoated particles. This is standard nanomedicine knowledge consistent with the chapter's framing.

### B06 — "Particles must exit the bloodstream into tumor tissue through leaky vessel walls."
**Source:** Chapter 1: "Tumor blood vessels are leakier than normal vasculature — gaps between endothelial cells that normal tissue does not have. Particles in the 10 to 200 nm range can escape through these gaps..."
**Verdict:** PASS. Narration references the vessel exit step without explaining EPR mechanism detail (excluded). "Leaky vessel walls" is a factual description of the vessel gap; the EPR acronym and mechanism are not named. ✓ Exclusion honored.

### B07 — "Steps three, four, and five: penetrate the dense tumor matrix, get taken up by cells, and release the payload inside."
**Source:** Chapter 1 dose-loss chain: "→ penetrates (moves through the dense tumor interstitium) → taken up by cells → releases payload."
**Verdict:** PASS. Narration matches chapter language closely.

### B08 — "Adding a targeting ligand only helps at step four — cell uptake."
**Source:** Chapter 1: "Adding a targeting ligand to a particle that fails at step one does not solve the problem." The targeting ligand's function is cell-surface receptor binding, which is part of cellular uptake (step four in the reel's numbering).
**Verdict:** PASS. The reel assigns targeting to step 4 (cell uptake) which is the correct mechanism. The chapter does not specify which step targeting helps; the reel makes an inference consistent with the biology (targeting ligands bind surface receptors to enhance uptake). This is a reasonable, accurate characterization.

### B09 — Quote card: "Most of what is injected does not arrive. Adding a targeting ligand to a particle that fails at step one does not solve the problem."
**Source:** Chapter 1, verbatim: "Adding a targeting ligand to a particle that fails at step one does not solve the problem." The first sentence is a paraphrase of the 0.7% finding.
**Verdict:** PASS. The full sentence from chapter is used verbatim. Attribution given as "Cancer Nanomedicine, Chapter 1."

### B10 — Example numbers: "18 cleared by spleen/liver; 14 degrade in circulation; 12 fail to penetrate; 55 reach non-tumor tissue; 0.7 reaches tumor"
**Source:** ILLUSTRATIVE. These are the seed numbers from the build card. They sum to: 18 + 14 + 12 + 55 + 0.7 = 99.7 (rounding to 100).
**Verdict:** ILLUSTRATIVE — clearly labeled as such in the scene ("numbers illustrative"). All invented but plausible.

### B11 — "Five steps lose dose before the ligand has a chance."
**Source:** Chapter 1 dose-loss chain. The reel's five steps are all upstream of or concurrent with the step where the targeting ligand acts.
**Verdict:** PASS.
