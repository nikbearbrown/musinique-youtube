## Statistics cited on-screen (all trace to this article, none invented)

- One is sampling probability: “given true ratio 50/50, there’s 95% chance my sample will be between 45% and 55% white.” The other is inferential probability: “given my sample was 50% white, there’s 95% chance true ratio is between 45% and 55%.” These aren’t the same.
- A blood test 99% accurate for rare disease—testing positive still means you probably don’t have it, if disease is rare enough.
- A 95% confidence interval doesn’t mean 95% probability true value falls within it—once computed, the interval either contains true value or doesn’t, so there’s no probability to assign.
- The chapter details subsequent replication projects showing failure rates around 50% in psychology, 40% in economics, 90% in preclinical cancer research.
- A 2015 study estimated $28 billion per year wasted on irreproducible research in life sciences alone.
- He begins with Sally Clark, convicted in 1999 based on testimony that two SIDS deaths in one family had odds of 73 million to one against them.
- When Jaynes says the probability of drawing a black ball from an urn containing one black and one white ball is 1/2 (given only that information), it’s not because he measured the frequency or because he feels 50% confident.
- He needs readers to feel the wrongness of convicting Sally Clark, of telling a healthy patient they have cancer because the test is “99% accurate,” of funding research based on p-values that measure the wrong thing.
