## Statistics cited on-screen (all trace to this article, none invented)

- Core Claim: The secretary problem yields a provably optimal algorithm (the “37% rule”) for sequential decision-making under uncertainty: reject the first 37% of options to establish a baseline, then commit to the first option that exceeds all prior observations.
- Lab studies (Rappoport & Seale) showing human performance at ~31% optimal — close but systematically early
- Variants: rejection scenarios (25% threshold), recall scenarios (61% threshold), full-information game (58% success rate vs.
- 37% for no-information)
- The 37% rule assumes a known total pool size, random presentation order, and zero cost of searching — none of which hold in most real decisions.
- Applied contexts: A/B testing (Obama 2008 campaign, $57M additional donations); clinical trials (ECMO adaptive design)
- The Copernican Principle applied to: Berlin Wall (8 years observed → 12 years remaining; actual 20), US nation, corporate lifespans
- Wedding seating as protein placement (Bellos): 11^107 search space; lab cluster ran 36 hours without converging
