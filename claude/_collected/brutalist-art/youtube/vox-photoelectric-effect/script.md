## COLD OPEN — the irony

**B01 · STILL·archive·kenburns** — Hertz, 1887
> In 1887, Heinrich Hertz proved light is a wave — the crowning triumph of
> classical physics. The same year, he noticed something that would destroy
> it.

**B02 · CARD** — title
> Ultraviolet light striking metal knocks out sparks. Simple to see,
> impossible to explain. This is the experiment that broke the wave theory
> of light.

## THE THREE IMPOSSIBLE FACTS

**B03 · STILL·archive·kenburns** — Lenard / early lab
> By 1902, Philipp Lenard had shown the sparks were electrons. By 1914,
> Robert Millikan had measured every feature of them. The data said three
> impossible things.

**B04 · COMPOSITE·archive** — arc lamp plate + threshold annotation
> One: below a threshold frequency, nothing. A blinding red arc lamp frees
> no electrons from sodium. A dim ultraviolet lamp frees them instantly.

**B05 · GRAPHIC·isotype** — more, not faster
> Two: brighter light means more electrons — but not faster ones. Double
> the brightness, and each electron carries exactly the same energy as
> before.

**B06 · STILL·archive·kenburns** — laboratory / electroscope
> Three: no delay. Emission begins within nanoseconds, at any brightness
> above threshold. Even the dimmest lamp never needs time to charge up an
> electron.

**B07 · GRAPHIC** — the wave picture fails all three
> Waves can't do any of this. A wave delivers energy smoothly, everywhere
> at once. Given enough time, any color should shake an electron loose.
> None of it happens.

## THE TURN — Einstein, 1905

**B08 · STILL·archive·kenburns** — Einstein at the patent office
> Enter a twenty-six-year-old patent clerk in Bern, in his miracle year:
> Albert Einstein, taking the step Planck himself refused.

**B09 · GRAPHIC** — light itself comes in packets
> Light energy isn't a smooth wave spread everywhere. It arrives in whole
> packets — Planck's chunks, but now for light itself. He called them light
> quanta. We call them photons.

**B10 · GRAPHIC** — one photon, one electron
> The rules become simple. One photon is absorbed by one electron. Whole
> packet or nothing — a photon cannot pay in installments.

## EQUATION TANGENT — K_max = hν − Φ (zones 2→3→4)

**T01 · GRAPHIC·equation_tangent·sentences**
> One line explains every impossible fact: the electron's energy is the
> photon's payment, minus the exit fee. Physicists call that fee the work
> function.

**T02 · GRAPHIC·equation_tangent·glossary** (spotlight: Φ)
> K max, the fastest electron's energy. H nu, the photon's payment. And
> phi — the work function — what the metal charges to let an electron
> leave.

**T03 · GRAPHIC·equation_tangent·example**
> Ultraviolet on sodium: pay 4.13, fee 2.28 — the electron keeps 1.85
> electron volts. Green light pays 2.27. One hundredth short. Nothing
> escapes. Ever.

## RESOLUTION

**B11 · GRAPHIC** — the three facts fall
> Threshold, explained: below the fee, no photon can pay. Faster electrons
> from bluer light: the payment is set by frequency alone. And no delay:
> one photon, one electron.

**B12 · STILL·archive·kenburns** — Millikan
> Robert Millikan thought photons were absurd. He spent two years trying to
> prove Einstein wrong — freshly scraped metal surfaces, vacuum, exquisite
> care.

**B13 · GRAPHIC·drawon** — the slopes (fig 1.2 idiom)
> Every metal gave the same slope: h over e. Einstein's equation fit
> perfectly. Millikan published his own defeat — "in spite of my personal
> conviction."

**B14 · GRAPHIC** — the Nobel irony
> Einstein's Nobel Prize came in 1921 — for the photoelectric effect, not
> relativity. Millikan's came two years later, for proving the theory he'd
> set out to kill.
