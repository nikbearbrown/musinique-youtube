# PEDAGOGY.md — H3: Claude, Your Quizmaster.

## Evidence Table

| Claim | Source | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Testing yourself beats rereading (the testing effect) | Roediger & Karpicke 2006, "Test-Enhanced Learning," *Psychological Science* | Well-replicated; consensus in cognitive psychology | Foundational finding, multiple independent replications |
| Retrieval practice strengthens memory traces | Roediger & Butler 2011; Kornell & Bjork 2008; extensive meta-analytic support | Consensus | Core mechanism cited without overclaiming |
| Spaced practice outperforms massed practice ("spacing effect") | Ebbinghaus 1885 (original); Cepeda et al. 2006 (meta-analysis); consistent replication | One of the most replicated findings in memory research | Today/+1day/+1week/+1month schedule is illustrative — not a prescription; actual intervals should be calibrated to material difficulty |
| Desirable difficulties — generation effect (having to produce an answer improves retention vs. recognition) | Bjork 1994; Jacoby 1978 (original generation effect) | Well-established | Supports "commit before reveal" claim |
| Explaining a concept back (protégé effect) improves own learning | Chi et al. 1994 ("self-explanation"); Nestojko et al. 2014 ("teaching improves learning") | Well-replicated | Supports the "explain-it-back" move |

### Evidence Integrity Notes

- No single study is load-bearing here. Every claim in H3 rests on consensus-level, replicated cognitive psychology. No "single study — flagged" caveat needed (contrast with H2/Bastani).
- The spaced review schedule (today, +1 day, +1 week, +1 month) is presented as illustrative, not clinically derived. The narration does not claim these are the optimal intervals — only that spacing beats cramming.
- "Retrieval practice" and "testing effect" are standard cognitive science terminology; not overclaimed.
