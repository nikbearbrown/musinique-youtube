| B00 | Nobody can hear your whole catalog at once — not you, not your producer, not the friend who swears track nine is the one. But a machine that reads can hold thirty songs in view and tell you what you keep doing. Today you get an editor for the body of work. |
| B01 | The move is simple and almost nobody does it: paste twenty, thirty songs — lyrics, chord charts, arrangement notes — and ask for the patterns. Not 'is this song good.' What do I overuse? Which images keep returning? Where do my choruses always resolve? Cited song by song, so you can check every claim against your own work. |
| B02 | What comes back — and these are illustrative, yours will differ — looks like: the same weather image in seven songs. The same resolution in eleven choruses. The second verse going abstract nine times out of twelve. None of it is a verdict. All of it is a mirror. A good editor doesn't tell you what to write; they show you what you can't see from inside. |
| B03 | Then flip it from mirror to map: which three threads are worth mining on purpose? The image you touched once and abandoned. The tuning you used on the song people actually finish. This is where the reading pays for itself — the next release built on evidence of your own strengths instead of a guess about the market's. |
| B04 | The boundary, stated plainly, because this channel doesn't oversell: it cannot tell you which song is best. That's taste, and taste stays in the room with you. What it can do is name patterns with citations — the determinable half. You keep the judgeable half. That split is the whole honest deal with these tools, and it never moves. |
| B05 | Housekeeping that matters: this is your unreleased work. Keep the project folder scoped to what the reading needs — lyrics, charts, notes. Financials, contracts, and stems don't belong in scope. The bot that works for you still only gets what the job requires. |
| B06 | One page. An editor across the whole body of work, receipts attached, taste untouched. That's more than most indies have ever had — and it costs you an afternoon of pasting. |
| B07 | Your turn — the afternoon this takes is the best trade in the season. Paste your catalog, hold it to the citation rule, and argue with the mirror. The arguing is where the next record starts. |
