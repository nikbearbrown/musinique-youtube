The Schrödinger equation is one differential equation for all of quantum mechanics. This video shows exactly how separation of variables reduces it to a problem you've already solved — and why the solutions are called "stationary" even though they're constantly spinning in the complex plane.

What you'll learn: A NotebookLM deep dive through the TDSE, the product ansatz that separates it into a trivial time ODE and the spatial eigenvalue problem (TISE), why the separation constant must be real and equal to the energy, what "stationary" actually means for $|\Psi_n|^2$, and how two stationary states superpose to produce a beat-frequency oscillation.

The physics: The TDSE $i\hbar\partial\Psi/\partial t = \hat{H}\Psi$ separates via $\Psi = \psi(x)\phi(t)$ into $\phi(t) = e^{-iEt/\hbar}$ and the eigenvalue problem $\hat{H}\psi = E\psi$. The probability density $|\Psi_n|^2 = |\psi_n(x)|^2$ is time-independent — "stationary" — but the wave function itself rotates at angular frequency $E_n/\hbar$. In a two-state superposition the interference term oscillates at the beat frequency $\omega_{12} = (E_2-E_1)/\hbar$, producing sloshing probability while the energy expectation $\langle\hat{H}\rangle = \sum_n|c_n|^2E_n$ remains exactly constant.
