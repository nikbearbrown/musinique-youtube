**Title:** The Matter Wave Proof: De Broglie, Davisson–Germer, and Wave–Particle Duality

In 1924, de Broglie proposed that electrons have a wavelength. Three years later, a laboratory accident with nickel crystals proved him right — with no adjustable parameters.

**What you'll learn:** A NotebookLM deep dive through de Broglie's symmetry argument ($\lambda = h/p$), the Davisson–Germer diffraction experiment, single-electron double-slit buildup, why a 70 kg person walking at 1 m/s has a de Broglie wavelength of $10^{-35}$ m, and what the wave function actually represents (and does not represent).

**The physics:** De Broglie's relation $\lambda = h/p$ gives electrons at 54 V a wavelength $\lambda = 0.167$ nm — the same scale as nickel's crystal planes ($d = 0.091$ nm). Bragg's law $2d\sin\theta = \lambda$ predicts a diffraction peak at ~50°, matching the Davisson–Germer measurement with no free parameters. A C₆₀ buckyball at 900 K has $\lambda \approx 2.5$ pm — smaller than the molecule itself — yet Zeilinger's group observed clear diffraction fringes in 1999, extending matter-wave behavior to 60-atom objects.
