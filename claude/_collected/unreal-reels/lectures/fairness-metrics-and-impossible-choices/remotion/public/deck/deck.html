AI Engineering · Chapter 07

Fairness metrics: choosing a definition and defending it

Three reasonable definitions. One dataset. Pick one — and learn why you cannot keep all three.
The problem

You built a classifier. You sit down to write “make it fair.”

A yes/no model — loan approval, recidivism, a hiring screen — applied to a population with two groups, A and B. The model predicts; the world produces outcomes; you compare the two.
You write down three requirements. Each one, on its own, sounds exactly like what fairness should mean.
I have to tell you: you cannot satisfy all three at once when the groups have different base rates — and on real data, they almost always do.
The impossibility theorem · 2016

Demographic parity, equalized odds, and calibration parity cannot all hold on the same dataset when base rates differ.

Not a tooling limit. Not a data problem. A structural fact — proved independently by Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan, and by Chouldechova. The impossibility doesn’t let you avoid the question. It forces a choice — and choices in engineering have to be defended.
Part one

Three definitions

Each captures something fairness intuitively requires. Each is a different statement about the model.
Metric 01 · Equation

Demographic parity

also called statistical parity — a statement about the model’s outputs
P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=a) = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=b)
In plain terms
The model says “yes” to the same fraction of group A and group B. Equal treatment in the prediction itself — measured before anyone checks who was actually right.
Values claim
Outcomes should be proportional regardless of base rates. Structural redress over conditional accuracy.
Real-world example

A bank approves loans at 40% in one neighborhood and 18% in another.

Demographic parity says: the loan — the thing with real consequences, a house, a business — should be handed out at the same rate to both groups, full stop. It does not care whether default rates differ underneath. The claim is that access to the resource should not track group membership, even if the historical numbers do.
When you reach for parity, you are betting that the prediction’s payoff matters more than its conditional accuracy. That is a defensible bet — in some deployments. Not all.
Metric 02 · Equation

Equalized odds

a statement about the model’s errors, conditional on the truth
True-positive rate equal
P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1, A=a) = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1, A=b)
False-positive rate equal
P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0, A=a) = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0, A=b)
In plain terms
The model makes its mistakes evenly. Among people who truly are positive, it catches the same share in both groups; among people who truly are negative, it falsely flags the same share.
Values claim
The cost of being wronged should be borne equally. Group membership should not change your risk of error.
Real-world example

A hiring screen rejects qualified candidates twice as often in one group.

Two qualified applicants, equally capable, different groups. If the model’s false-rejection rate is higher for one of them, that group systematically absorbs more of the harm the model causes. Equalized odds says: whatever errors the model makes, distribute them evenly — a false “no” should not be more likely just because of which group you’re in.
This is the metric to reach for when the error itself is the injury — a wrongful arrest flag, a wrongful denial — and you want no group bearing more of it than another.
Metric 03 · Equation

Calibration parity

also called predictive parity — a statement about whether the probabilities are honest
P(Y=1 \mid \hat{P}=p, A=a) = P(Y=1 \mid \hat{P}=p, A=b) = p \quad \forall p
In plain terms
When the model says “70%”, it means a 70% chance — and it means that identically for group A and group B. A score has one meaning, not a group-specific one.
Values claim
If you act on the score, its meaning must not depend on group membership.
Real-world example

A clinician reads “high risk: 0.8” and acts the same way every time.

A hospital’s readmission model outputs a probability. The doctor doesn’t know — or want to know — the patient’s group when they read it. For that to be safe, 0.8 must mean 0.8 for everyone: among all patients scored 0.8, exactly 80% are readmitted, in each group. Calibration parity is what lets a downstream decision-maker treat the number uniformly without secretly needing a group-specific decoder.
Reach for calibration when a human or system consumes the probability directly and must trust that its meaning is stable across groups.
Three metrics · three values claims

Each metric is a different statement — and a different value.

Demographic parity
statistical parity
Equal rate of positive predictions — the visible outputs.
Claim: outcomes proportional, regardless of base rates.
Equalized odds
TPR / FPR parity
Equal false- and true-positive rates — the errors.
Claim: the cost of error should be borne equally.
Calibration parity
predictive parity
A score means the same probability across groups — honest outputs.
Claim: a score’s meaning must not depend on group.
They look compatible. They are not.
Part two

The arithmetic

The impossibility is not something you take on authority. It becomes obvious once you see the numbers.
The setup

One number does all the damage: the base rate.

Group A
0.6
60% truly positive
Group B
0.3
30% truly positive
The classifier is calibrated — among everyone it scores at probability p̂, the realized positive rate really is p̂, in both groups. Now watch what calibration forces.
Equation · precision

Among those flagged positive, how many are right?

\text{PPV} = \frac{p \cdot \text{TPR}}{p \cdot \text{TPR} + (1-p)\cdot \text{FPR}}
In plain terms
Precision depends on the base rate p. Where positives are common (A, p=0.6), a flag is more likely to be correct. Where positives are rare (B, p=0.3), the same flag is more likely a false alarm.
Why it bites
To hold PPV equal across groups with different p, the ratio TPR/FPR must differ — which breaks equalized odds.
Real-world example

The same smoke alarm is “more trustworthy” in the kitchen than the bedroom.

One detector, one sensitivity. Put it in a kitchen, where fires are relatively common, and most of its alarms are real. Put the identical detector in a bedroom, where fires are rare, and most of its alarms are false — even though the device hasn’t changed at all. Prevalence, not the device, sets how often a positive is correct.
That is exactly why a calibrated classifier with different base rates cannot also equalize its error rates. The math you just saw is the smoke alarm.
Equation · the inescapable constraint

Chouldechova’s identity — straight from Bayes

\frac{v}{1-v} = \frac{p}{1-p}\cdot \frac{t}{f}
equal PPV across groups, with different base rates, requires…
\frac{t_a}{f_a} = \frac{p_b(1-p_a)}{p_a(1-p_b)}\cdot \frac{t_b}{f_b}
In plain terms
Here v = precision, t = TPR, f = FPR, p = base rate. Unless pa = pb, the ratio t/f must differ across groups. But equalized odds demands ta=tb and fa=fb — forcing t/f equal. You cannot have both. One of them has to give.
The theorem, drawn

Satisfy any two. The third breaks.

The trade-off, in bars

Same model. Different threshold. You choose what breaks.

Part three

The COMPAS case

Broward County, 2016. The theorem, in public, with real consequences.
A risk score that decided bail and sentencing

Two true claims. One values disagreement.

ProPublica
Black defendants who were not re-arrested were flagged high-risk about twice as often as white defendants who weren’t.
→ equalized odds violated
Northpointe
Within each risk score, the actual re-arrest rate was about the same across races. A 7 meant a 7 for everyone.
→ calibration parity satisfied
Base rates of re-arrest differed in the data. The theorem says: given that, you cannot have both. They weren’t having a factual fight more data could settle — they were disagreeing about which definition of fairness should win.
Both pictures are true at the same time

The disagreement was never about the numbers.

The lesson

Each metric is a coherent value. None is the obvious right one.

The choice depends on the deployment: who uses the prediction, what decisions they make, who bears the cost of error, and whether the base-rate difference is itself something you have a stance on. The math cannot answer that for you.
Part four

Beyond groups:
individual fairness

A model can satisfy demographic parity while treating two nearly-identical people completely differently. Similar individuals should be treated similarly.
Equation · Dwork et al. 2012

The (D, d)-Lipschitz condition

D\big(M(x), M(y)\big) \leq d(x, y) \quad \forall\, x, y \in \mathcal{X}
In plain terms
How differently the model treats two people (left) can be no bigger than how different the two people are (right). D measures distance between outputs; d measures distance between individuals.
The whole game is d
Who counts as “similar” for this task? That choice isn’t in the math — it precedes the math, and it’s a values decision.
Real-world example

Two applicants, identical finances, different ZIP codes.

If d ignores ZIP, the model must treat them alike. If d encodes ZIP distance, different treatment is permitted — and structural inequity slips into the fairness condition itself.
A tempting shortcut that doesn’t work

“Just delete the sensitive attribute.”

Fairness through unawareness
Drop race, gender, etc. from the model. Intuitive — and mathematically weak. ZIP code, surname, occupation are proxies that reconstruct the attribute. Omit race, keep ZIP, and you may still use race.
Fairness through awareness
Reason explicitly about the attribute — how much it enters d. Auditing means computing d and checking the Lipschitz condition, not confirming a column was deleted.
Takeaway: unawareness is not a fairness guarantee. It is a documentation choice.
Part five

Causal fairness

Statistical metrics read the data. But to tell discrimination from a justified disparity, you need a model of how the data was generated.
Three ways A can reach the prediction

Same correlation, three different causes.

Disparate treatment is the direct path; disparate impact is the indirect one. The right policy response differs for each — and observational metrics cannot tell them apart.
Equation · Kusner et al. 2017

Counterfactual fairness

P\!\left(\hat{Y}_{A \leftarrow a}(U)=y \mid X=x, A=a\right) = P\!\left(\hat{Y}_{A \leftarrow a'}(U)=y \mid X=x, A=a\right)
In plain terms
Would this exact person get the same decision if we flipped their group and held their background fixed? If yes, the prediction is counterfactually fair to them.
1
Abduction
infer the latent background U from what you observe
2
Action
intervene: set A to a′, hold U fixed
3
Prediction
propagate forward, read the new Ŷ
Real-world example

A loan model that “only looks at education” can still encode race.

Race (A)
→
Credential (E)
→
Predicted default
Historical access to education was shaped by race. So if you counterfactually changed this applicant’s race and held everything else fixed, their credential would change — and the prediction with it. Counterfactual fairness is violated, even though “race” was never a feature. The remedy is to drop variables downstream of A along paths you judge illegitimate — and which paths are illegitimate is, again, a values question.
Part six

The Generalized
Entropy Index

Not “is it fair, yes/no” — but how much unfairness, and where it comes from. Borrowed from income-inequality economics.
Equation · how much, and where

A single number for total unfairness

GE(\alpha) = \frac{1}{N\alpha(\alpha-1)} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[\left(\frac{b_i}{\mu}\right)^{\alpha} - 1\right]
α = 0
Mean Log Deviation
α = 1
Theil index
α = 2
½ (σ/μ)² — common pick
In plain terms
bi is each person’s benefit or error burden; μ its mean. The α dial decides where in the distribution you look.
Real-world example · choosing α

α decides whose harm you most want to catch.

Same distribution of benefit, five settings of α. A low α makes the index scream about the worst-served tail; a high α responds more to the top. Picking α is itself a values choice about who you’re protecting.
The decomposition · its best trick

Total unfairness = within-group + between-group

GE(\alpha) = \underbrace{\sum_{g}\frac{n_g}{N}\left(\frac{\mu_g}{\mu}\right)^{\alpha} GE_g(\alpha)}_{\text{within-group}} \;+\; \underbrace{GE(\alpha)\big|_{\text{between-group}}}_{\text{between-group}}
Part seven

The defense is
the deliverable

The toolkit lets you implement a choice. It does not make the choice for you — or absolve you of defending it.
What the toolkit can — and can’t — do

Three families. Each implements a choice; none resolves the theorem.

Pre-processing
Reweight, resample, transform the training data before fitting.
+ model-agnostic, upstream
− can’t reach mis-recorded truth
In-processing
Add fairness as a constraint or penalty in the training objective.
+ baked into the representation
− accuracy trade-off locked in
Post-processing
Adjust thresholds or scores after training, per group.
+ applies to a frozen model
− per-group thresholds invite legal objection
None of them resolve the impossibility theorem, or reach upstream structural bias in the data-generating process.
The deliverable, structured

A defended choice — six boxes, every time.

A clean statement of the limits

What no fairness metric can do for you.

Group metrics can’t tell you two similar individuals are treated consistently. Individual fairness needs a similarity metric d that encodes values. Causal fairness needs a structural model the data alone can’t supply.
None tells you whether the prediction task is fair to formulate. A model can ace re-arrest metrics — but re-arrest is not the construct society cares about.
None tells you whether affected people consider it fair — procedural fairness, fairness of opportunity, participation in the design all sit outside the metrics.
These are limits on the metrics, not failures of them. The supervisory work continues past the metric — into the construct, the deployment, and the participation.
The supervisory move

Understand the math. Choose the values. Defend the integration.

Three formally distinct metrics can be incompatible on one dataset when base rates differ. The impossibility is structural. The defense — connecting the chosen metric to the deployment, the cost-bearers, and the construct — is the work.
