**B01 · STILL·kenburns** — de Broglie, 1924 (PORTRAIT prompt)
> In 1924, a doctoral student handed Paris a thesis so strange the committee
> nearly rejected it. They asked Einstein. Einstein called it brilliant.

**B02 · CARD** — MATTER WAVES / why electrons make stripes
> His name was Louis de Broglie, and his idea takes one sentence: if light
> waves act like particles, then particles should act like waves.

**B03 · GRAPHIC** — the reversal (arrow flips)
> Einstein had shown light carries momentum — waves behaving like particles.
> De Broglie simply reversed the arrow: anything with momentum has a
> wavelength.

**T01–T03 · equation tangent — λ = h/p** (zones 2→3→4; spotlight λ)
> T01: The whole thesis is one line: wavelength equals h over momentum. The
> faster or heavier the thing, the shorter its wave.
> T02: Lambda, the wavelength of matter itself. P, the momentum — mass times
> velocity. And h, Planck's constant again — the same tiny number from the
> last two films.
> T03: An electron accelerated through 150 volts: wavelength one tenth of a
> nanometer. Exactly the spacing between atoms in a crystal. That coincidence
> made it testable.

**B04 · STILL·kenburns** — Davisson & Germer at Bell Labs (PORTRAIT prompt)
> In New Jersey, Clinton Davisson and Lester Germer were firing electrons at
> nickel, recording boring, featureless scatter. Then an accident
> contaminated their sample.

**B05 · GRAPHIC·drawon** — the accident makes a crystal; peaks appear
> Cleaning the nickel with heat turned it into a single crystal — orderly
> rows of atoms. The boring scatter vanished. Sharp peaks appeared at
> specific angles.

**B06 · GRAPHIC** — the numbers agree (54 V, Bragg, ~50°)
> Electrons at 54 volts should diffract like a wave of point one six seven
> nanometers. Run it through Bragg's law: the peak lands where observed.
> Exactly.

**B07 · STILL·kenburns** — Tonomura at Hitachi, 1989 (PORTRAIT prompt)
> Decades later in Japan, Akira Tonomura made it undeniable. He fired
> electrons through a splitter one at a time — never two in the machine at
> once.

**B08 · GRAPHIC** — the buildup (THE visual: 10 → 200 → 6,000 → 70,000)
> Each electron lands as a single dot. Ten dots: random. Two hundred: faint
> clumps. Six thousand: stripes forming. Seventy thousand: perfect
> interference fringes.

**B09 · GRAPHIC** — one electron, both paths, interfering with itself
> The electrons never touched each other. Each one's wave explored both
> paths at once and interfered with itself. The dot is where the wave
> collapsed.

**B10 · STILL·kenburns** — person walking through a doorway (NATGEO prompt)
> So why don't you diffract through a doorway? Compute it: seventy kilograms
> walking equals a wavelength of ten to the minus thirty-five meters.

**B11 · GRAPHIC** — the scale bar (electron → buckyball → you)
> That's twenty orders of magnitude smaller than a proton. Quantum mechanics
> doesn't switch off at human scale — its stripes just get impossibly fine.

**B12 · COMPOSITE** — buckyballs and beyond (ai plate)
> The frontier keeps moving: sixty-atom carbon balls diffracted in 1999.
> Molecules of two thousand atoms by 2019. The wave nature never stops. We
> just stop seeing it.

**B13 · STILL·kenburns** — de Broglie kicker (PORTRAIT prompt, direct gaze)
> Who was this doctoral student? French aristocracy, a history degree, a
> Nobel Prize five years after the thesis they almost refused. A story of
> its own.

**B14 · CARD** — next: THE WAVE FUNCTION (ch 3)
> Next: if every particle is a wave — what, exactly, is waving?
