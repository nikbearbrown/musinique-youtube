# PEDAGOGY — pfs-surrogate-endpoint

## Learning Objective
Understand the PFS-to-OS surrogate endpoint problem in oncology clinical trials, including when PFS is a valid surrogate, when it fails, and what the systemic consequences of surrogate-based approvals are.

## Core Concept
Progression-free survival (PFS) measures the time from treatment start until tumor growth or patient death. Overall survival (OS) measures time until death. PFS is used as a surrogate for OS because it can be measured faster, enabling quicker drug approvals. The surrogacy assumption requires that controlling tumor growth translates into longer patient survival.

## When Surrogacy Works
- Colorectal cancer first-line: PFS strongly predicts OS (validated meta-analysis)
- Settings where effective post-progression therapy is limited or non-existent
- Cancers where disease burden directly causes death (vs. competing causes)

## When Surrogacy Fails
- When effective post-progression therapies erase the PFS benefit (subsequent treatments restore survival)
- When the drug has toxicity that decreases quality of life, erasing PFS gain in survival terms
- Second and later line settings where tumor dynamics are heterogeneous

## Case Studies
- Bevacizumab breast: PFS benefit, no OS benefit, approval withdrawn
- Bevacizumab GBM: PFS benefit, no OS benefit, approval maintained
- Multiple accelerated approvals never confirmed for OS (Prasad 2017 data)

## Systemic Consequence
Patients pay for and take drugs that may not help them live longer. This is a clinical trial design problem, not a pharmaceutical fraud problem — but it has the same effect on patients.
