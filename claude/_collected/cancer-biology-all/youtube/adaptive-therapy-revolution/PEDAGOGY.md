# PEDAGOGY — adaptive-therapy-revolution

## Learning Objective
Understand adaptive therapy as an evolutionary strategy that exploits the fitness cost of drug resistance to maintain competitive suppression of resistant cell populations.

## Core Concept
Standard maximum-tolerated-dose oncology eliminates drug-sensitive cells, removing the competitive pressure on resistant cells and allowing their unopposed expansion. Adaptive therapy modulates drug dosing to maintain a population of drug-sensitive cells that compete with resistant subclones.

## Key Insight
The critical biological assumption is that drug-resistant cells pay a fitness cost in the absence of drug — they replicate more slowly, require more nutrients, or are otherwise disadvantaged. If this assumption holds, sensitive cells will outcompete resistant cells whenever drug pressure is withdrawn. This competition is what adaptive therapy weaponizes.

## Evidence Hierarchy
1. **Strongest**: Moffitt Phase 2 trial (Zhang et al. 2017) — prospective controlled, 27.0 vs 16.8 months PFS
2. **Moderate**: Gatenby mathematical modeling framework (2009) — theoretical rationale validated
3. **Weak**: Other cancer types — preclinical only or early Phase 1

## Pedagogical Flow
- Problem: why does maximum-dose therapy fail? → selection for resistance
- Solution mechanism: keep sensitive cells alive as competitive suppressors
- Evidence: one strong trial, narrow cancer type
- Gap: fitness cost assumption must be true for this to work

## Why This Matters
Adaptive therapy represents a paradigm shift from "cure" to "control" — treating advanced cancer as a manageable chronic disease rather than an acute eradication target.
