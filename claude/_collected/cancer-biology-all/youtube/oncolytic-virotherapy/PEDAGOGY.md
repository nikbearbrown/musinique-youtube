# PEDAGOGY — oncolytic-virotherapy

## Learning Objective
Understand how oncolytic virotherapy converts immunologically cold tumors to hot ones, why T-VEC works intraturmorally but not systemically, and what engineering approaches are being pursued to overcome systemic delivery barriers.

## Core Concept
Oncolytic viruses are engineered to selectively replicate in and lyse cancer cells, while triggering an immune response against tumor antigens released upon lysis. T-VEC (talimogene laherparepvec) is a modified HSV-1 with:
- Deleted ICP34.5: prevents viral immune evasion (tumor cells lack PKR pathway to stop replication)
- Deleted ICP47: allows antigen presentation
- Inserted GM-CSF: recruits dendritic cells and macrophages to the lysed tumor

## Why Intratumoral Works
Direct injection delivers virus at high local concentration, bypassing neutralizing antibodies. The virus replicates locally, lyses cells, releases neoantigens, and recruits immune cells. This converts a "cold" immunosuppressive tumor into a "hot" immunogenic one — even causing abscopal responses in uninjected lesions.

## Why Systemic Fails
1. Pre-existing neutralizing antibodies (HSV seroprevalence ~67% in adults)
2. Liver sequestration (Kupffer cells rapidly clear IV-administered particles)
3. Innate immune recognition (complement, interferons clear virus before tumor delivery)

## Engineering Solutions in Development
- Polymer coating (PEGylation) to evade antibodies
- Cell carrier vehicles (T cells, MSCs) to shield virus during trafficking
- Stealth capsid modifications
