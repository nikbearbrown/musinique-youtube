# PEDAGOGY — cancer-dormancy-recurrence

## Learning Objective
Understand cancer dormancy as an active molecular state maintained by specific signaling pathways, identify the triggers that reactivate dormant cells, and evaluate which interventions are clinically actionable.

## Core Concept
Disseminated tumor cells can persist in distant organs for years to decades in a non-proliferating, therapy-resistant dormant state. Dormancy is not passive cellular inactivity — it is actively maintained by TGF-beta2/p38 MAPK signaling, BMP niche signals, and low uPAR:uPA ratios. Reactivation occurs when these suppressive signals are disrupted by aging niches, inflammation, or surgery.

## Clinical Evidence for Long Dormancy
- Breast cancer: 20-year recurrence risk shown by ATLAS/aTTom trials (endocrine therapy extension)
- Prostate cancer: late recurrences 10-15 years post-prostatectomy common
- Melanoma: cases of recurrence 20+ years after apparent cure documented

## Therapeutic Gap
The pathways maintaining dormancy (TGF-beta2, BMP) are receptor-mediated and theoretically drugable. The challenge is:
1. We cannot reliably detect dormant cells (ctDNA may be negative)
2. We cannot predict when/if reactivation will occur
3. Long-term preventive therapy has cumulative toxicity

## Pedagogical Framework
- Establish dormancy as active (not passive) — paradigm shift
- Map known mechanisms with evidence tiers
- Identify actionable vs. non-actionable triggers
- Frame the detection gap as the core clinical problem
